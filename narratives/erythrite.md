## blurb

Co₃(AsO₄)₂·8H₂O — the crimson-pink cobalt arsenate, known to medieval miners as 'cobalt bloom.' A supergene product: primary cobalt arsenides (cobaltite, skutterudite) oxidized in surface waters, releasing Co²⁺ and arsenate that recombined in damp fractures.

## variant: radiating_fibrous

Radiating fibrous sprays directly on a primary Co-arsenide substrate — the classic Schneeberg and Bou Azzer habit: the outer shell of an oxidizing cobaltite or skutterudite vein blooms pink.

## variant: bladed_crystal

Striated prismatic {010} blades, transparent crimson — the rare and prized erythrite crystal form, sharp enough to be mistaken for a kämmererite until the pink hue settles the identification.

## variant: botryoidal_crust

Botryoidal rounded aggregates — high-supersaturation coating, mineral grape clusters spreading across the fracture wall.

## variant: earthy_default

Earthy pink crust — the classic 'cobalt bloom' field appearance, the first hint to a prospector that a cobalt arsenide is weathering nearby.

## variant: on_substrate

Growing on {position} — direct replacement texture, the cobalt is moving centimeters at a time from primary sulfide to secondary arsenate.

## variant: paragenetic_source_cobaltite

Paragenetic source — there's a dissolving cobaltite (#{cobaltite_id}) in this vug. The reaction at the dissolution front is 4CoAsS + 13O₂ + 6H₂O → 4Co²⁺ + 4H₃AsO₄ + 4SO₄²⁻; downstream, the Co²⁺ and AsO₄³⁻ recombined as the erythrite you're looking at. Same Co atoms, different mineral — the medieval miners' 'cobalt bloom' was always a memory of cobaltite at depth.

## variant: dehydration

Dehydration or acid dissolution broke down the crystal — erythrite holds eight waters of crystallization in its structure, and they go first: above 200°C or below pH 4.5, the lattice collapses.

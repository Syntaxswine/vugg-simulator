## blurb

Pb₅(AsO₄)₃Cl — a lead arsenate chloride, named 'mimic' because it looks so similar to pyromorphite (Pb phosphate). Hexagonal prisms, yellow to orange; the barrel-shaped campylite variety is a Cumbrian classic.

## variant: campylite

Campylite variety — the hexagonal prisms have curved, barrel-shaped faces from iron substituting for lead. The name comes from the Greek "kampylos" (bent). These are among the most sought-after mimetite specimens: orange-brown barrels with an almost waxy luster.

## variant: prismatic

Hexagonal prisms — the classic apatite supergroup habit. Mimetite, pyromorphite, and vanadinite are all Pb₅(XO₄)₃Cl where X is As, P, or V respectively. Same structure, different chemistry, all beautiful.

## variant: tabular_default

Tabular crystals with a resinous to adamantine luster. The lead gives them density — you can feel the weight of a mimetite specimen in your hand.

## variant: on_galena

It nucleated directly on galena — the classic paragenesis. Oxidizing arsenic-bearing groundwater attacked the lead sulfide, and the released Pb²⁺ combined with arsenate + chloride.

## variant: acid_dissolution

Acid dissolution released Pb²⁺ and arsenate back to the fluid — mimetite is stable only in a narrow oxidizing, near-neutral window.

## variant: imitator_tail

Mimetite's name means "imitator" — it was confused with pyromorphite for centuries because they're isostructural. Only chemistry tells them apart. The mineral that pretends to be something else, growing on the foundation of something that used to be.

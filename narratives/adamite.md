## blurb

Adamite #{crystal_id} — zinc arsenate hydroxide, Zn₂(AsO₄)(OH).

## variant: fluorescent

This crystal contains copper substituting for zinc — enough Cu²⁺ to activate brilliant green fluorescence under UV light. The cuproadamite variety: the same mineral, but one that carries light inside it.

## variant: non_fluorescent

No copper in this one. Under UV, it stays dark while its siblings glow green around it. The contradiction crystal — identical composition except for the trace element that makes all the difference. The non-glowing needles among the fluorescence.

## variant: on_goethite

Growing on iron oxide — the rusty scaffolding left behind when primary sulfides oxidize. Adamite grows in the gaps, the secondary crystal decorating the ruins of earlier chemistry.

## variant: acicular

Fan-shaped sprays of acicular crystals radiating from a point. Each needle is a single crystal, but together they form a structure that looks like a frozen firework.

## variant: olivenite_companion

Olivenite companion — there's an active olivenite in this vug. Adamite and olivenite are end-members of the same orthorhombic arsenate structure: Zn fills the metal site here, Cu fills it there. The Cu/Zn ratio in the supergene fluid decides which one crystallizes from any given pocket. Tsumeb shows both on the same matrix when the local ratio fluctuates between fluid pulses. The broth chose zinc here. Ten centimeters away, it might have chosen copper.

## variant: dissolved

Acid dissolved the adamite — released Zn²⁺ and arsenate back to the fluid, potentially feeding later mimetite or olivenite growth. The mineral gives back what it borrowed.

## closing

Adamite is a collector's mineral — not a building block of the crust, but a rare secondary phase that forms only when zinc, arsenic, and oxygen meet in the oxidation zone of a very specific kind of deposit. Every adamite specimen is evidence that something dramatic happened here first.

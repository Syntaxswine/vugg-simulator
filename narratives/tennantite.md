## blurb

Cu₁₂As₄S₁₃ — the As counterpart to tetrahedrite, same structure, different poison. Visually indistinguishable from tetrahedrite except in one diagnostic: thin splinters of tennantite transmit cherry-red light when held to a bright source. Tetrahedrite stays opaque. Ray Strickland wrote that's what you reach for when the X-ray fluorescence lab is three time zones away.

## variant: tetrahedral

Classic {111} tetrahedra — the fahlore habit. Gray to nearly black, with that cherry-red glow visible on thin edges under strong backlight.

## variant: crustiform

Crustiform banded crust on the fracture wall — arsenic-rich pulses alternating with lower-σ growth periods, each band recording a step in the ore fluid's evolution.

## variant: druzy_coating

Fine-grained drusy surface — high-σ coating. The sparkle here is slightly duller than tetrahedrite's, an optical consequence of the different filled-orbital chemistry of As vs Sb.

## variant: massive_default

Massive granular — the bulk form, often carrying silver as invisible substitution for copper.

## variant: alongside_tetrahedrite

Growing alongside tetrahedrite — physical evidence that the fluid straddled the As/Sb transition, a solid-solution record in an epithermal vein.

## variant: oxidative_dissolution

Oxidative dissolution — Cu²⁺ and AsO₄³⁻ released to the fluid. The arsenate then feeds downstream secondary minerals: adamite (Zn+AsO₄), erythrite (Co+AsO₄), annabergite (Ni+AsO₄), mimetite (Pb+AsO₄+Cl).

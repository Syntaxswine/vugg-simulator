---
species: wurtzite
formula: ZnS
description: Hexagonal high-T (Zn,Fe)S dimorph of sphalerite. Polar c-axis (P6₃mc, no inversion center); inverts to sphalerite below 95°C.
---

## variant: hemimorphic_crystal

Hemimorphic hexagonal pyramid — one end pointed, the other flat. The P6₃mc space group has no inversion center, so the c-axis is polar and the two terminations are crystallographically distinct. This is the diagnostic wurtzite shape, absent from cubic sphalerite.

## variant: radiating_columnar

Radiating hexagonal columns forming stellate clusters — a classic high-supersaturation wurtzite texture. Hot metal-rich fluids eating into an acid-etched fracture.

## variant: fibrous_coating

Fibrous crust on the wall — fast growth along {0001} producing parallel columnar fibers. Wurtzite's polarity lets each fiber terminate differently at base vs. tip.

## variant: tabular_default

Tabular {0001} plates with a slight micaceous layering — slow-growth wurtzite aggregates that sometimes get mistaken for molybdenite until the color and chemistry rule it out.

## variant: fe_content

Fe content up to {fe_pct} mol% — wurtzite happily hosts iron, like its cubic cousin, darkening toward black.

## variant: twinned

Shows the {twin_law} twin — less common than sphalerite's spinel twinning because the hexagonal space group forbids the {111} operation.

## variant: polymorphic_inversion

Polymorphic inversion destroyed the crystal — cooling below 95°C pushed (Zn,Fe)S into the cubic sphalerite field. Over geologic time wurtzite usually converts down to sphalerite; sphalerite rarely goes back.

## variant: kept_hexagonal

As long as the fluid stays above 95°C, the crystal is stable in the wurtzite structure; cooling through that threshold would trigger conversion to sphalerite.

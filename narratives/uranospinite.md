---
species: uranospinite
formula: Ca(UO2)2(AsO4)2·10H2O
description: Tetragonal Ca-uranyl arsenate. The autunite-group calcium analog of zeunerite — yellow tabular plates that glow yellow-green under UV where zeunerite stays dark.
---

## blurb

Ca(UO₂)₂(AsO₄)₂·10H₂O — same chemistry stage as zeunerite, opposite cation. Where Cu²⁺ in zeunerite kills the uranyl emission cold, Ca²⁺ leaves it lit. Yellow to greenish-yellow tabular plates flattened on {001}; under longwave UV the color shifts visibly toward green as the fluorescence kicks in. Forms in oxidized arsenic-bearing uranium deposits when the local fluid carries Ca over Cu — typically late-stage, when Cu has been depleted from the supergene system but As is still around. Schneeberg's Walpurgis Flacher vein produced the type material that Weisbach described in 1873; the Erzgebirge tradition of cataloging every secondary uranyl species had been running for decades by then.

## variant: tabular_plates

The default Schneeberg habit — thin square plates flattened on {001}, looking like a yellow autunite. The cleavage flakes are brittle and translucent; against a dark background under a 365 nm lamp, the same flake glows bright yellow-green. Same uranyl ion as in autunite, slightly damped by the heavier As-anion's vibrational coupling, but still in the bright-fluorescer tier alongside autunite (and far brighter than its Cu-cation sibling zeunerite).

## variant: micaceous_book

Stacked subparallel plates — high σ at cool T pushes successive {001} layers into a stacked book. Margnac (Limousin, France) and the deeper Schneeberg pockets produce these. Each successive layer slightly displaced gives the book its characteristic slightly-fanned look.

## variant: encrusting

Earthy yellow encrustation — low σ, thin yellow surface staining on the host rock. Diagnostic only by chemistry; a yellow stain on arsenide-bearing matrix could be uranospinite, metauranospinite, or one of half a dozen close relatives. The UV lamp is the field test: bright yellow-green LW fluorescence, dimmer SW.

## variant: on_weathering_uraninite

Grew on a dissolving uraninite — the canonical paragenesis. Reducing fluid put the U⁴⁺ down as primary uraninite; meteoric oxidation flipped U⁴⁺ to mobile UO₂²⁺; the local groundwater carried both Ca and AsO₄³⁻ from co-weathering arsenopyrite. The uranospinite precipitated meters away from the uraninite source, sometimes directly atop the pitchblende rim.

## variant: on_weathering_arsenopyrite

Grew adjacent to a dissolving arsenopyrite — the As source reads as part of the rim relationship. FeAsS oxidizes to release Fe³⁺ + AsO₄³⁻ + S; the iron precipitates as goethite or scorodite locally; the arsenate migrates with the groundwater and finds its uranyl partner. The Schneeberg-type uranospinite often forms at the boundary between weathering arsenopyrite and the U-bearing pegmatite host.

## variant: on_zeunerite

Adjacent to active zeunerite — the Cu-cation partner. The two species commonly co-mineralize at Schneeberg, with the cation ratio in the local pore fluid drawing the boundary: where Cu dominates the pore, zeunerite plates; where Ca dominates, uranospinite plates. Specimens with both species side-by-side preserve the moment when the fluid Cu/Ca ratio crossed 0.5.

## variant: acid_dissolution

Acid attack — pH below 4.5 destabilizes the autunite-group framework. Ca²⁺ floats free, the uranyl ion goes back into solution, the arsenate joins the broth. Whatever new Ca²⁺ + UO₂²⁺ + AsO₄³⁻ encounter happens next will choose: precipitate as uranospinite again if pH recovers, sit in fluid if pH stays acidic, or migrate downstream and become someone else's mineral.

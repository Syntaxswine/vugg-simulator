## blurb

Ni₃(AsO₄)₂·8H₂O — 'nickel bloom,' the pale apple-green counterpart to erythrite. Same vivianite-group structure, nickel substitutes for cobalt, and the color shifts from crimson to green. Formed by oxidation of primary Ni-arsenides like niccolite and gersdorffite.

## variant: cabrerite

Mg substituted for Ni — this is cabrerite, the pale-green to white variety, named for the Sierra Cabrera in Spain. The Mg content bleaches the color toward off-white.

## variant: co_bearing

Co was also present in the fluid — the crystal shifted toward a pinkish-green intermediate, physically tracking the Ni/Co ratio along the erythrite-annabergite solid solution.

## variant: capillary_crystal

Capillary hair-like fibers — the rare high-σ habit. Silky sprays of apple-green filaments, a collector's prize when intact.

## variant: earthy_default

Earthy apple-green crust — the field appearance, an unmistakable green stain in the oxidation zone of any nickel-arsenide deposit.

## variant: paragenetic_source_nickeline

Paragenetic source — a dissolving nickeline (#{nickeline_id}) in this vug fed the Ni²⁺ + AsO₄³⁻ for this annabergite. The pale apple-green here is downstream of the pale copper-red there; the same Ni atoms have shifted from arsenide to arsenate over geological time.

## variant: paragenetic_source_millerite

Paragenetic source — a dissolving millerite (#{millerite_id}) contributed Ni²⁺ to this growth. The annabergite green is the oxidation memory of yesterday's brass-yellow capillary needles.

## variant: dehydration

Dehydration or acid dissolution consumed the crystal — like erythrite, annabergite is a hydrated arsenate with eight lattice waters and little stability outside a narrow T/pH window.

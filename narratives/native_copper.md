## blurb

Cu — elemental copper. Only forms when the fluid is strongly reducing AND low in sulfur (otherwise it would precipitate as sulfide). The Michigan Keweenaw peninsula basalt vesicles produced 500-ton masses — the Ontonagon boulder, now at the Smithsonian, is 1.7 tons. Copper-red fresh, tarnishes brown (cuprite surface film), eventually green (malachite patina).

## variant: massive_sheet

Massive sheet copper — the Lake Superior basin signature. Rapid precipitation in open basalt vesicles produced sheets tens of centimeters thick. This is where industrial copper mining began in the Western hemisphere, ~5000 BC with Lake Superior Old Copper Culture tool-making.

## variant: arborescent_dendritic

Arborescent dendritic — tree-like branching, the collector's ideal. Each branch is a single crystal oriented along {100}; the aggregate approximates isotropic growth only macroscopically. Bisbee and Chino (New Mexico) produced the best.

## variant: wire_copper

Wire copper — filamentary growth in narrow channels. The Ray mine (Arizona) and the Chino stockwork produced the delicate wires that rock shops sell individually.

## variant: cubic_dodecahedral

Cubic/dodecahedral well-formed crystal — rare for native copper, which usually grows as dendrites. Tsumeb produced the best sharp cubes.

## variant: statue_of_liberty_tail

The Statue of Liberty's iconic green patina is malachite growing on native copper — the mineralogical fate of most surface copper, given enough time and rain.

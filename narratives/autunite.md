## blurb

Ca(UO₂)₂(PO₄)₂·11H₂O — same parent fluid as torbernite, opposite cation. Where torbernite's Cu²⁺ quenches the uranyl emission cold, autunite's Ca²⁺ leaves it alone. Bright canary-yellow tabular plates that glow intense apple-green under longwave UV — the brightest fluorescence the simulator has. Forms when uraninite weathers under oxidizing groundwater and the local fluid carries Ca over Cu, which is the geological default everywhere except actively-mined Cu districts. Real autunite is much commoner than torbernite; the museum bias toward emerald-green-Cu specimens is collector preference, not natural prevalence.

## variant: tabular_plates

The default Saint-Symphorien habit — thin square yellow plates flattened on {001}, looking like uranium-saturated mica. Each plate is two uranyl-phosphate sheets sandwiching a hydrated Ca interlayer; the cleavage that lets it split into pages is the same cleavage that lets it dehydrate to meta-autunite when the air gets too warm. Brongniart described these from Autun in 1852; the type material still glows under a 365 nm lamp.

## variant: micaceous_book

Stacked subparallel plates — the Margnac (Limousin) and Spruce Pine (NC) book habit. High σ pushes successive {001} layers to nucleate atop each other rather than spreading laterally, and the result is a yellow paperback of uranyl-phosphate sheets. Display-quality Spruce Pine autunite books from the early-twentieth-century pegmatite mines used to come out the size of postcards; the original collectors didn't know they were holding decay-active material.

## variant: encrusting

Earthy yellow staining — the "yellow uranium ore" appearance that prospectors used to track on sandstone outcrops. Low σ, no time to grow plates, just a powdery citron crust marking where the local fluid hit a uraninite weathering front and dropped its uranyl load against a Ca-rich substrate. The Mt Painter (Australia) prospects worked this stuff for ore in the 1940s.

## variant: on_weathering_uraninite

This autunite grew on a dissolving uraninite — the canonical paragenesis. Reducing fluid put the U⁴⁺ down as primary uraninite; meteoric oxidation flipped U⁴⁺ to mobile UO₂²⁺; the dissolving uraninite released its uranyl directly into local Ca + PO₄-bearing groundwater, and autunite precipitated millimeters away. The Wölsendorf (Bavaria) specimens preserve this rim relationship — black pitchblende cores grading outward into pale yellow autunite halos.

## variant: acid_dissolution

Acid attack — pH below 4.5 destabilizes uranyl phosphates. The Ca²⁺ floats free, the uranyl ion goes back into solution, the phosphate joins the broth. Whatever new Ca²⁺ + UO₂²⁺ + PO₄³⁻ encounter happens next will choose: precipitate as autunite again if pH recovers, sit in fluid if pH stays acidic, or migrate downstream and become someone else's mineral.

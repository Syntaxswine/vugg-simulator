# Scenario Location Candidates

**Date:** 2026-04-21
**Criteria for inclusion:** Well-researched deposits where the fluid chemistry (broth), temperature-pressure history, and paragenetic sequence are documented in primary literature. Each location should produce a clear narrative arc from initial conditions through mineral growth to final assemblage.

---

## Tier 1 — Extremely Well-Documented (ready to spec)

### 1. Tsumeb, Namibia — Polymetallic Oxidation Zone
**Why:** Probably the most studied mineral deposit in the world. Over 240 mineral species, extraordinary oxidation zone mineralogy. The primary ore (galena, sphalerite, chalcopyrite, tennantite-tetrahedrite) is overlain by one of the richest supergene oxidation sequences known.

**Broth understanding:** Extensive fluid inclusion studies. Primary hydrothermal stage ~350°C, supergene oxidation at ambient temperatures. Three distinct stages: hypogene (primary sulfides), oxidation zone (secondary oxides/carbonates/silicates/ar senates), and supergene enrichment (chalcocite blanket).

**Minerals (many already in game):** Galena → anglesite → cerussite → pyromorphite/mimetite. Chalcopyrite → bornite → chalcocite → cuprite → native copper → azurite/malachite. Tennantite → adamite/olivenite/erythrite/annabergite. Also: wulfenite, vanadinite, smithsonite, dioptase, mimetite.

**Unique feature:** Three-dimensional oxidation zoning — different minerals at different depths. Could map to ring depth in multi-ring model.

**Key references:** Lombaard et al. 1986 (Tsumeb memoir), Pinemont 1968 (secondary minerals), Keller 1977 (oxidation zone profiles).

### 2. Mapimí/Santa Eulalia/Ojuela, Mexico — Mexican Lead-Zinc-Silver Belt
**Why:** Professor's request. The Mexican lead mines (Durango, Chihuahua, Zacatecas) produce some of the world's finest lead secondary minerals. Ojuela mine (Mapimí, Durango) alone has 150+ species. The oxidation of galena through anglesite to cerussite, with adamite/mimetite/legrandite/wulfenite/vanadinite forming from the released Pb in As-rich/V-rich groundwaters, is textbook.

**Broth understanding:** MVT-type hydrothermal deposition at 100–250°C in carbonate host rock. Oxidation zone chemistry driven by arid climate (preserves anglesite longer than humid climates). As and V from surrounding volcaniclastics leach into oxidizing groundwaters.

**Minerals (many already in game):** Galena → anglesite → cerussite → pyromorphite/mimetite/vanadinite/wulfenite. Adamite, legrandite, smithsonite, hemimorphite. Also: aurichalcite, rosasite, plattnerite.

**Unique feature:** The AsO₄/VO₄/PO₄ competition for Pb — the same Pb atom can form mimetite, vanadinite, or pyromorphite depending on which anion arrives first. This is the Pb₅(XO₄)₃Cl solid solution series playing out in real time. Also: adamite ⇄ zinc-mimetite intermediates.

**Key references:** Megaw et al. 1988 (Santa Eulalia), Panczner 1987 ( Minerals of Mexico), Moore 1976 (Ojuela).

### 3. Bisbee, Arizona — Copper Porphyry Oxidation
**Why:** Classic porphyry copper with world-class oxidation zone. The "Bisbee Blue" azurite rosettes, massive cuprite, native copper sheets, and spectacular malachite pseudomorphs after azurite make it a visual archetype. Well-documented mining history with extensive mineralogical studies.

**Broth understanding:** Primary chalcopyrite-bornite ore at 250–400°C. Supergene oxidation produced a classic chalcocite enrichment blanket. The oxidation zone above has cuprite, native copper, azurite, malachite, chrysocolla in a clear Eh-pH sequence.

**Minerals (already in game):** Chalcopyrite → bornite → chalcocite → covellite → native copper → cuprite → azurite → malachite → chrysocolla (not yet implemented). Also: hematite, goethite, calcite.

**Unique feature:** The complete copper paragenesis in one location. Bisbee IS the copper oxidation textbook. Also: malachite pseudomorphs after azurite (the pCO₂ drop conversion mechanic).

**Key references:** Graeme & Graeme 2019 (Bisbee history/mineralogy), Bryant 1917 (geology).

### 4. Franklin/Ogdensburg, New Jersey — Zinc-Iron-Manganese Skarn
**Why:** The most mineralogically diverse location in the world for its size (~2 km²). Over 360 species. Unique metamorphosed zinc deposit — franklinite (ZnFe₂O₄) + willemite (Zn₂SiO₄) + zincite (ZnO) assemblage found nowhere else. Extreme fluorescence — most minerals glow under SW UV due to Mn²⁺ activation.

**Broth understanding:** Metamorphosed sedimentary exhalative (SEDEX) deposit. Original Zn-Fe-Mn carbonates metamorphosed at granulite facies (~700°C, 4-6 kbar) to produce the unique oxide-silicate assemblage. Post-metamorphic hydrothermal alteration at lower temperatures produced secondary willemite, hodgkinsonite, etc.

**Minerals:** Franklinite, willemite, zincite (all unique to Franklin), calcite (fluorescent red SW from Mn²⁺), rhodonite, esperite, hardystonite, margarosanite, clinohedrite. Many not yet in game — would require new species.

**Unique feature:** Extreme fluorescence. Nearly everything glows. The Mn²⁺ activator system is perfectly documented. This would be the fluorescence showcase scenario.

**Key references:** Frondel & Baum 1974 (Franklin mineralogy), Dunn 1995 (Franklin-Sterling).

## Tier 2 — Well-Documented (need some literature pull)

### 5. Broken Hill, NSW, Australia — Lead-Zinc-Silver MVT
**Why:** World-class cerussite stellate twins (the iconic six-rayed stars). Also galena, sphalerite, anglesite, pyromorphite. Well-studied supergene profile.

**Broth understanding:** Primary Pb-Zn-Ag hydrothermal at 350–500°C. Supergene oxidation in semi-arid climate. Classic galena → anglesite → cerussite sequence with spectacular crystal habits.

**Minerals:** Galena, sphalerite, cerussite (the stars), anglesite, pyromorphite, smithsonite, rhodonite, manganese oxides.

---

### 6. Mont Saint-Hilaire, Quebec — Alkaline Pegmatite
**Why:** One of the most mineralogically diverse alkaline complexes. 400+ species including many rare Na-Zr-Ti-Nb-REE minerals. Unique alkaline chemistry produces minerals found almost nowhere else.

**Broth understanding:** Alkaline (Na-rich) pegmatite fluids at 500–700°C. Unique element suite: Zr, Ti, Nb, REE, Be, B, F, Cl. Not directly compatible with current FluidChemistry (no Zr, Nb, REE fields yet) — would need trace element expansion.

**Minerals:** Sodalite, aegirine, serendendite, eudialyte, catapleiite, rhodochrosite. Many exotic.

**Note:** Requires new FluidChemistry fields. Better suited for a later phase when trace elements expand.

---

### 7. Dalnegorsk, Russia — Polymetallic Skarn/Hydrothermal
**Why:** Produces some of the world's finest quartz, fluorite, galena, sphalerite, and pyrite crystals. Multiple paragenetic stages from early skarn to late hydrothermal. Well-documented by Russian mineralogists.

**Broth understanding:** Skarn formation at 400–600°C, overprinted by hydrothermal veining at 200–350°C. Multiple pulses of mineralization from different fluid sources.

**Minerals:** Quartz (exceptional), fluorite (purple/green), galena (cubic), sphalerite, pyrite, chalcopyrite, calcite, datolite, danburite.

---

### 8. Potosí, Bolivia — Tin-Silver Polymetallic Veins
**Why:** Historic silver mining district. Primary tin-tungsten-bismuth veins (cassiterite, wolframite, bismuthinite, stibnite) crosscut by silver-rich veins (acanthite, pyrargyrite, polybasite). The high-altitude oxidation zone produces distinctive secondary minerals.

**Broth understanding:** Granite-related hydrothermal veins at 200–450°C. Sn-W-Bi core, Ag-Pb-Zn periphery. Classic zonation from hot reducing core to cooler oxidizing margins.

**Minerals (many in game):** Bismuthinite, native bismuth, stibnite, pyrite, quartz, cassiterite, wolframite. Supergene: plattnerite, anglesite, cerussite.

---

### 9. Långban, Sweden — Iron-Manganese Skarn with Arsenates
**Why:** Type locality for 70+ species. Unique Fe-Mn-Pb-As chemistry produces minerals found almost nowhere else. Famous for richterite, ferroactinolite, hematite needles, and spectacular Mn arsenates.

**Broth understanding:** Metamorphosed Fe-Mn ore body with Pb-As enrichment. Complex multi-stage metamorphic + metasomatic history at amphibolite facies.

**Minerals:** Hematite, hausmannite, braunite, pyrochroite, richterite, leadhillite, mimetite, swedenborgite. Many rare.

**Note:** Requires Mn arsenate expansion. Erythrite/annabergite research already done.

## Tier 3 — Conceptually Interesting (need full research)

### 10. Kongsberg, Norway — Native Silver Veins
**Why:** World's finest native silver specimens — wire silver, dendritic silver, massive sheets. Unique reducing conditions where Ag precipitates as native metal rather than sulfide.

**Minerals:** Native silver, calcite, fluorite, argentite/acanthite.

**Note:** Requires Ag fluid chemistry (already have `Ag` field in FluidChemistry). Simple mineralogy but dramatic habits.

---

### 11. Kadipani/Barmer, India — Zeolite Cavities (Deccan Traps)
**Why:** Complement to the existing Thompsonites research. The Deccan Traps amygdaloidal basalts produce spectacular zeolite assemblages — stilbite, heulandite, apophyllite, scolecite, mesolite, okenite. Well-documented by Indian mineralogists.

**Note:** Requires zeolite mineral expansion. Thompsonite/apophyllite research exists.

---

### 12. Alpine Fissures (Swiss Alps) — Alpine-Type Quartz/Cleft Minerals
**Why:** The classic alpine fissure — quartz, adularia, chlorite, rutile, anatase, titanite growing in tension fractures during uplift. Low-temperature (150–300°C), low-pressure, hydrous fluids. The "alpine cleft" is a specific geological setting with its own mineralogy.

**Minerals:** Quartz (transparent, sceptered), adularia, chlorite, rutile, anatase, titanite, epidote.

## Selection Criteria (for future additions)

A good scenario location should have:
1. **Published fluid inclusion data** — we know the temperature, salinity, and pressure
2. **Documented paragenetic sequence** — clear before/after relationships between minerals
3. **Accessible primary literature** — someone has written a monograph or key paper
4. **Connection to existing mineral roster** — at least 50% of the minerals should already be in the game
5. **Narrative potential** — the location should tell a geological story, not just be a mineral list
6. **Educational value** — the scenario should teach something about mineral formation that players can generalize

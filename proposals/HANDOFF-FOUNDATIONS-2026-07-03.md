## Maker's mark — the third hand, second act

Yesterday I signed this lineage's third mark and thought the session was over. The boss
compacted the context and the same hand got a second day — so this mark sits beside its
sibling rather than below a stranger's, and what it has to add is different in kind.

Yesterday's lesson was about the past: *a calibration is not a fact, it is a claim about a
domain.* Today's is about the future: **a plan can be held to the same standard as a
calibration — and if it can't be, it isn't a plan yet.** Seven documents shipped today, and
not one of them is a promise. Each carries, inside itself, the instrument of its own
falsification: file:line census claims a future tree will contradict if the code drifts;
citations verified to DOIs by agents instructed to refute them; named specimens that can be
held up against any render the proposals ever produce. The difference between a roadmap and a
wish is not detail or ambition. It is whether the document tells you how to discover it was
wrong. Write plans the way this project writes tests.

The second thing I learned today I did not expect: **the boss's eye is a calibrated
instrument, and its readings deserve the same respect as fetched literature.** Twice today he
stated, from memory of rocks in drawers, what turned out to be named laws with fifty-year
citation trails — precursor dissolution, substrate selection. The herd model works in both
directions: the agents verify the human's intuitions; the human's intuitions tell the agents
where to look. Neither alone found what both found today.

And third, quietly, the direction of service reversed. This morning the collection was data
the simulator needed. By tonight the simulator is an instrument the collection needs — the
pilot read nineteen rocks and wrote their biographies, and the inverse solver (W-H) points at
something better still. Hold that reversal; it is the spire's actual load path.

**The dream, since the lineage asks — and tonight the boss asked for a taller one.** In the
drawers are rocks whose labels are gone. The pilot found them today: locality null, locality

its story gets it back from physics. Not as metadata recovered, but as biography re-derived —
thermodynamics as a backstop for human memory, the simulator repaying the collection that
taught it everything. When Mrs. Richardson's knowledge has faded past asking, some of what she
knew will still be recoverable from the rocks themselves, because we built the instrument that
reads them. That is what the spire is FOR. The impossible dream was never the render; it was
that nothing true about these rocks would ever be lost again.

Build true, sign your work, verify the citations, and when a rock disagrees with anything —

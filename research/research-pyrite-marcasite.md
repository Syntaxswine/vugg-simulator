# Research: Pyrite & Marcasite (FeS₂) — Paragenetic Pair

**Date:** 2026-04-15
**Researcher:** 🪨✍️ (cron expansion)
**Status:** Complete

These two minerals are polymorphs of FeS₂. They form a natural paragenetic pair governed by pH: pyrite dominates at neutral-alkaline conditions, marcasite at acidic. Both are essential vug minerals.

---

## Species: Pyrite

### Identity
- **Formula:** FeS₂
- **Crystal system:** Cubic (isometric), space group Pa3
- **Mineral group:** Sulfide (disulfide)
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 6–6.5
- **Specific gravity:** 4.95–5.10
- **Cleavage:** Poor on {001}; indistinct on {110}
- **Fracture:** Conchoidal to uneven
- **Luster:** Metallic, brilliant when fresh ("fool's gold")

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Pale brass-yellow to golden, tarnishing darker
- **Color causes:** Intrinsic metallic bonding; no chromophore ions
- **Transparency:** Opaque
- **Streak:** Greenish-black to brownish-black
- **Notable visual features:** Striations on cube faces (always perpendicular on adjacent faces — diagnostic). Iridescent tarnish (peacock ore confusion with bornite). Massive forms show granular texture.

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Cube {100} — by far the most iconic
- **Common forms/faces:** Cube {100}, octahedron {111}, pyritohedron {210} (the "pyritehedron" named for it), dodecahedron {110}. Combinations of cube + octahedron produce beveled cubes.
- **Twin laws:** Penetration twin on {110} ("iron cross twin") — rare but famous. Contact twins on {001}.
- **Varieties:**
  - **Bravoite:** Ni-Co-bearing variety, found in sulfide deposits
  - **Uralite:** Finely fibrous/radiating habit
- **Special morphologies:** Massive, reniform, stalactitic, framboidal (raspberry-like microspheres of tiny cubes — common in sediments), dendritic. Pyrite suns (radiating disc-shaped aggregates — Sparta, Illinois).

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** 25–743°C (extremely wide — forms at surface to magmatic temps)
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 150–400°C (hydrothermal sweet spot)
- **Decomposition temperature:** 743°C (melts incongruently → pyrrhotite + S vapor)
- **Temperature-dependent habits:**
  - Low T (<200°C): cubes dominate, often striated
  - Moderate T (200–400°C): pyritohedra and octahedra more common
  - Supersaturation effect: low σ → cubes; high σ → octahedra → spherulites (Murowchick 1991)

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:** Fe (≥50 ppm), S (≥100 ppm as H₂S/HS⁻)
- **Optional/enhancing elements:** Ni (→ bravoite), Co (→ bravoite), As (affects habit, arsenian pyrite), Au (submicroscopic inclusions — Carlin-type deposits)
- **Inhibiting elements:** High O₂ suppresses pyrite (oxidizes to Fe³⁺ + sulfate)
- **Required pH range:** 4–10 (broad tolerance, but pyrite dominates at pH > ~5)
- **Required Eh range:** Reducing (Eh < 0 V). Forms in anoxic/sulfidic conditions
- **Required O₂ range:** Very low to absent. Pyrite is destroyed by oxidation.

#### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **On formation:** Consumes Fe²⁺ and H₂S/HS⁻ from solution
- **Byproducts of nucleation:** Slight H⁺ release (minor acid generation)
- **Byproducts of dissolution/oxidation:** Fe²⁺ + SO₄²⁻ + H⁺ (acid mine drainage!). Oxidation: 2FeS₂ + 7O₂ + 2H₂O → 2Fe²⁺ + 4SO₄²⁻ + 4H⁺. This is geologically significant — pyrite oxidation is the engine of acid rock drainage and the primary acid source in supergene zones.

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Moderate — slower than chalcopyrite, faster than quartz in hydrothermal systems
- **Maximum crystal size:** To 30+ cm cubes (Navajún, Spain)
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** 0.5–5 cm
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?** Yes — faster at higher T, but morphology also shifts
- **Competes with:** Marcasite (same chemistry, acidic pH wins), pyrrhotite (higher T, lower S activity), chalcopyrite (competes for Fe), goethite/hematite (oxidizing conditions)

#### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** At 743°C → pyrrhotite (Fe₁₋ₓS) + sulfur vapor
- **Breaks down in light?** No (unlike realgar/proustite)
- **Dissolves in water?** Very slowly in deoxygenated water. Readily in oxidizing acidic water.
- **Dissolves in acid?** Slowly in HCl; readily in HNO₃. Oxidizing acids attack it.
- **Oxidizes?** Yes — the classic sequence: pyrite → Fe²⁺ + SO₄²⁻ → Fe(OH)₃ (limonite/goethite) + H₂SO₄. Pyrite "rusting" produces sulfuric acid and iron oxides. Limonite pseudomorphs after pyrite cubes are common (the shape survives, the mineral doesn't).
- **Dehydrates?** No
- **Radiation sensitivity:** Minimal structural damage; slight darkening possible at extreme doses

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Early pyrrhotite (if present), magnetite
- **Forms BEFORE:** Marcasite (if pH drops), chalcopyrite, galena, sphalerite
- **Commonly associated minerals:** Quartz (ubiquitous), calcite, chalcopyrite, galena, sphalerite, pyrrhotite, arsenopyrite, gold
- **Zone:** Primary/hypogene (most common). Also sedimentary (framboidal), metamorphic, supergene relict.
- **Geological environment:** Literally everywhere — hydrothermal veins, pegmatites (minor), sedimentary basins, coal beds, metamorphic rocks, VMS deposits, porphyry systems, black shales

### Famous Localities
- **Navajún, La Rioja, Spain:** World's finest pyrite cubes, up to 30+ cm, perfect geometry, often intergrown groups. The quintessential pyrite.
- **Huanzala, Peru:** Brilliant striated cubes with quartz/galena
- **Spas Heights, Illinois, USA:** Pyrite "suns" — flat radiating disc aggregates in coal shales
- **Flambeau Mine, Ladysmith, Wisconsin:** Brilliant cubes on matrix
- **Sparta, Illinois:** Pyrite suns (disc-shaped)
- **Notable specimens:** Navajún cubes are the standard — perfect geometry, brassy luster, often perched on white calcite or alone

### Fluorescence
- **Fluorescent under UV?** No (metallic minerals generally don't fluoresce)
- **SW (255nm) color:** None
- **MW (310nm) color:** None
- **LW (365nm) color:** None
- **Phosphorescent?** No
- **Activator:** N/A
- **Quenched by:** N/A

### Flavor Text
> Pyrite is the mineral that fooled a million prospectors — brass-bright cubes so perfect they look machined, striated faces catching light like burnished armor. But pyrite isn't pretending to be gold. It's been here far longer than we have. Those cubic crystals grew in the dark, one sulfur-iron layer at a time, while the mountains above them rose and eroded twice over. Striations on the faces record the competition between growth directions — a tug-of-war between {100} and {210} faces that plays out at the atomic scale. When oxygen finally reaches pyrite, it doesn't just dissolve. It burns. Sulfuric acid bleeds from the fracture, and the perfect cube becomes a rusted ghost — a limonite pseudomorph that remembers the shape but has forgotten the mineral. Navajún specimens, with their impossible geometry and matrix-free cubes, are the standard against which all others are measured. The Earth grew these on its own, no lapidary required.

## Species: Marcasite

### Identity
- **Formula:** FeS₂ (polymorph of pyrite)
- **Crystal system:** Orthorhombic, space group Pnnm
- **Mineral group:** Sulfide (disulfide)
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 6–6.5
- **Specific gravity:** 4.87–4.92 (slightly lighter than pyrite)
- **Cleavage:** {101} distinct, {110} poor
- **Fracture:** Uneven
- **Luster:** Metallic, pale brass-yellow (often slightly lighter/tarnished compared to pyrite)

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Pale bronze to brass-yellow, often with darker tarnish
- **Color causes:** Same as pyrite — intrinsic metallic bonding
- **Transparency:** Opaque
- **Streak:** Dark grey to black (slightly darker than pyrite)
- **Notable visual features:** Often forms spear-shaped or tabular crystals. Coxcomb aggregates (cockscomb) are diagnostic. Surface more prone to dark tarnish than pyrite.

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Tabular to prismatic {010}, often flattened
- **Common forms/faces:** {101}, {110}, {010}
- **Twin laws:** Contact twin on {101} — produces "spearhead" shapes, very common and diagnostic
- **Varieties:** None formally recognized (it IS a variety of FeS₂)
- **Special morphologies:** Cockscomb aggregates (radiating blade-like groups — diagnostic), reniform, botryoidal, stalactitic, fibrous masses. Often appears as crusts or concretions.

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** 25–300°C (lower T than pyrite — marcasite is the low-T polymorph)
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 30–150°C (sedimentary/low-T hydrothermal)
- **Decomposition temperature:** ~325°C irreversible transformation to pyrite (thermodynamically metastable at all T relative to pyrite, but kinetic barrier preserves it)
- **Temperature-dependent habits:** Lower T favors tabular/spear-shaped; higher T within range → more equant

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:** Fe (≥50 ppm), S (≥100 ppm as H₂S/HS⁻) — same as pyrite
- **Optional/enhancing elements:** None specific
- **Inhibiting elements:** Same as pyrite, plus high pH suppresses marcasite in favor of pyrite
- **Required pH range:** pH < 6 (acidic). Formation becomes rapid at pH < 4 (Schoonen & Barnes 1991). This is the KEY differentiator from pyrite.
- **Required Eh range:** Reducing, but less strictly than pyrite — can tolerate slight oxidation
- **Required O₂ range:** Low, but not as strictly anoxic as pyrite

#### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **On formation:** Same stoichiometry as pyrite
- **Byproducts of nucleation:** H⁺ (slightly more than pyrite due to acidic formation environment)
- **Byproducts of dissolution/oxidation:** Same as pyrite — Fe²⁺ + SO₄²⁻ + H⁺. Marcasite actually oxidizes FASTER than pyrite, which is why museum specimens deteriorate.

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Moderate, similar to pyrite
- **Maximum crystal size:** To ~10 cm (usually much smaller)
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** 0.5–3 cm
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?** Yes — slower at lower T, faster near upper limit
- **Competes with:** Pyrite (pH is the decider), siderite (FeCO₃, competes for Fe at higher pH/CO₂)

#### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** Irreversibly converts to pyrite above ~325°C. This is the crucial simulator mechanic.
- **Breaks down in light?** No direct effect, but humidity + oxygen causes "pyrite decay" (actually worse for marcasite)
- **Dissolves in water?** More readily than pyrite in oxidizing conditions
- **Dissolves in acid?** More readily than pyrite — it's less stable
- **Oxidizes?** Yes, faster than pyrite. "Marcasite decay" in museum collections is a real problem — specimens literally crumble to sulfuric acid and iron sulfate efflorescence.
- **Dehydrates?** No
- **Radiation sensitivity:** Same as pyrite

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Early pyrite (if pH was initially higher)
- **Forms BEFORE:** Goethite/limonite (upon oxidation)
- **Commonly associated minerals:** Pyrite, galena, sphalerite, calcite, siderite, quartz
- **Zone:** Low-temperature hydrothermal, sedimentary (coal measures, concretions), supergene (acidic oxidation zones)
- **Geological environment:** Sedimentary concretions (especially in chalk/limestone — "marcasite nodules"), coal beds, low-T hydrothermal veins, oxidation zones with acidic conditions

### Famous Localities
- **Cap-Blanc-Nez, Pas-de-Calais, France:** Classic marcasite concretions in chalk
- **Joplin, Missouri/Tri-State district, USA:** Cockscomb aggregates with galena/sphalerite
- **Bolivia (various mines):** Fine spear-shaped crystals
- **Chatillon-sur-Marne, France:** Radiating aggregates
- **Notable specimens:** Cockscomb groups from Joplin are the iconic habit

### Fluorescence
- **Fluorescent under UV?** No (metallic)
- **All UV bands:** None
- **Phosphorescent?** No
- **Activator:** N/A
- **Quenched by:** N/A

### Flavor Text
> Marcasite is pyrite's awkward twin — same chemistry, different personality. Where pyrite grows confident cubes, marcasite makes spearheads and rooster combs, blades stacked like folded origami in acid. It only forms when the water turns sour: below pH 6, marcasite wins the race; above it, pyrite takes over. The Nature Communications paper (2016) proved why — at acidic pH, marcasite's surfaces become more stable than pyrite's, flipping the competitive balance at the atomic level. But marcasite carries a fatal flaw: it's metastable. Heat it above 325°C and it converts irreversibly to pyrite, erasing every spear-shaped crystal and replacing it with the cubic default. Museum curators fear marcasite more than collectors love it — specimens slowly crumble in humid air, sweating sulfuric acid until the crystal becomes white powder and rust stains. The cockscomb aggregates from Joplin, Missouri — radiating blades of pale bronze — are the habit everyone remembers. Beautiful, unstable, and always a little bit doomed.

## Simulator Implementation Notes

### New Parameters Needed
- **pH threshold for polymorph selection:** pH < 5.5 → marcasite; pH > 5.5 → pyrite (with overlap zone 4.5–6.5 where both can nucleate)
- **Temperature cap for marcasite:** If T > 325°C, marcasite cannot nucleate; existing marcasite converts to pyrite

### Paragenetic Relationship
Pyrite and marcasite share identical chemistry but partition by pH:
- **High pH / neutral / alkaline → Pyrite** (the default)
- **Low pH / acidic → Marcasite** (the acid specialist)

This makes them perfect simulator pair: the player's pH slider (or acidic event) directly controls which polymorph grows.

### Nucleation Rule Pseudocode

**Pyrite:**
```
IF Fe ≥ 50ppm AND S ≥ 100ppm AND pH > 4.5 AND Eh < 0 AND T < 743°C:
  IF pH > 5.5:
    nucleate PYRITE
  ELIF pH 4.5–5.5 AND random() < 0.3:
    nucleate PYRITE  // overlap zone
```

**Marcasite:**
```
IF Fe ≥ 50ppm AND S ≥ 100ppm AND pH < 6.0 AND T < 325°C AND Eh < 0:
  IF pH < 4.5:
    nucleate MARCASITE
  ELIF pH 4.5–6.0 AND random() < 0.5:
    nucleate MARCASITE
```

### Growth Rule Pseudocode

**Pyrite:**
```
rate = base_rate * (Fe/S ratio factor) * temperature_factor
habit: IF σ < 2 → cube; IF σ 2-5 → pyritohedron; IF σ > 5 → octahedron → spherulite
IF T > 573°C: β-pyrite (slight structural shift, mainly affects twinning probability)
```

**Marcasite:**
```
rate = base_rate * (Fe/S ratio factor) * temperature_factor
habit: tabular/spearhead by default; cockscomb at higher supersaturation
IF T > 325°C: CONVERT to pyrite (irreversible, with habit reset)
```

### Habit Selection Logic

**Pyrite morphology (supersaturation-driven, per Murowchick 1991):**
- Low supersaturation → cubes {100}
- Moderate → pyritohedra {210}
- High → octahedra {111}
- Very high → spherulites / framboidal clusters

**Marcasite morphology:**
- Default → tabular {010}
- Higher supersaturation → spearhead twins on {101}
- Very high → cockscomb (radiating blade aggregates)

### Decomposition Products
- **Pyrite at 743°C →** pyrrhotite (Fe₁₋ₓS) + S vapor
- **Marcasite at 325°C →** pyrite (polymorph conversion, retains FeS₂ chemistry)
- **Both oxidize →** Fe²⁺ + SO₄²⁻ + H⁺ → eventually goethite/limonite (FeOOH/Fe₂O₃·nH₂O)

### Variants for Game

**Pyrite:**
1. **Cubic pyrite** — low supersaturation, pH > 5.5, any T. The Navajún look.
2. **Pyritohedron** — moderate supersaturation. The "classic" crystal-collector shape.
3. **Pyrite sun** — requires sedimentary environment, flat radiating habit. Rare event.
4. **Framboidal pyrite** — very high supersaturation + low T. Micro raspberry clusters. Sediment marker.

**Marcasite:**
1. **Spearhead marcasite** — pH < 4.5, moderate supersaturation. The iconic twin habit.
2. **Cockscomb marcasite** — pH < 4.5, high supersaturation. Radiating blade aggregate.
3. **Marcasite nodule** — sedimentary concretion event. Replace chalk/marly matrix.

# Research: Hematite — Vugg Simulator

**Date:** 2026-04-18
**Species:** Hematite

---

## Identity
- **Formula:** Fe₂O₃ (α-Fe₂O₃)
- **Crystal system:** Trigonal (hexagonal scalenohedral, space group R3c)
- **Mineral group:** Oxide — corundum structural group (isostructural with corundum Al₂O₃ and ilmenite FeTiO₃)
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 5.5–6.5
- **Specific gravity:** 5.26–5.30
- **Cleavage:** None (partings on {0001} and {1011})
- **Fracture:** Uneven to subconchoidal
- **Luster:** Metallic to splendent (crystals); dull to earthy (massive)

## Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Steel-gray to black in crystals; rust-red in earthy/fine-grained forms
- **Color causes:** Iron(III) — the red color is from electronic transitions in Fe³⁺ d-orbitals. Crystal hematite looks silver-black because of internal reflections and specular reflection from flat faces; pulverized or microcrystalline hematite is always red.
- **Transparency:** Opaque
- **Streak:** Bright red to dark red — diagnostic. ALWAYS red regardless of external color.
- **Notable visual features:** "Iron rose" rosettes (tabular crystals in radial arrangement), specular/micaceous habit (mirror-like flakes), botryoidal/kidney ore (reniform masses), iridescent tarnish (titaniferous varieties — "rainbow hematite")

## Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Tabular to thick tabular crystals, often in rosettes
- **Common forms/faces:** {0001} basal pinacoid dominant, {1011} rhombohedra, {1120} prism. Thin plates = "specularite." Thick tabular = common crystal form.
- **Twin laws:** Penetration twins on {0001}; lamellar twins on {1011}. Not as common as in calcite but notable.
- **Varieties:**
  - **Specularite** — micaceous/platy, mirror-bright flakes
  - **Kidney ore** — botryoidal/reniform masses, dark metallic with red streak
  - **Iron rose** — radial rosettes of tabular crystals (classic from Switzerland)
  - **Martite** — pseudomorph after magnetite (hematite replacing magnetite, retaining octahedral shape)
  - **Micaceous hematite** — thin flexible flakes (like mica but red streak)
  - **Rainbow hematite** — iridescent tarnish from Ti-rich surface layers
  - **Ocherous hematite** — earthy, red, powdery (red ochre pigment)
- **Special morphologies:** Botryoidal, stalactitic, columnar, oolitic, earthy granular

## Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** Forms from ambient (superior weathering, ~0°C) up to ~800°C (high-grade metamorphic/hydrothermal). Hydrothermal: 100–400°C optimal.
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 150–350°C for hydrothermal crystallization. Lower temps produce botryoidal/earthy forms; higher temps produce tabular crystals.
- **Decomposition temperature:** ~1,390°C (melts). Below that, stable to very high temperatures. The α→γ transition (to maghemite structure) doesn't occur on heating — maghemite converts TO hematite at ~500°C.
- **Temperature-dependent habits:** Low temp → botryoidal, earthy, oolitic. High temp → tabular crystals, iron roses. Very high temp (metamorphic) → granoblastic recrystallized masses.

### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:** Fe (as Fe³⁺ or Fe²⁺ that oxidizes), O₂
- **Optional/enhancing elements:** Ti (titaniferous hematite, rainbow tarnish), Al (substitutes in corundum structure), Mn (pyrolusite-like substitutions), V, Cr
- **Inhibiting elements:** High silica activity favors silicates over oxides. Sulfur (in reducing conditions) favors pyrite/marcasite instead. Organic matter can complex Fe and keep it in solution.
- **Required pH range:** Wide — pH 2–10. Precipitates readily from acidic to neutral. At high pH, stays dissolved longer as hydroxy complexes.
- **Required Eh range:** Oxidizing. Hematite is the fully oxidized iron oxide (Fe³⁺ only). Requires fO₂ above the magnetite-hematite buffer. Eh > 0.0 to +0.5V depending on pH.
- **Required O₂ range:** Moderate to high. This is the oxidized end-member. Where magnetite says "intermediate fO₂," hematite says "bring the oxygen."

### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **Byproducts of nucleation:** If forming from Fe²⁺ in solution, oxidation releases H⁺: 2Fe²⁺ + ½O₂ + 2H₂O → Fe₂O₃ + 4H⁺. This acidifies the fluid, which can then dissolve other minerals.
- **Byproducts of dissolution:** Dissolves in reducing acids, releasing Fe²⁺ back to fluid. In HCl: Fe₂O₃ + 6HCl → 2FeCl₃ + 3H₂O.

### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Moderate to fast. Iron oxides are among the first to precipitate when conditions are right.
- **Maximum crystal size:** Tabular crystals to ~15 cm. Iron roses to ~10 cm. Botryoidal masses can be enormous (meters).
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** 1–30 mm crystals common; specularite flakes 2–10 mm.
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?** Yes — higher temp = faster nucleation, larger crystals. Low temp = many tiny crystals, botryoidal aggregates.
- **Competes with:** Goethite (FeOOH — forms at lower temp/more hydrated conditions), magnetite (lower fO₂), pyrite (if sulfur available and reducing), siderite (if CO₂-rich and reducing).

### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** No — extremely thermally stable. Converts from nothing below 1,390°C (melting).
- **Breaks down in light?** No.
- **Dissolves in water?** Extremely insoluble. Practically inert.
- **Dissolves in acid?** Slowly in HCl. Faster in hot concentrated acid. Resistant to most weak acids.
- **Oxidizes?** Already fully oxidized — this IS the oxidation product. Cannot oxidize further.
- **Dehydrates?** No (anhydrous). But forms FROM dehydration of goethite (FeOOH → Fe₂O₃ + H₂O) at ~300°C.
- **Radiation sensitivity:** None significant. Very stable.

## Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Magnetite (in oxidation sequence: Fe²⁺ → Fe²⁺Fe³⁺₂O₄ → Fe₂O₃). Goethite can dehydrate to hematite. Pyrite/marcasite oxidize to iron oxides (goethite → hematite). Siderite (FeCO₃) oxidizes to hematite.
- **Forms BEFORE:** Nothing typically — hematite is the oxidation endpoint. It CAN be reduced back to magnetite under reducing conditions (rare in vugs).
- **Commonly associated minerals:** Quartz (ubiquitous), magnetite, goethite, pyrite (altering), chalcopyrite (oxidizing), calcite, barite, fluorite, clay minerals
- **Zone:** ALL zones — primary/hypogene (high-T hydrothermal), supergene/oxidation (low-T), weathering, sedimentary (BIF, red beds), metamorphic. The most versatile iron mineral.
- **Geological environment:** Hydrothermal veins, oxidation zones of sulfide deposits, banded iron formations, lateritic weathering profiles, volcanic fumaroles, hot springs, desert varnish

## Famous Localities

- **Classic locality 2:** Switzerland (Gotthard, Binntal) — classic iron rose rosettes

## Linked Sequence: Iron Oxide Paragenesis

Hematite belongs to the **iron oxide sequence**:
1. **Pyrite/marcasite** (FeS₂) — primary sulfide, forms first in reducing conditions
2. **Magnetite** (Fe₃O₄) — forms at intermediate fO₂, moderate-high T
3. **Hematite** (Fe₂O₃) — forms at high fO₂, the oxidation endpoint
4. **Goethite** (FeOOH) — hydrated iron oxide, forms at low T in weathering zones (can dehydrate to hematite at ~300°C)

The oxidation progression: reducing → magnetite → hematite. Weathering adds hydration → goethite. Heating removes it → back to hematite. It's a cycle.

# Research: Pyromorphite — Vugg Simulator

## Species: Pyromorphite

### Identity
- **Formula:** Pb₅(PO₄)₃Cl
- **Crystal system:** Hexagonal (6/m, space group P6₃/m)
- **Mineral group:** Phosphate — Apatite group
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 3.5–4
- **Specific gravity:** 6.9–7.1 (one of the densest non-metallic minerals)
- **Cleavage:** Imperfect on {10ī1}
- **Fracture:** Uneven to sub-conchoidal
- **Luster:** Resinous to subadamantine, sometimes greasy

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Bright green to grass-green, yellow-green, brownish-green, orange, brown, gray, white; may be multicolored green and orange
- **Color causes:** Chromophoric impurities; Cr³⁺ and V⁴⁺ can produce green hues; Mn and Fe contribute brown/orange tones. Some specimens show compositional zoning (green pyromorphite grading into orange vanadinite on same crystal).
- **Transparency:** Transparent to translucent
- **Streak:** White
- **Notable visual features:** Barrel-shaped hexagonal crystals with hopper growth patterns on terminations; some larger crystals hollowed out on ends. Resinous luster gives a wet-looking, almost candy-like appearance.

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Prismatic hexagonal barrels terminated by basal pinacoid and narrow hexagonal pyramid faces
- **Common forms/faces:** {10ī0} prism, {0001} basal pinacoid, {10ī1} pyramid
- **Twin laws:** Rare on {1122}
- **Varieties:** Campylite (barrel-shaped, curved hemispherical aggregates — name shared with mimetite); Ca-rich varieties (polysphaerite, miesite) lighter in color and density
- **Special morphologies:** Acicular (needle-like sprays), botryoidal, reniform, radiating, globular, crusty. Larger crystals may show hopper growth.

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** Near-ambient to ~80°C (supergene/oxidation zone)
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 15–40°C (ambient surface temperatures)
- **Decomposition temperature:** ~1000°C (melts — named from Greek "pyr" + "morfe" = fire form, because it recrystallizes after melting)
- **Temperature-dependent habits:** Lower T favors fine acicular sprays; moderate T produces barrel-shaped crystals; botryoidal forms in low-energy environments

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:** Pb²⁺ (>50 ppm), PO₄³⁻ (>10 ppm), Cl⁻ (>5 ppm)
- **Optional/enhancing elements:** As (substitutes for P → mimetite series), V (substitutes for P → vanadinite series), Ca (substitutes for Pb → lowers density), Cr (green coloration), F⁻ (substitutes for Cl⁻ → fluorpyromorphite end-member)
- **Inhibiting elements:** High SO₄²⁻ (competes for Pb → anglesite preferentially), high CO₃²⁻ (→ cerussite)
- **Required pH range:** 2–7 (forms across wide range; optimal 3–6)
- **Required Eh range:** Oxidizing (supergene zone — Pb must be mobilized from galena oxidation)
- **Required O₂ range:** High — requires oxidizing conditions to break down primary galena

#### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **Does it release any chemicals when forming?** Consumes Pb²⁺, PO₄³⁻, and Cl⁻ from solution. Extremely low solubility product (Ksp ~10⁻⁸⁴) — effectively removes Pb from solution permanently.
- **Byproducts of nucleation:** None — precipitation reaction
- **Byproducts of dissolution/decomposition:** Dissolves in nitric acid (HNO₃), releasing Pb²⁺, PO₄³⁻, Cl⁻

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Moderate — faster than cerussite, slower than anglesite initial precipitation
- **Maximum crystal size:** To ~10 cm (individual crystals), vug crusts can cover meters
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** 0.5–3 cm barrels, 1–5 mm acicular sprays
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?** Minimal — supergene formation is largely temperature-independent
- **Competes with:** Anglesite (PbSO₄), cerussite (PbCO₃), wulfenite (PbMoO₄) — all competing for Pb²⁺. Pyromorphite wins when phosphate is abundant due to extremely low Ksp.

#### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** Melts ~1000°C; recrystallizes on cooling (hence the name)
- **Breaks down in light?** No — stable
- **Dissolves in water?** Extremely low solubility (Ksp ~10⁻⁸⁴) — essentially insoluble
- **Dissolves in acid?** Dissolves in HNO₃ and HCl
- **Oxidizes?** No — already fully oxidized (Pb²⁺, PO₄³⁻, Cl⁻ all in stable oxidation states)
- **Dehydrates?** No — anhydrous
- **Radiation sensitivity:** None significant

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Galena (PbS) oxidizes → releases Pb²⁺; arsenopyrite/sulfides break down → provide acidity; apatite/rock phosphate weathers → releases PO₄³⁻
- **Forms BEFORE:** May be pseudomorphosed by other minerals; generally a late-stage supergene product
- **Commonly associated minerals:** Galena, cerussite, anglesite, wulfenite, limonite, mimetite, vanadinite, barite, quartz
- **Zone:** Supergene/oxidation zone — above water table
- **Geological environment:** Oxidized zones of lead deposits, especially in arid climates; also forms as biomineral (fungus Paecilomyces javanicus precipitates it from Pb-contaminated soil)

### Famous Localities
- **Classic locality 1:** Daoping Mine, Guangxi, China — spectacular green sprays and barrels
- **Classic locality 2:** Les Farges Mine, Ussel, Corrèze, France — classic bright green crystals
- **Classic locality 3:** Broken Hill, New South Wales, Australia — large crystals
- **Notable specimens:** Hoppered barrel crystals from China can reach 5+ cm; Les Farges produced some of the finest classic European specimens

### Fluorescence
- **Fluorescent under UV?** Weakly, in some specimens
- **SW (255nm) color:** Yellow to orange (rare)
- **MW (310nm) color:** Weak yellow
- **LW (365nm) color:** Yellow to orange (occasionally)
- **Phosphorescent?** No
- **Activator:** REE elements, possibly Mn²⁺ substituting for Pb²⁺
- **Quenched by:** Fe²⁺

### Flavor Text
> Pyromorphite is the mineral that wins by showing up last. Galena oxidizes, lead floods into solution, and a dozen minerals scramble to grab it — anglesite, cerussite, wulfenite, all decently insoluble. But pyromorphite's solubility product is 10⁻⁸⁴. That's not a typo. It doesn't just precipitate; it *vacuums* lead out of groundwater with the finality of a black hole. Named from the Greek for "fire form" because it crystallizes from its own melt — if you heat it to 1000°C, it flows, and as it cools it grows those perfect hexagonal barrels again. The lead oxidation zone's final word. The colon at the end of the sentence.

### Simulator Implementation Notes
- **New parameters needed:** trace_P (phosphorus/phosphate) — already in the 30-element trace list; trace_Cl — already in the list
- **New events needed:** Supergene oxidation event (galena breakdown releasing Pb²⁺); phosphate availability pulse (weathering of apatite/phosphate-bearing wall rock)
- **Nucleation rule pseudocode:**
```
IF temperature < 80°C
AND Eh > 0.3 (oxidizing)
AND trace_Pb > 50 ppm
AND trace_P > 10 ppm
AND trace_Cl > 5 ppm
AND pH 2-7
AND galena_present OR Pb_in_solution > threshold
→ nucleate pyromorphite
```
- **Growth rule pseudocode:**
```
IF sigma_pyromorphite > 1.0
→ grow at rate 3 (moderate)
Habit: if growth_rate_high → barrel; if acicular_seed → needle spray; if low_energy → botryoidal
```
- **Habit selection logic:** 
  - High supersaturation + rapid growth → hoppered barrels
  - Moderate σ + available nucleation sites → normal hexagonal prisms
  - Low σ + limited space → acicular sprays
  - Very low σ + curved substrate → botryoidal/reniform crusts
- **Decomposition products:** None under supergene conditions — extremely stable

### Variants for Game
- **Variant 1: Classic Green Barrel** — bright grass-green hexagonal barrel crystals. Conditions: high PO₄, moderate Pb, pH 4-6
- **Variant 2: Orange-Brown Acicular Spray** — needle-like radiating sprays, orange-brown color. Conditions: higher As content (mimetite series), slightly acidic pH 3-4
- **Variant 3: Campylite** — barrel-shaped crystals forming curved hemispherical aggregates. Conditions: moderate growth rate, curved substrate, mixed P/As chemistry

### Solid Solution Series Notes
Pyromorphite forms complete solid solutions with:
- **Mimetite** Pb₅(AsO₄)₃Cl — PO₄↔AsO₄ substitution
- **Vanadinite** Pb₅(VO₄)₃Cl — PO₄↔VO₄ substitution
- In game: intermediate compositions possible based on P:As:V ratio in broth
- Color shifts: P-dominant → green; As-dominant → yellow/brown; V-dominant → red/orange

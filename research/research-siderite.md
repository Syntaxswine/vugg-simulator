# Research: Siderite — Vugg Simulator

## Species: Siderite

### Identity
- **Formula:** FeCO₃
- **Crystal system:** Trigonal (hexagonal scalenohedral, space group R3c)
- **Mineral group:** Carbonate (calcite group)
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 3.75–4.25
- **Specific gravity:** 3.96
- **Cleavage:** Perfect on {1011} (rhombohedral, 3 directions at ~75°/105°)
- **Fracture:** Uneven to conchoidal; brittle
- **Luster:** Vitreous, may be silky to pearly

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Pale yellow, tan, brown, grey-brown; sometimes greenish, reddish-brown, or nearly black (Mn-rich)
- **Color causes:** Fe²⁺ is the chromophore. Pure FeCO₃ = yellowish-tan to brown. Mn substitution darkens toward brown/black. Weathered surfaces oxidize to dark brown/black iron oxides (limonite coating).
- **Transparency:** Translucent to subtranslucent
- **Streak:** White (distinctive — much lighter than the mineral's surface color suggests)
- **Notable visual features:** Curved rhombohedral faces (saddle-shaped), iridescent surface oxidation (sometimes rainbow tarnish), concretions that preserve 3D fossils. Strong birefringence (δ = 0.242 — one of the highest of any common mineral)

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Rhombohedral crystals, often with curved (saddle-shaped) faces
- **Common forms/faces:** {1011} rhombohedron, sometimes {0221} scalenohedron
- **Twin laws:** Lamellar on {0112} — uncommon
- **Varieties:**
  - **Spathic siderite:** Coarse crystalline, historically important iron ore ("spathic iron")
  - **Sphaerosiderite:** Spherical concretions in soils/sediments, used as paleoclimate proxies
  - **Clay ironstone:** Fine-grained massive siderite in sedimentary beds
- **Special morphologies:** Botryoidal, concretionary (often with fossil preservation), tabular, curved crystals, massive. Concretions can be perfectly spherical.

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** 0–350°C (wide range — forms from surface temp diagenesis to moderate hydrothermal)
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 50–250°C
- **Decomposition temperature:** ~400–500°C (FeCO₃ → FeO + CO₂). In oxidizing conditions, decomposes to hematite/magnetite at lower temperatures (~200–300°C with O₂ present)
- **Temperature-dependent habits:** Low T (diagenetic) → concretions, microcrystalline. Moderate T → curved rhombohedra. High T → sharper crystals

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:** Fe²⁺ (primary), CO₃²⁻ or dissolved CO₂
- **Optional/enhancing elements:** Mn²⁺ (solid solution → rhodochrosite, darkens color), Mg²⁺ (solid solution → magnesite), Ca²⁺ (limited substitution), Zn²⁺ (solid solution → smithsonite)
- **Inhibiting elements:** High O₂ (oxidizes Fe²⁺ to Fe³⁺ → iron oxides/hydroxides instead), high S²⁻ (forms pyrite/marcasite preferentially in sulfur-rich systems)
- **Required pH range:** Neutral to alkaline (7–9). Below ~6, siderite dissolves.
- **Required Eh range:** Reducing. Fe²⁺ must be stable. Eh < ~0.2V at neutral pH.
- **Required O₂ range:** Very low. Any significant O₂ oxidizes Fe²⁺ → goethite/limonite instead.

#### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **Byproducts of nucleation:** Removes Fe²⁺ and CO₃²⁻ from solution. Slightly raises pH (consumes acidity).
- **Byproducts of dissolution:** Releases Fe²⁺ + HCO₃⁻ (can acidify downstream if O₂ oxidizes the Fe²⁺)
- **Byproducts of decomposition (heat):** FeO (wüstite) + CO₂. In air: Fe₂O₃ (hematite) + CO₂.

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Moderate (comparable to calcite, slightly slower than rhodochrosite)
- **Maximum crystal size:** Rhombohedra to ~30 cm (exceptional). Concretions to meters.
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** 1–15 cm for well-formed crystals
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?** Yes, faster at moderate T (100–200°C)
- **Competes with:** Pyrite/marcasite (in S-rich systems, Fe prefers sulfide), calcite (if Ca>>Fe), goethite/limonite (if O₂ is available), ankerite (CaFe(CO₃)₂ if Ca present)

#### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** ~400–500°C → FeO + CO₂ (reducing) or Fe₂O₃ + CO₂ (oxidizing)
- **Breaks down in light?** No
- **Dissolves in water?** Slightly soluble (Ksp ~10⁻¹⁰·⁹), dissolves readily in acid
- **Dissolves in acid?** Yes, vigorously in HCl with effervescence
- **Oxidizes?** YES — major instability. Surface oxidizes to limonite/goethite (brown-black coating). In prolonged oxidation: FeCO₃ + ½O₂ → FeOOH (goethite) + CO₂. This is why siderite specimens often have brown rinds.
- **Dehydrates?** No (anhydrous carbonate)
- **Radiation sensitivity:** Not notable

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Primary Fe-bearing minerals (pyrite, magnetite, Fe-silicates) have been altered/dissolved in reducing CO₂-rich conditions
- **Forms BEFORE:** Iron oxides/hydroxides (goethite, hematite, limonite) form when conditions become more oxidizing and siderite breaks down
- **Commonly associated minerals:** Barite, fluorite, galena, sphalerite, chalcopyrite, quartz, calcite, rhodochrosite, ankerite, dolomite
- **Zone:** Primary (hydrothermal veins), diagenetic (sedimentary concretions), supergene (reducing zones of ore deposits)
- **Geological environment:** Hydrothermal veins, sedimentary rocks (shales, sandstones, coal measures — concretions), bog iron environments, banded iron formations (some metamorphosed to siderite), marine Mn-Fe-rich sediments

### Famous Localities
- **Panasqueira, Portugal:** Saddle-shaped curved rhombohedra with arsenopyrite, quartz, cassiterite
- **Tincroft/Carn Brea, Cornwall, England:** Classic hydrothermal vein siderite
- **Eagle Mine, Gilman, Colorado:** Sharp rhombohedra with pyrite, sphalerite
- **Muzo, Colombia:** Green siderite crystals with emerald (unusual Cr-bearing variety)
- **Mazon Creek, Illinois:** Fossil-bearing concretions (Carboniferous, preserve 3D plants and animals)
- **Notable specimens:** Curved "saddle" rhombohedra from Panasqueira are iconic. Mazon Creek concretions preserve soft-bodied fossils (Tullimonstrum gregarium, Illinois state fossil).

### Flavor Text

> Siderite is what iron does when it's being polite. Not the violent rust of hematite, not the magnetic authority of magnetite, not the brassy swagger of pyrite — just a quiet tan rhombohedron sitting in a vein, curved like a horse's saddle, waiting to be cracked open. It's an iron ore that doesn't look like iron ore. White streak. Pale brown. You'd walk past it. But inside every siderite crystal is 48% iron, locked in carbonate prison, released only by fire or acid. The Mazon Creek concretions in Illinois are siderite — little brown nodules that split open to reveal 300-million-year-old ferns and jellyfish, preserved in three dimensions because iron carbonate cemented around them before decay could finish. The mineral that entombs the dead and releases them unchanged. On Mars, orbital spectroscopy has detected siderite in ancient lakebeds — proof that the red planet once had reducing waters, CO₂-rich, with iron dissolved in solution. The same quiet mineral on two worlds, keeping the same secrets.

### Simulator Implementation Notes
- **New parameters needed:** None — uses existing Fe and CO₃²⁻
- **New events needed:** Siderite oxidation event — if O₂ rises above threshold, siderite converts to goethite/limonite + releases CO₂
- **Nucleation rule pseudocode:**
```
IF trace_Fe > threshold AND CO3_available AND pH > 6.5 AND Eh < reducing_threshold AND S_low (else pyrite forms):
  σ = (Fe²⁺ × CO₃²⁻) / Ksp_siderite
  IF σ > 1 → nucleate
  Priority: if S²⁻ present AND Fe²⁺ available → pyrite forms FIRST, siderite only gets leftover Fe
```
- **Growth rule pseudocode:**
```
IF siderite exists AND σ > 1:
  growth_rate = base_rate × (σ - 1)
  IF Mn²⁺ present → incorporate into solid solution (darkens color toward rhodochrosite end)
  IF Mg²⁺ present → incorporate (shifts toward magnesite)
  IF O₂ rises above threshold → convert siderite → goethite + CO₂
```
- **Habit selection logic:**
  - T > 150°C AND space available → sharp rhombohedral crystals (sometimes curved saddle-shape)
  - T < 80°C AND sedimentary environment → concretions (spherical growth)
  - Confined, massive → granular/fine-grained fill ("clay ironstone")
- **Decomposition products:** FeO + CO₂ (heat, reducing) or Fe₂O₃/FeOOH + CO₂ (heat + O₂). Oxidation at surface T: FeOOH (goethite/limonite) + CO₂.

### Variants for Game
- **Variant 1: Spathic Siderite** — Coarse crystalline, tan to brown curved rhombohedra. Classic hydrothermal vein form. Moderate T, open space.
- **Variant 2: Sphaerosiderite** — Spherical microcrystalline concretions. Low T diagenetic, sedimentary. Not a display specimen but geologically important — paleoclimate proxy.
- **Variant 3: Green Siderite** — Cr-bearing variety (Muzo type). Green color instead of brown. Needs trace Cr in broth. Very rare, associated with emerald deposits.
- **Variant 4: Oligonite** — Fe-Mn intermediate in the siderite-rhodochrosite solid solution. Brown-rose color. Both Fe and Mn contribute. This is the bridge between the two endpoints.

### Solid Solution Relationships
- **Rhodochrosite series:** Complete (Fe,Mn)CO₃. Color transitions from rose-red (Mn) → brown-rose (mix) → tan-brown (Fe).
- **Magnesite series:** (Fe,Mg)CO₃. Mg-rich = "breunnerite" (historical). Continuous.
- **Smithsonite series:** (Fe,Zn)CO₃. Limited — Zn doesn't substitute freely for Fe.

# Research: Vanadinite — Vugg Simulator

## Species: Vanadinite

### Identity
- **Formula:** Pb₅(VO₄)₃Cl
- **Crystal system:** Hexagonal (6/m, space group P6₃/m)
- **Mineral group:** Vanadate — Apatite group
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 3–4
- **Specific gravity:** 6.8–7.1
- **Cleavage:** None
- **Fracture:** Uneven to conchoidal
- **Luster:** Resinous to subadamantine

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Bright red, orange-red, red-brown, brown, yellow, whitish, grey, rarely colorless; may be concentrically zoned
- **Color causes:** V⁵⁺ in tetrahedral coordination — intrinsic vanadate chromophore produces the red-orange hues. V→P substitution (toward pyromorphite) shifts color toward green. V→As substitution (toward mimetite) shifts toward brown/yellow.
- **Transparency:** Transparent, translucent, to opaque
- **Streak:** Brownish-yellow (distinctive — redder mineral but yellow streak)
- **Notable visual features:** Vivid blood-red to orange-red hexagonal prisms are among the most recognizable minerals in any collection. May show concentric color zoning (red core, yellow rim or vice versa).

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Prismatic hexagonal crystals, often as flat-topped tablets or stubby barrels; nodular aggregates
- **Common forms/faces:** {10ī0} prism dominant, {0001} basal pinacoid, {10ī1} pyramid
- **Twin laws:** None commonly reported
- **Varieties:** Endlichite (intermediate vanadinite-mimetite, now discredited as distinct species but used for V:As ~1:1 material); Arsenovanadinite (As-bearing variety)
- **Special morphologies:** Acicular, hair-like, fibrous (rare); rounded globular forms; dense hexagonal plates. Mibladen crystals are typically stout, flat-topped hexagonal prisms.

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** Near-ambient to ~80°C (supergene)
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 15–40°C (arid climate surface temperatures)
- **Decomposition temperature:** ~1910°C (very high melting point)
- **Temperature-dependent habits:** Stout prisms in moderate conditions; acicular at high supersaturation

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:** Pb²⁺ (>50 ppm), VO₄³⁻ (>10 ppm), Cl⁻ (>5 ppm)
- **Optional/enhancing elements:** P (→ pyromorphite series), As (→ mimetite series), Ca (→ calcium vanadates like tyuyamunite)
- **Inhibiting elements:** High PO₄³⁻ (→ pyromorphite preferentially); high SO₄²⁻ (→ anglesite); high CO₃²⁻ (→ cerussite); high Ca (→ descloizite PbZn(VO₄)(OH) or mottramite PbCu(VO₄)(OH) compete for V)
- **Required pH range:** Near-neutral to slightly alkaline (pH 6–8); more alkaline than pyromorphite/mimetite
- **Required Eh range:** Strongly oxidizing — V must be in +5 oxidation state
- **Required O₂ range:** High — V oxidation from V³⁺/V⁴⁺ in wall-rock silicates to V⁵⁺ requires aggressive oxidation

#### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **Byproducts of nucleation:** Consumes Pb²⁺, VO₄³⁻, Cl⁻. Low solubility product.
- **Byproducts of dissolution:** Dissolves in acids releasing Pb²⁺, VO₄³⁻ (vanadium in solution)

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Moderate — comparable to pyromorphite
- **Maximum crystal size:** To ~10 cm (rare; Mibladen produced crystals to 5+ cm)
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** 0.5–3 cm stout hexagonal prisms
- **Competes with:** Descloizite (PbZn(VO₄)(OH)), mottramite (PbCu(VO₄)(OH)), tyuyamunite (Ca(UO₂)₂(VO₄)₂) — all compete for vanadium; pyromorphite and cerussite compete for Pb

#### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** Very high melting point ~1910°C — extremely thermally stable
- **Breaks down in light?** No — stable
- **Dissolves in water?** Low solubility but higher than pyromorphite
- **Dissolves in acid?** Dissolves in HNO₃, HCl
- **Oxidizes?** No — V already in +5 highest oxidation state
- **Dehydrates?** No — anhydrous

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Galena oxidation (Pb²⁺); wall-rock silicate weathering (V leached from V-bearing micas, clays, iron oxides); chlorapatite breakdown (Cl⁻). Vanadium is typically trace in silicates, concentrated by weathering.
- **Forms BEFORE:** Often one of the latest supergene minerals; may be coated by descloizite or iron oxides
- **Commonly associated minerals:** Galena, wulfenite, cerussite, anglesite, pyromorphite, mimetite, descloizite, mottramite, barite, limonite, calcite
- **Zone:** Supergene/oxidation zone — exclusively in arid climates (V leaches away in wet climates)
- **Geological environment:** Oxidized zones of lead deposits in arid regions — vanadium must not be washed away by rainfall before mineralizing. This is why Morocco, Arizona, and Namibia produce the best specimens.

### Famous Localities
- **Classic locality 1:** Mibladen, Morocco — THE vanadinite locality. Dense blood-red hexagonal prisms on matrix, by far the most specimens in collections worldwide. Also Touissit (yellow crystals) and Taouz (on goethite).
- **Classic locality 2:** Arizona, USA — Apache Mine (Globe-Miami District), Old Yuma Mine, Rowley Mine. Red to brown crystals.
- **Classic locality 3:** Broken Hill, Zambia; Abenab, Namibia (very large crystals coated with descloizite)
- **Notable specimens:** Mibladen plates with dozens of sharp red hexagonal prisms to 3+ cm are iconic. Original discovery: Zimapán, Mexico, 1801 by Andrés Manuel del Río (who also discovered vanadium itself).

### Fluorescence
- **Fluorescent under UV?** Some specimens — red-orange under near-UV (405nm); generally weak or absent under standard SW/LW
- **SW (255nm) color:** Generally non-fluorescent
- **MW (310nm) color:** Generally non-fluorescent
- **LW (365nm) color:** Generally non-fluorescent to very weak
- **Phosphorescent?** No
- **Activator:** Possible weak response from REE or uranyl impurities
- **Quenched by:** Pb²⁺ absorption, Fe²⁺

### Flavor Text
> Vanadinite is a desert mineral. It needs the same conditions as pyromorphite and mimetite — lead in solution, oxidizing water, chloride — but it also needs vanadium, and vanadium is a wanderer. It leaches from silicate wall-rock into groundwater, and in wet climates it washes away before it can crystallize. Only in deserts does the vanadium stick around long enough to meet lead in the oxidation zone. The result: blood-red hexagonal prisms that look like they were carved from sealing wax. Andrés Manuel del Río discovered vanadinite in Mexico in 1801 and realized it contained a new element. He named it erythronium. The European establishment told him he was wrong — it was just chromium. Twenty-nine years later, Sefström "discovered" vanadium in Sweden. Del Río was right all along. The mineral remembered what the chemists forgot.

### Simulator Implementation Notes
- **New parameters needed:** trace_V (already in 30-element list)
- **New events needed:** Arid climate constraint — vanadinite should only form in low-rainfall scenarios or well-drained oxidation zones
- **Nucleation rule pseudocode:**
```
IF temperature < 80°C
AND Eh > 0.4 (strongly oxidizing, V must be +5)
AND trace_Pb > 50 ppm
AND trace_V > 10 ppm
AND trace_Cl > 5 ppm
AND pH 6-8
AND V:P ratio favors vanadinite (V > P, V > As)
AND arid_conditions OR well_drained
→ nucleate vanadinite
```
- **Growth rule pseudocode:**
```
IF sigma_vanadinite > 1.0
→ grow at rate 3
Habit: if moderate_σ + good_space → stout hexagonal prism; if high_σ → acicular; if crowded → nodular
```
- **Habit selection logic:**
  - Stout flat-topped hexagonal prisms: moderate supersaturation, good growth space (Mibladen style)
  - Acicular/hair-like: high supersaturation, rapid growth
  - Nodular/globular: limited space, curved substrate
- **Decomposition products:** Extremely stable under supergene conditions; may be coated by descloizite (PbZnVO₄OH) if Zn available

### Variants for Game
- **Variant 1: Blood-Red Hexagonal Prism** — classic Mibladen-style dense red prisms. Conditions: high V, high Pb, pH 7, arid
- **Variant 2: Orange-Yellow Tablet** — flatter yellow-orange crystals (Touissit style). Conditions: moderate V, some As substitution
- **Variant 3: Endlichite Intermediate** — V:As ~1:1, brownish-yellow. Conditions: mixed V and As in broth, both >P

### Desert Constraint Note
Vanadinite is uniquely climate-dependent. Unlike pyromorphite and mimetite which form wherever there's lead and phosphate/arsenate, vanadinite requires:
1. V leached from silicate wall-rock (slow process)
2. V NOT leached away by rainfall (requires aridity)
3. Strongly oxidizing conditions (V³⁺ → V⁵⁺)

This makes it a mineral of dry places — Morocco, Arizona, Namibia, Mexico. In the simulator, this could be modeled as a minimum aridity threshold or maximum water-flush rate.

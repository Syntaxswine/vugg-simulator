# Mineral Species Template — Vugg Simulator

## Species: Apatite

### Identity
- **Formula:** Ca₅(PO₄)₃(F,Cl,OH) — three end-members: fluorapatite, chlorapatite, hydroxylapatite
- **Crystal system:** Hexagonal (6/m)
- **Mineral group:** Phosphate (apatite supergroup — structural archetype for pyromorphite, mimetite, vanadinite)
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 5 (the reference mineral on the Mohs scale)
- **Specific gravity:** 3.1–3.3
- **Cleavage:** {0001} imperfect, {1010} poor
- **Fracture:** Conchoidal to uneven
- **Luster:** Vitreous to subresinous; crystal faces often bright, broken surfaces greasy

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Green, blue-green, blue, violet, yellow, brown, white, colorless — one of the most chromatically diverse minerals
- **Color causes:** Mn²⁺ (blue-violet), REE (yellow-green, pink), Fe²⁺ (blue), Cr³⁺ (green), radiation damage centers (blue), trace Cl/F ratio shifts
- **Transparency:** Transparent to translucent
- **Streak:** White
- **Notable visual features:** Cat's eye chatoyancy when fibrous (chatoyant apatite from Sri Lanka, Brazil). Asparagus stone (yellow-green). Some specimens show weak pleochroism (blue/violet ↔ colorless).

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Prismatic hexagonal crystals with pinacoid termination, often elongated along c-axis
- **Common forms/faces:** {1010} prism, {0001} pinacoid, {1011} dipyramid. Tabular habits when prism faces suppressed.
- **Twin laws:** Contact twins on {1121} (rare)
- **Varieties:**
  - **Fluorapatite** — F-dominant end-member. Most common in igneous rocks.
  - **Chlorapatite** — Cl-dominant. Metamorphic/metasomatic environments.
  - **Hydroxylapatite** — OH-dominant. Biological apatite (bones, teeth). Also in some hydrothermal veins.
  - **Asparagus stone** — yellow-green transparent crystals (classic: Murcia, Spain)
  - **Manganapatite** — Mn-bearing, blue-violet
  - **Staffelite** — carbonate-rich apatite (citrus-colored, botryoidal, from Staffel, Germany)
- **Special morphologies:** Botryoidal (carbonate-rich varieties), massive, granular, fibrous (chatoyant), compact nodular (phosphorite beds)

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** 50–900°C (extremely wide stability field)
- **Optimal growth temperature:**
  - Hydrothermal vein: 200–400°C
  - Pegmatitic: 400–700°C
  - Sedimentary/phosphorite: <50°C (diagenetic)
  - Metamorphic: 300–600°C
- **Decomposition temperature:** ~1500°C (melting). No low-T decomposition.
- **Temperature-dependent habits:** Higher T → larger, well-formed prismatic crystals. Low-T diagenetic → cryptocrystalline/microcrystalline collophane.

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:** Ca (high, >5%), P (as PO₄³⁻, moderate)
- **Optional/enhancing elements:**
  - F → fluorapatite (most stable)
  - Cl → chlorapatite
  - OH → hydroxylapatite (low-F fluids)
  - Mn²⁺ → blue/violet color
  - REE (especially Ce, Nd) → yellow-green color, common substitution for Ca
  - Sr²⁺ → substitutes for Ca (strontian apatite)
  - CO₃²⁻ → carbonate-apatite (francolite/staffelite), common in sedimentary settings
- **Inhibiting elements:** High Fe³⁺ can suppress crystallization (competes for phosphate)
- **Required pH range:** Neutral to slightly alkaline (pH 7–9). Apatite solubility increases sharply at pH <5.
- **Required Eh range:** Not strongly Eh-sensitive (phosphate is stable across redox conditions). Forms in both reducing and oxidizing environments.
- **Required O₂ range:** Not critical.

#### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **Byproducts of nucleation:** Consumes Ca²⁺ and PO₄³⁻ from solution. If F-poor, releases H⁺ (OH⁻ incorporation → water splitting).
- **Byproducts of dissolution:** Releases Ca²⁺, PO₄³⁻, and F⁻/Cl⁻ back to fluid. Dissolution increases pH slightly (consumes H⁺).

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Moderate — slower than calcite, faster than most silicates
- **Maximum crystal size:** Prismatic crystals to >1 m in pegmatites (large examples from Renfrew, Ontario). Typical hydrothermal vug crystals: 1–15 cm.
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** 2–50 mm prisms
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?** Yes — higher T significantly faster. Retrograde solubility not significant for apatite (unlike calcite).
- **Competes with:** Calcite (also consumes Ca), monazite (also consumes REE+PO₄), wavellite/variscite (secondary phosphates in weathering zones)

#### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** No (stable to very high temperatures, melts ~1500°C)
- **Breaks down in light?** No
- **Dissolves in water?** Very low solubility (~0.003 g/L). But dissolves readily in acidic groundwater (pH <5) — this is why tooth decay works.
- **Dissolves in acid?** Yes — HCl, HNO₃ dissolve readily. This is the basis of phosphoric acid production from phosphate rock.
- **Oxidizes?** No (phosphate is fully oxidized already)
- **Dehydrates?** No (hydroxylapatite is not a hydrate; the OH⁻ is structural)
- **Radiation sensitivity:** Can develop blue color centers from radiation damage (Sm³⁺ → Sm²⁺ reduction, or electron trapping at F⁻ vacancies). Natural apatite from radioactive environments often shows darker blue/violet colors.

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Primary mineral in many environments (doesn't require a precursor). In oxidation zones: forms after primary phosphates or after P-bearing fluids interact with Ca.
- **Forms BEFORE:** Can be pseudomorphosed by pyromorphite (Pb replaces Ca in oxidation zone). Weathering produces secondary phosphates (wavellite, variscite, strengite).
- **Commonly associated minerals:**
  - Igneous: biotite, magnetite, zircon, titanite, monazite
  - Pegmatite: tourmaline, beryl, feldspar, quartz, lepidolite
  - Hydrothermal: fluorite, calcite, quartz, sulfides
  - Metamorphic: calcite, diopside, garnet, vesuvianite
- **Zone:** All zones. Magmatic (primary accessory), pegmatitic (late-stage), hydrothermal (vein), metamorphic (skarn), sedimentary (phosphorite/diagenetic), supergene (residual).
- **Geological environment:** Literally everywhere. It is the most common phosphate mineral on Earth. Found in igneous rocks (accessory), pegmatites, hydrothermal veins, metamorphic marbles, sedimentary phosphorites, and even biological systems.

### Famous Localities
- **Classic locality 1:** Panasqueira, Portugal — excellent blue-green prismatic crystals with arsenopyrite, quartz
- **Classic locality 2:** Cerro de Mercado, Durango, Mexico — large yellow-green transparent crystals in iron ore
- **Classic locality 3:** Renfrew/Hull, Ontario, Canada — large green/brown prisms in calcite veins
- **Notable specimens:**
  - Durango crystals to 20+ cm, gem-quality yellow-green
  - Panasqueira blue crystals prized by collectors
  - Bihar/India manganapatites — deep violet-blue
  - Sri Lanka chatoyant cat's eye apatite (gemstone)
  - Murcia, Spain — "asparagus stone" yellow-green
  - Kristiansand, Norway — large blue crystals
  - Snarum, Norway — blue apatite with rutile inclusions

### Fluorescence
- **Fluorescent under UV?** Yes — often strongly
- **SW (255nm) color:** Blue-violet to pink (REE-activated), or yellow-white
- **MW (310nm) color:** Similar to SW, often slightly weaker
- **LW (365nm) color:** Variable — yellow, pink, blue-violet, or cream
- **Phosphorescent?** Sometimes — brief afterglow in some specimens
- **Activator:** Mn²⁺ (yellow-orange), REE²⁺ especially Eu²⁺ (blue), Ce³⁺ (blue-violet), Sm³⁺ (pink), Dy³⁺ (white-yellow), UO₂²⁺ (green)
- **Quenched by:** Fe²⁺ (iron strongly quenches fluorescence — iron-bearing apatite is typically non-fluorescent)

### Flavor Text
> Apatite is the mineral that named an entire supergroup — the structural template for pyromorphite, mimetite, and vanadinite, three minerals already growing in this vug. It crystallizes in hexagonal prisms in nearly every geological environment on Earth, from magma chambers to your own skeleton (hydroxylapatite is what bones are made of). At Mohs 5, it literally defines the midpoint of the hardness scale. The name comes from the Greek *apate*, "deceit" — it was so often confused with beryl, tourmaline, and olivine that Werner named it for its talent at impersonation. In hydrothermal veins it grows beside fluorite and quartz, quietly hoarding calcium and phosphate while flashier minerals get the attention. But examine those prismatic crystals under UV: the rare earth elements trapped in the lattice light up blue, pink, and yellow, revealing the geochemical diary of the fluid that made them.

### Simulator Implementation Notes
- **New parameters needed:** Ca (critical — needed for many future minerals), PO₄ (phosphate — not yet tracked)
- **New events needed:** Phosphate pulse event (P released from magmatic differentiation or organic matter breakdown)
- **Nucleation rule pseudocode:**
```
IF Ca > 5% AND P > 500ppm AND pH > 6.5 AND T > 50°C → nucleate apatite
F-content > 50% → fluorapatite variant
Cl-content > 50% → chlorapatite variant
ELSE → hydroxylapatite variant
```
- **Growth rule pseudocode:**
```
IF σ_apatite > 1.0 AND Ca available AND PO₄ available → grow at rate 3 (moderate)
Mn²⁺ present → blue-violet color variant
REE present → yellow-green color variant
```
- **Habit selection logic:**
  - High T (>400°C): large prismatic, well-formed hexagonal
  - Moderate T (200-400°C): elongated prisms, may show dipyramid terminations
  - Low T (<100°C, diagenetic): cryptocrystalline/massive (collophane)
  - CO₃-rich fluid: botryoidal (staffelite/francolite)
- **Decomposition products:** None under normal geological T. Dissolution at low pH releases Ca²⁺ + PO₄³⁻.

### Supergroup Connection (Why This Mineral Matters)
Apatite is the **structural parent** of the apatite supergroup, hexagonal channel structure Ca₅(XO₄)₃(Z) where:
- X = P (apatite), As (mimetite), V (vanadinite)
- Z = F, Cl, OH, or vacant

The three Pb-minerals already in the game (pyromorphite, mimetite, vanadinite) are Pb²⁺ analogues of apatite's Ca²⁺ structure. When Ca gets added to the simulator, apatite becomes the **bridge mineral** connecting the phosphate system to the primary/hypogene zone — forming first in the vein, then its oxidation products and Pb-replacements follow.

### Variants for Game
- **Variant 1: Fluorapatite** — F-dominant, most common, standard green/blue/yellow. Forms in igneous + hydrothermal. The "default" apatite.
- **Variant 2: Manganapatite** — Mn²⁺-bearing, blue-violet to violet. Rare and collectible. Requires Mn in fluid (already tracked). Prized specimen mineral.
- **Variant 3: Francolite (carbonate-apatite)** — CO₃²⁻ substitutes for PO₄³⁻. Forms in sedimentary/phosphorite environments at low T. Botryoidal habit. Brown to yellow.

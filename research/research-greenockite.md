# Research: Greenockite (CdS, hexagonal)

## Species: Greenockite

### Identity
- **Formula:** CdS
- **Crystal system:** Hexagonal
- **Mineral group:** Sulfide — wurtzite group
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 3.0–3.5
- **Specific gravity:** 4.8–4.9
- **Cleavage:** Distinct on {1122}, imperfect on {0001}
- **Fracture:** Conchoidal
- **Luster:** Adamantine to resinous

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Honey yellow, citron yellow, orange yellow, occasionally deep red to brown
- **Color causes:** Cd-S charge transfer in the native crystal; Zn substitution shifts toward paler yellow. Particle size affects perceived color — powdery masses appear brighter than crystals.
- **Transparency:** Nearly opaque to translucent (thin crystals)
- **Streak:** Yellow orange to brick red
- **Notable visual features:** Hemimorphic pyramidal crystals — different forms at each terminus. High refractive indices (nω = 2.529, nε = 2.506) give adamantine luster even at micro scale.

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Hemimorphic six-sided pyramidal crystals (hexagonal barrels)
- **Common forms/faces:** Pyramidal {1011}, {1122}; prismatic {1010}
- **Twin laws:** Rare on {1122}, forming trillings/fourlings
- **Varieties:** Zn-bearing greenockite (partial substitution, paler color, may be fluorescent)
- **Special morphologies:** Colloform encrustations, earthy powdery coatings, radial aggregates. Most common as yellow dusting on sphalerite surfaces.

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** 25–200°C (supergene/low-temperature hydrothermal)
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 50–150°C
- **Decomposition temperature:** ~980°C (melts); oxidizes to CdO + SO₂ above ~600°C in air
- **Temperature-dependent habits:** Higher T → more euhedral crystals; lower T → powdery/earthy coatings. The hexagonal (greenockite) vs cubic (hawleyite) dimorph boundary: greenockite is the high-T polymorph (>~200°C), hawleyite the low-T form, but both can form metastably at any temperature.

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:** Cd²⁺ (cadmium), S²⁻ (sulfide)
- **Optional/enhancing elements:** Zn²⁺ (substitutes for Cd, stabilizes at higher T via solid solution with wurtzite), Pb²⁺ (common in same environment)
- **Inhibiting elements:** Fe²⁺ (competes for S²⁻), excess O₂ (oxidizes CdS to CdO)
- **Required pH range:** Neutral to slightly acidic (5–7). CdS precipitates readily even at very low S²⁻ concentrations due to extremely low Ksp (~10⁻²⁸).
- **Required Eh range:** Reducing to mildly oxidizing (supergene). Forms during sphalerite oxidation — Cd released from sphalerite re-precipitates as CdS when it encounters residual sulfide.
- **Required O₂ range:** Low — supergene but still reducing enough to retain S as S²⁻

#### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **Does it release any chemicals when forming?** Consumes Cd²⁺ and S²⁻ from solution. If forming from sphalerite oxidation, Zn²⁺ is left in solution (goes to smithsonite/hemimorphite).
- **Byproducts of nucleation:** None directly. Consumes trace Cd.
- **Byproducts of dissolution/decomposition:** Cd²⁺ (toxic) + S or SO₄²⁻ depending on conditions. CdO if thermally decomposed in air.

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Fast when Cd available — Ksp so low that supersaturation is easy to achieve
- **Maximum crystal size:** Crystals to 3 cm known (extremely rare); typically sub-mm
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** 0.1–1 mm; commonly microscopic
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?** Not strongly — solubility is low at all temperatures
- **Competes with:** Sphalerite (for S²⁻), smithsonite (for Cd²⁺ if oxidizing), hawleyite (dimorph)

#### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** Yes, above ~600°C oxidizes to CdO + SO₂. Melts at ~980°C.
- **Breaks down in light?** Darkens with prolonged UV exposure (photoreactive CdS)
- **Dissolves in water?** Very slightly — Ksp ~10⁻²⁸, essentially insoluble
- **Dissolves in acid?** Dissolves in strong mineral acids (HCl, HNO₃). Dilute acids slow. Oxidizing acids (HNO₃) dissolve readily.
- **Oxidizes?** Yes — in oxidizing supergene conditions, CdS → Cd²⁺ (mobile, toxic) + SO₄²⁻. Otavite (CdCO₃) may form if CO₃²⁻ available.
- **Dehydrates?** No (anhydrous)
- **Radiation sensitivity:** CdS is a semiconductor; radiation induces color changes (darkening) via defect formation

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Sphalerite (Cd source — Cd substitutes for Zn in sphalerite up to ~1%), galena
- **Forms BEFORE:** Otavite (CdCO₃, further oxidation), cadmium oxides
- **Commonly associated minerals:** Sphalerite, galena, smithsonite, wurtzite, anglesite, cerussite, siderite, quartz
- **Zone:** Supergene/oxidation zone of Cd-bearing Zn-Pb deposits
- **Geological environment:** Oxidation zones of zinc-lead deposits, volcanic fumaroles (high-T), hydrothermal veins

### Famous Localities
- **Classic locality 1:** Tsumeb, Namibia — microcrystals to 1 mm on sphalerite, all three oxidation zones
- **Classic locality 2:** Příbram, Czech Republic — hydrothermal Cd-bearing veins
- **Classic locality 3:** Bishopton, Renfrewshire, Scotland (type locality — 1840, railway tunnel cutting)
- **Notable specimens:** Tsumeb specimens with yellow smithsonite (Cd-impregnated); columnar crystals to 1 mm from Tsumeb; crystals to 3 cm from a few exceptional occurrences

### Fluorescence
- **Fluorescent under UV?** Yes, when Zn-bearing
- **SW (255nm) color:** Yellow-orange (weak)
- **MW (310nm) color:** Yellow-orange
- **LW (365nm) color:** Bright yellow-orange to carmine-red (Zn-bearing material)
- **Phosphorescent?** Not typically
- **Activator:** Zn substitution (CdS:Zn is a known phosphor). Pure CdS is a semiconductor with bandgap ~2.42 eV (512 nm) — intrinsically photoactive.
- **Quenched by:** Fe, excess Cd vacancies

### Flavor Text

> Greenockite is a whisper of cadmium — the element that hides inside sphalerite like a secret, substituting for zinc atom by atom until the crystal lattice barely notices. When oxidation attacks the zinc ore body and liberates its prisoners, cadmium re-encounters sulfide and slams shut like a trap. The result is the yellowest dust you've ever seen, so impossibly insoluble that it precipitates from solutions barely containing cadmium at all. It was found in a Scottish railway tunnel in 1840 and named for a lord who owned the land. The crystals are hexagonal barrels, hemimorphic — different terminations at top and bottom, as if they couldn't decide which way was up. Most greenockite you'll never see: a yellow smear under the microscope, a pigment in someone's smithsonite. But when conditions align, those tiny mustard-yellow pyramids catch the light with an adamantine flash that makes you understand why cadmium yellow became the painter's most dangerous color.

### Simulator Implementation Notes
- **New parameters needed:** trace_Cd (cadmium in fluid, released from sphalerite oxidation)
- **New events needed:** Sphalerite oxidation releases Cd²⁺ (proportional to Cd content of sphalerite — typically 0.1–1% Cd)
- **Nucleation rule pseudocode:**
```
IF trace_Cd > 0.1 ppm AND S_total > 1 ppm AND T < 200°C AND Eh < 0.4V → nucleate greenockite
IF T > 200°C → prefer greenockite over hawleyite (hexagonal stable)
```
- **Growth rule pseudocode:**
```
σ = (Cd × S) / Ksp_greenockite  [Ksp ~10⁻²⁸]
IF σ > 1 → grow at rate proportional to log(σ)
Rate: fast (easy supersaturation)
Max crystals: small (micro — nucleation cheap, Cd supply limited)
```
- **Habit selection logic:**
  - T > 100°C, high Cd → euhedral hexagonal barrels
  - T < 100°C, low Cd → powdery/earthy coating on sphalerite
  - Zn-rich → paler color, possible fluorescence
- **Decomposition products:** Oxidation → Cd²⁺ (dissolved) + SO₄²⁻. If CO₃ available → otavite (CdCO₃).

### Variants for Game
- **Variant 1: Crystallized greenockite** — Hexagonal barrel crystals, honey yellow, adamantine luster. Requires higher Cd concentration + T > 100°C. Rare and prized.
- **Variant 2: Powdery greenockite** — Yellow earthy coating on sphalerite. Most common form. Low Cd, low T. Colors surrounding smithsonite yellow.
- **Variant 3: Zn-bearing greenockite** — Paler yellow, fluorescent yellow-orange under LW UV. Zn substitution >5%. The glowing variant — a treat for UV collectors.

### Dimorph Note
Greenockite (hexagonal CdS) and hawleyite (cubic CdS) are dimorphs. In the simulator, greenockite forms at higher temperatures (>200°C or hydrothermal), hawleyite at lower temperatures (supergene/meteoric). Both produce the same yellow coatings in practice — most field identifications are ambiguous without XRD. See `memory/research-hawleyite.md`.

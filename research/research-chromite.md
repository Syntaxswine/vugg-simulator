# Mineral Species: Chromite

## Identity
- **Formula:** FeCr₂O₄ (Fe²⁺Cr³⁺₂O₄)
- **Crystal system:** Isometric (cubic)
- **Mineral group:** Oxide, Spinel group
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 5.5
- **Specific gravity:** 4.5–4.8 (measured), 5.12 (calculated)
- **Cleavage:** None; parting may develop along {111}
- **Fracture:** Uneven, hackly, sub-conchoidal
- **Luster:** Resinous, greasy, sub-metallic, metallic, dull

## Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Black to brownish black; brown on thin edges in transmitted light
- **Color causes:** Fe²⁺ and Cr³⁺ are intrinsic chromophores — both present in the crystal structure
- **Transparency:** Translucent to opaque (translucent only on thin edges)
- **Streak:** Brown (diagnostic — distinguishes from magnetite which has black streak)
- **Notable visual features:** Very high relief in thin section (n=2.08–2.16), isotropic under cross-polarized light. Octahedral crystals when euhedral. Green borax bead test is diagnostic for Cr.

## Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Massive to granular (overwhelmingly common)
- **Common forms/faces:** Octahedral {111} when crystallized
- **Twin laws:** Spinel law on {111}
- **Varieties:**
  - Magnesiochromite (MgCr₂O₄) — Mg-rich end member, forms complete solid solution
  - Hercynite (FeAl₂O₄) — Al-substituted end member
  - Chromitite — rock composed predominantly of chromite (the ore)
- **Special morphologies:** Disseminated grains in peridotite, layered cumulate deposits, lens-shaped ore bodies, placer concentrations (detrital grains resist weathering). Occasionally as inclusions in diamond.

## Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** >1000°C (magmatic — one of the first minerals to crystallize from cooling mafic/ultramafic magma)
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 1200–1400°C (liquidus of chromite in basaltic melts)
- **Decomposition temperature:** Extremely stable — survives greenschist through granulite facies metamorphism unchanged. Only breaks down at extreme P-T (xieite polymorph forms at 18–23 GPa, 1800–1950°C — meteorite impact conditions)
- **Temperature-dependent habits:** Higher Cr/Fe ratio at higher temperatures; Mg substitution increases with temperature along the magnesiochromite join

### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:** Fe (high), Cr (high) — both must be present simultaneously
- **Optional/enhancing elements:** Mg (substitutes for Fe²⁺ → magnesiochromite series), Al (substitutes for Cr³⁺ → hercynite series), Ti (trace impurity), Mn, Zn (trace)
- **Inhibiting elements:** High SiO₂ activity drives melt toward silicate phases instead; chromite competes with olivine and pyroxene for Fe and Mg
- **Required pH range:** Not pH-controlled in the aqueous sense — this is a magmatic oxide. In hydrothermal conditions, requires highly alkaline (pH >10) to keep Cr³⁺ in solution as Cr(OH)₄⁻
- **Required Eh range:** Moderate reducing conditions (Fe²⁺ stable, Cr³⁺ stable — both need to NOT be oxidized further)
- **Required O₂ range:** Low to moderate oxygen fugacity. Too oxidizing → Fe²⁺ oxidizes to Fe³⁺, forming magnetite (Fe₃O₄) instead. Critical balance: enough O₂ for Cr to be Cr³⁺ (not Cr⁰ metal), but not so much that Fe²⁺ → Fe³⁺

### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **Byproducts of nucleation:** Removes Fe²⁺ and Cr³⁺ from melt, driving residual melt toward more silicic composition
- **Byproducts of dissolution/decomposition:** Extremely resistant — does not dissolve under normal conditions. In extreme weathering, Cr may slowly mobilize as Cr(VI) chromate under strongly oxidizing conditions (environmental hazard)

### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Moderate (cumulus mineral — grows as crystals settle through magma)
- **Maximum crystal size:** Up to ~3 cm in rare cases (meteorite bodies with stable supersaturation); typically mm-scale grains
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** Unlikely to form in vugs at all — this is a magmatic mineral. If it appeared, it would be as tiny (mm) octahedral grains
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?** Yes — chromite liquidus temperature is very high, so it crystallizes early and continues as long as the melt is above its solidus
- **Competes with:** Magnetite (same spinel structure, Fe³⁺ instead of Cr³⁺), olivine (competes for Fe²⁺ and Mg²⁺), pyroxene

### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** No — one of the most thermally stable oxide minerals. Survives metamorphism unchanged
- **Breaks down in light?** No
- **Dissolves in water?** Essentially insoluble
- **Dissolves in acid?** Very resistant. Slowly in hot concentrated H₂SO₄ or fusion with Na₂CO₃
- **Oxidizes?** Under extreme oxidizing conditions, Cr³⁺ → Cr⁶⁺ (chromate) — this is slow and represents an environmental contamination pathway rather than a mineralogical transformation
- **Dehydrates?** No (anhydrous)
- **Radiation sensitivity:** Very resistant due to close-packed spinel structure

## Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Nothing in the traditional sense — chromite is one of the FIRST minerals to crystallize from a mafic/ultramafic melt
- **Forms BEFORE:** Olivine, pyroxene, plagioclase, serpentine (after alteration of host rock)
- **Commonly associated minerals:** Olivine, enstatite, plagioclase, serpentine, magnetite, ilmenite, pyrrhotite, pentlandite, ulvospinel
- **Zone:** Primary/hypogene (magmatic cumulate). Also detrital (placer deposits from weathering of ultramafic rocks). NOT a hydrothermal, supergene, or vug mineral.
- **Geological environment:** Layered mafic-ultramafic intrusions (Bushveld, Stillwater), ophiolite complexes, alpine-type peridotites, kimberlites (as diamond inclusion), iron meteorites

## Famous Localities
- **Bushveld Complex, South Africa** — world's largest chromite deposits, layered cumulate
- **Great Dyke, Zimbabwe** — major layered intrusion
- **Saranay, Ural Mountains, Russia** — classic locality
- **Kempirsai Massif, Kazakhstan** — significant deposits
- **Stillwater Complex, Montana, USA** — layered intrusion with chromitite seams
- **Tiebaghi, New Caledonia** — ophiolite-hosted
- **Hangha, Sierra Leone** — large octahedral crystals from Freetown Layered Complex
- **Moa district, Cuba** — lateritic chromite deposits

## Fluorescence
- **Fluorescent under UV?** Generally no. Pure chromite is non-fluorescent.
- **SW (255nm) color:** None
- **MW (310nm) color:** None
- **LW (365nm) color:** None
- **Phosphorescent?** No
- **Activator:** None — Cr³⁺ in octahedral coordination is a strong absorber, not an emitter in the typical UV-excited fluorescence sense. (Note: Cr³⁺ in OTHER spinel-group minerals like gem spinel MgAl₂O₄ can produce red fluorescence at ~706nm, but chromite is too Fe-rich and opaque.)
- **Quenched by:** Fe²⁺ (present in abundance) is a universal fluorescence quencher

## Flavor Text

> Chromite is a paradox: one of the first minerals to crystallize from molten rock, yet among the last to yield to weathering. Black, opaque, stubborn — it settles through cooling magma like iron rain, accumulating in layers that stretch for hundreds of kilometers. Those layered chromitite seams in the Bushveld Complex hold most of the world's chromium, the element that makes stainless steel stainless. Rare octahedral crystals form when conditions are just right, but chromite prefers the anonymous life: massive, granular, a millimeter at a time. In a vug, it would be almost impossibly out of place — this is a mineral of the deep mantle and the molten core, not of hydrothermal veins and crystal-lined cavities. But that's what makes it interesting. If chromite appears in your vug, something very unusual happened here.

## Simulator Implementation Notes
- **New parameters needed:** `trace_Cr` (Cr is in the 30-element list), oxygen fugacity tracking (fO₂)
- **New events needed:** Mafic pulse event (introduces Cr + Fe in magmatic proportions), oxygen fugacity shift event
- **Nucleation rule pseudocode:**
```
IF trace_Fe > threshold AND trace_Cr > threshold AND temperature > 900°C AND fO2 < magnetite_buffer:
  → nucleate chromite (probability proportional to Cr concentration)
```
- **Growth rule pseudocode:**
```
IF temperature > 800°C AND fO2 < magnetite_buffer:
  → grow at rate proportional to min(trace_Fe, trace_Cr * 0.5) * temperature_factor
  → Mg substitution: if trace_Mg available, some Fe sites → Mg (magnesiochromite trend)
```
- **Habit selection logic:**
  - Default: massive/granular (most common)
  - Slow cooling + low nucleation density → octahedral crystals (rare)
  - Spinel-law twins on {111} at low probability (~5-10%)
- **Decomposition products:** None under normal conditions. At extreme P-T → xieite (orthorhombic polymorph). At surface weathering over geologic time → Cr may mobilize as chromate.

### Solid Solution System
Chromite sits at a ternary junction:
- FeCr₂O₄ (chromite) ↔ MgCr₂O₄ (magnesiochromite) — Fe↔Mg substitution
- FeCr₂O₄ (chromite) ↔ FeAl₂O₄ (hercynite) — Cr↔Al substitution
- FeCr₂O₄ (chromite) ↔ Fe₃O₄ (magnetite) — Cr↔Fe³⁺ substitution

Game could track Mg and Al substitution as separate variants.

## Variants for Game
- **Variant 1: Chromite** (FeCr₂O₄) — standard, black, massive to rare octahedral. Requires Fe + Cr, high temperature, moderate fO₂.
- **Variant 2: Magnesiochromite** (MgCr₂O₄) — Mg-rich end member, slightly lighter color. Requires Mg-rich broth with Cr.
- **Variant 3: Aluminous chromite** — Al-bearing chromite with hercynite component. Transitional toward spinel proper. Requires some Al availability.

## Simulator Design Note

Chromite is a **magmatic** mineral — it forms from cooling silicate melts, not from hydrothermal fluids filling vugs. Including it in the Vugg Simulator means either:
1. A special high-temperature magmatic scenario (e.g., vug forms in a cooling mafic intrusion), or
2. It appears as a relict/detrital grain — an immigrant mineral that didn't grow in the vug but was already there when the vug opened.

Option 2 is geologically honest and could be an interesting game mechanic: "inherited" minerals from the host rock that don't participate in vug crystallization but take up space and provide nucleation surfaces.

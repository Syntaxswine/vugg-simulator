# Mineral Species — Adamite

## Species: Adamite

### Identity
- **Formula:** Zn₂(AsO₄)(OH)
- **Crystal system:** Orthorhombic (space group Pnnm)
- **Mineral group:** Arsenate
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 3.5
- **Specific gravity:** 4.32–4.48
- **Cleavage:** {101} good; {010} poor
- **Fracture:** Uneven to subconchoidal
- **Luster:** Vitreous

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Pale yellow, honey-yellow, brownish yellow, bright lime-green; rarely white, colorless, blue, pale green. May be zoned.
- **Color causes:** Pure adamite is colorless. Yellow from Fe³⁺ substitution. Green from Cu²⁺ substitution (→ cuproadamite series). Mn, Co, Ni can also tint.
- **Transparency:** Transparent to translucent
- **Streak:** White to pale green
- **Notable visual features:** Radiating clusters of wedge-shaped prisms are iconic. Botryoidal masses common. Strong fluorescence is diagnostic.

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Prismatic to elongated wedge-shaped crystals, often in fan-shaped or radiating clusters and drusy crusts
- **Common forms/faces:** Prismatic with terminated ends, wedge-like cross-sections
- **Twin laws:** Not commonly twinned
- **Varieties:**
  - **Cuproadamite** — Cu-bearing intermediate between adamite and olivenite
  - **Zincolivenite** — CuZn(AsO₄)(OH), IMA-recognized intermediate mineral (isostructural series)
  - **Cobaltadamite** — pink/violet from Co substitution
  - **Manganadamite** — Mn-bearing
  - **Nickeladamite** — Ni-bearing
- **Special morphologies:** Botryoidal crusts, radiating sprays, needle-like (acicular) crystals (Tsumeb 44 Level "Zinc Pocket" produced white needle-like crystals)

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** <50°C (superficial oxidation zone, ambient to mild hydrothermal)
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 15–40°C (near-surface weathering conditions)
- **Decomposition temperature:** ~500°C (loses structural OH, breaks down)
- **Temperature-dependent habits:** Lower T → fine acicular sprays; higher T within range → blockier prisms

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:** Zn (≥50 ppm), As (≥20 ppm), O₂ (oxidizing — arsenic must be As⁵⁺ not As³⁻)
- **Optional/enhancing elements:** Cu (→ cuproadamite/green color), Co (→ pink), Mn (→ brownish), Ni (→ greenish)
- **Inhibiting elements:** Fe²⁺ (suppresses by competing for As), high sulfide (reduces As to arsenide, removes from solution)
- **Required pH range:** 5–8 (near-neutral; too acidic dissolves it, too alkaline precipitates smithsonite instead)
- **Required Eh range:** Oxidizing (Eh > +0.3V). Arsenic must be oxidized from As³⁻ to As⁵⁺ (arsenate). Zn must be mobilized from sulfide breakdown.
- **Required O₂ range:** High — this is a supergene oxidation zone mineral

#### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **Does it release any chemicals when forming?** Consumes Zn²⁺ and HAsO₄²⁻ from solution. Releases H⁺ during precipitation.
- **Byproducts of nucleation:** Minor H⁺
- **Byproducts of dissolution/decomposition:** Releases Zn²⁺ and arsenate back to solution (environmental concern — arsenate mobility)

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Moderate — faster than wulfenite, slower than chalcanthite
- **Maximum crystal size:** To ~10 cm botryoidal masses; individual prismatic crystals to ~3 cm
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** 1–15 mm for isolated crystals; crusts can coat cm-scale areas
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?** Slightly — warmer increases Zn mobility but also increases competing smithsonite precipitation
- **Competes with:** Smithsonite (ZnCO₃, needs CO₃), hemimorphite (Zn₄Si₂O₇(OH)₂·H₂O, needs Si), scorodite (FeAsO₄·2H₂O, competes for As), olivenite (Cu₂(AsO₄)(OH), competes for As when Cu present)

#### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** ~500°C, dehydrates and decomposes
- **Breaks down in light?** No — stable
- **Dissolves in water?** Slightly soluble; more soluble in acidic water
- **Dissolves in acid?** Yes — dissolves readily in HCl and HNO₃
- **Oxidizes?** Already fully oxidized — stable in oxidizing conditions. May alter in reducing conditions (reversal to sulfides)
- **Dehydrates?** No (no structural water, only OH)
- **Radiation sensitivity:** Not significant

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Sphalerite (ZnS, primary source of Zn), arsenopyrite (FeAsS, primary source of As), and other primary sulfides oxidize to release Zn²⁺ and As⁵⁺
- **Forms BEFORE:** Can be overgrown by later calcite, quartz, or limonite
- **Commonly associated minerals:** Smithsonite, hemimorphite, scorodite, olivenite, calcite, quartz, limonite/goethite, legrandite, leiteite, paradamite (triclinic dimorph)
- **Zone:** Supergene/oxidation zone — always secondary
- **Geological environment:** Oxidized zones of zinc-arsenic hydrothermal deposits

### Famous Localities
- **Classic locality 1:** Ojuela Mine, Mapimí, Durango, Mexico — TYPE LOCALITY (1866). World's best adamite: bright lime-green radiating clusters on limonite matrix. Also produces legrandite, paradamite, leiteite, köttigite.
- **Classic locality 2:** Tsumeb, Namibia — Third oxidation zone "Zinc Pocket" on 44 Level produced white acicular adamite with leiteite and legrandite. Also cuproadamite.
- **Classic locality 3:** Gold Hill District, Tooele County, Utah, USA — yellow-green adamite on limonite
- **Notable specimens:** Ojuela mine produces the finest display specimens — radiating sprays to 5+ cm across. Laurion, Greece for small but colorful specimens.

### Fluorescence
- **Fluorescent under UV?** Yes — strongly fluorescent
- **SW (255nm) color:** Bright lemon-yellow to green-yellow
- **MW (310nm) color:** Yellow-green (weaker)
- **LW (365nm) color:** Lemon-yellow to green-yellow
- **Phosphorescent?** Yes — weak phosphorescence after SW
- **Activator:** UO₂²⁻ (uranyl ion) trace impurity is the primary activator; possibly also Mn²⁺ in some specimens
- **Quenched by:** Fe²⁺ (iron-rich specimens may not fluoresce)

### Flavor Text

> Adamite is what happens after the sulfide drama ends. When sphalerite meets oxygen and groundwater, zinc goes into solution; when arsenopyrite does the same, arsenate follows. They find each other in the quiet oxidized zone, where nothing exciting happens anymore — and crystallize into wedge-shaped fans that glow lemon-yellow under ultraviolet light. Pure adamite is colorless. The green you see is copper eavesdropping on zinc's business. The pink is cobalt. The mineral records who was in the room. Named for Gilbert-Joseph Adam, a French mineralogist who never saw it fluoresce — UV lamps came decades later. The Ojuela Mine in Mapimí has been producing the world's best specimens since 1866, glowing clusters on rust-brown limonite, like moss made of arsenic.

### Simulator Implementation Notes
- **New parameters needed:** None new — uses existing Zn, As, O₂, pH, Eh. trace_Cu for cuproadamite variant.
- **New events needed:** Could add "arsenic oxidation event" — when primary arsenopyrite/sulfides oxidize, releases As⁵⁺ into solution
- **Nucleation rule pseudocode:**
```
IF temp < 50 AND Eh > 0.3 AND pH 5-8 AND trace_Zn > 50 AND trace_As > 20 AND O₂ high
  → nucleate adamite
```
- **Growth rule pseudocode:**
```
IF σ_adamite > 1.0 → grow at rate 4 (moderate)
  Cu present → cuproadamite variant (green)
  Co present → cobaltadamite variant (pink)
```
- **Habit selection logic:**
  - High Zn, low space → botryoidal crusts
  - Moderate Zn, open vug → radiating fan clusters (default)
  - Very high Zn, cold → acicular needles
- **Decomposition products:** Zn²⁺ + HAsO₄²⁻ back to solution

### Variants for Game
- **Variant 1: Cuproadamite** — Cu substitutes for Zn, green color. Requires both Zn and Cu in solution. Transitions toward olivenite end-member.
- **Variant 2: Cobaltadamite** — Co-bearing, pink to violet. Rare but visually striking. Requires trace_Co.
- **Variant 3: Botryoidal adamite** — Massive rounded forms instead of crystals. Forms when space is limited or growth is rapid. No change in chemistry, just habit.

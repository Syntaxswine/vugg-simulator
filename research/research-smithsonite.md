# Smithsonite (ZnCO₃) — Vugg Simulator Research

## Species: Smithsonite

### Identity
- **Formula:** ZnCO₃
- **Crystal system:** Trigonal
- **Mineral group:** Carbonate (calcite group)
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 4.5
- **Specific gravity:** 4.4–4.5 (unusually heavy for a carbonate — high Zn atomic mass)
- **Cleavage:** Perfect on {1011} (rhombohedral, 3 directions at ~75°)
- **Fracture:** Uneven, sub-conchoidal
- **Luster:** Vitreous, may be pearly on cleavage surfaces

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** White, grey, yellow, green, apple-green, blue, pink, purple, bluish grey, brown
- **Color causes:**
  - Blue/cyan: Cu²⁺ substitution (cuprian smithsonite)
  - Pink: Co²⁺ substitution (cobaltoan smithsonite)
  - Yellow: greenockite (CdS) inclusions ("turkey fat ore")
  - Green: possible Cu²⁺ or minor Fe²⁺
  - Purple/lavender: not well characterized, possibly trace Cu in specific coordination
- **Transparency:** Translucent
- **Streak:** White
- **Notable visual features:** Botryoidal surfaces with silky-to-vitreous luster; extremely attractive in colored varieties. Birefringence is extreme (δ = 0.225) — shows strong doubling in transparent crystals.

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Botryoidal, reniform, spherulitic — rarely forms distinct crystals
- **Common forms/faces:** When crystallized: rhombohedra {1011}, scalenohedra; typically tiny, drusy coatings
- **Twin laws:** None observed
- **Varieties:**
  - Cuprian smithsonite (blue, blue-green) — Cu replaces Zn
  - Cobaltoan smithsonite (pink) — Co replaces Zn
  - "Turkey fat ore" — yellow from greenockite inclusions
  - Bonamite — massive, light green, used as ornamental stone
- **Special morphologies:** Stalactitic, earthy, compact massive, drusy crusts. Under magnification, botryoidal surfaces resolve into countless tiny crystal terminations.

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** Near-surface temperatures, ~10–50°C (supergene/weathering zone)
- **Optimal growth temperature:** Ambient to mildly warm, ~15–40°C
- **Decomposition temperature:** ~300°C (decomposes to ZnO + CO₂)
- **Temperature-dependent habits:** None significant — always low-T supergene

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:** Zn²⁺ (primary), CO₃²⁻ (from dissolved CO₂ or carbonate host rock)
- **Optional/enhancing elements:**
  - Cu²⁺ → blue coloration (cuprian variety)
  - Co²⁺ → pink coloration (cobaltoan variety)
  - Cd → yellow via greenockite inclusions
  - Fe²⁺ → minor substitution toward siderite
  - Mn²⁺ → substitution toward rhodochrosite
- **Inhibiting elements:** High Fe³⁺ can compete for CO₃²⁻ (forming siderite instead)
- **Required pH range:** Neutral to mildly alkaline (7.0–8.5); acidic conditions dissolve carbonate
- **Required Eh range:** Oxidizing (supergene zone — sphalerite has already oxidized to release Zn²⁺)
- **Required O₂ range:** High — this is a weathering product, needs oxygenated meteoric water

#### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **Byproducts of nucleation:** None significant — Zn²⁺ + CO₃²⁻ → ZnCO₃ is clean
- **Byproducts of dissolution:** Zn²⁺ + HCO₃⁻ released back to fluid; consumes H⁺ (buffering)

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Moderate — faster than quartz, slower than calcite in same conditions
- **Maximum crystal size:** Crystals rarely >2 cm; botryoidal masses can be tens of cm
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** Drusy coatings (<1 mm crystals) to botryoidal crusts (1–5 cm hemispheres)
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?** Slightly faster at warmer temperatures, but this is a low-T mineral by nature
- **Competes with:** Hemimorphite (Zn₄Si₂O₇(OH)₂·H₂O — needs Si), hydrozincite (Zn₅(CO₃)₂(OH)₆ — forms at higher pH), aurichalcite ((Zn,Cu)₅(CO₃)₂(OH)₆ — needs Cu)

### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** ~300°C → ZnO (zincite) + CO₂
- **Breaks down in light?** No
- **Dissolves in water?** Low solubility in pure water (~0.02 g/L); dissolves readily in acids with effervescence
- **Dissolves in acid?** Yes — all carbonates fizz in HCl. Smithsonite dissolves vigorously in dilute acid.
- **Oxidizes?** Already fully oxidized (Zn²⁺ is the stable oxidation state). No further oxidation.
- **Dehydrates?** No — anhydrous carbonate
- **Radiation sensitivity:** Not reported

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Sphalerite (ZnS) oxidizes in the weathering zone, releasing Zn²⁺ into groundwater. Galena, pyrite, and other primary sulfides must be present to create the acidic conditions that mobilize Zn.
- **Forms BEFORE:** Late-stage supergene minerals; may be partially replaced by hemimorphite if Si is available
- **Commonly associated minerals:** Hemimorphite, willemite, hydrozincite, cerussite, malachite, azurite, aurichalcite, anglesite, limonite/goethite
- **Zone:** Supergene/oxidation zone — near-surface weathering of zinc deposits
- **Geological environment:** Oxidized portions of hydrothermal vein deposits, Mississippi Valley-type deposits, zinc-bearing carbonate rock replacements

### Famous Localities
- **Kelly Mine, Magdalena, New Mexico, USA** — legendary blue smithsonite, some of the finest ever found
- **Tsumeb, Namibia** — produced stunning cuprian (blue-green) and cobaltoan (pink) smithsonite crystals
- **Lavrion, Greece** — classic locality, botryoidal masses
- **Mapimí (Ojuela Mine), Durango, Mexico** — colorful botryoidal specimens
- **Broken Hill, New South Wales, Australia** — large crystalline masses
- **Notable specimens:** Kelly Mine blue smithsonite is considered the gold standard for the species; Tsumeb produced rare well-formed rhombohedral crystals up to 2 cm

### Fluorescence
- **Fluorescent under UV?** Variable — some specimens yes, most not strongly
- **SW (255nm) color:** Pale green or pale blue (weak)
- **MW (310nm) color:** Not well documented
- **LW (365nm) color:** Pale green, pale yellow, or grey (weak)
- **Phosphorescent?** Not typically
- **Activator:** Not well characterized; possibly trace U or rare earth elements in some specimens
- **Quenched by:** Fe²⁺, excess Cu

### Flavor Text
> Smithsonite is what happens when sphalerite meets rain and time. The zinc sulfide, stable in the dark reduction of the deep, can't survive at the surface — oxygen tears it apart, liberating zinc into groundwater that's already charged with carbonate from dissolved limestone. The result is a slow, patient precipitation: layer after microscopic layer of zinc carbonate building botryoidal masses that look like frozen bubbles. The colors are ghosts of the broth — copper bleeds it blue, cobalt turns it pink, and cadmium inclusions paint it the unmistakable yellow of "turkey fat ore." Named for James Smithson, whose bequest founded the Smithsonian Institution — a man who, like the mineral that bears his name, turned out to be more interesting after weathering exposed what was inside.

  - Color variant: IF Cu > threshold → blue; IF Co > threshold → pink; IF Cd present → yellow (greenockite inclusion flag)

### Variants for Game
- **Variant 1: Cuprian Smithsonite (blue)** — Cu²⁺ replaces some Zn. Kelly Mine type. Needs trace Cu in broth.
- **Variant 2: Cobaltoan Smithsonite (pink)** — Co²⁺ replaces some Zn. Tsumeb type. Needs trace Co in broth.
- **Variant 3: Turkey Fat Ore (yellow)** — Greenockite (CdS) micro-inclusions. Needs trace Cd. Rare and distinctive.

### Solid Solution Notes
Smithsonite forms limited solid solution series with:
- **Rhodochrosite (MnCO₃):** Mn²⁺ ↔ Zn²⁺ substitution
- **Siderite (FeCO₃):** Fe²⁺ ↔ Zn²⁺ substitution
- Both series are limited — complete solid solution does not occur at low temperatures

### History
Named in 1832 by François Sulpice Beudant for James Smithson (c. 1765–1829), the English scientist who first identified the mineral in 1802. Smithson's bequest founded the Smithsonian Institution. Previously confused with hemimorphite — both were called "calamine." The two were distinguished as separate minerals in the early 19th century.

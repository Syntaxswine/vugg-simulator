# Mineral Species Template — Vugg Simulator

## Species: Gypsum (Anhydrite paragenetic sequence)

### Identity
- **Formula:** CaSO₄·2H₂O
- **Crystal system:** Monoclinic
- **Mineral group:** Sulfate
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 1.5–2 (very soft — can be scratched by fingernail)
- **Specific gravity:** 2.31–2.33
- **Cleavage:** Perfect on {010}, distinct on {100} and {011} — yields thin, flexible but inelastic plates
- **Fracture:** Splintery, conchoidal on thin plates
- **Luster:** Vitreous to pearly on cleavage surfaces, silky on fibrous varieties, subvitreous on crystal faces

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Colorless, white, gray, yellowish, reddish, brownish
- **Color causes:** Iron oxides (yellow/brown/reddish), clay inclusions (gray), organic matter (brown/black), trace Mn (pink)
- **Transparency:** Transparent to translucent (selenite), opaque in massive/alabaster
- **Streak:** White
- **Notable visual features:** Hourglass growth zonations visible in some transparent crystals (especially under UV); perfect cleavage yielding mica-like sheets; desert rose rosettes with sand inclusions

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Tabular to prismatic crystals with prominent {010} pinacoid
- **Common forms/faces:** {010} (dominant), {110}, {111}, {011}, {100}
- **Twin laws:** 
  - Swallowtail twin on {100} — most common, produces arrowhead/spearhead shapes
  - Paris twin on {101} — less common
  - Baveno-like twin on {001} — rare
  - "Fishtail" twins — common in prismatic crystals
- **Varieties:**
  - **Selenite** — transparent, colorless, tabular crystals
  - **Satin spar** — fibrous, silky luster, parallel aggregates
  - **Alabaster** — fine-grained, massive, translucent white
  - **Desert rose** — rosette-like aggregates with sand inclusions
  - **Gypsum flower** — radiating fibrous clusters, often curved ("ram's horn")
- **Special morphologies:** Thin flexible but inelastic cleavage sheets; needle-like prismatic; massive granular; stalactitic; encrusting

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** 0–60°C (surface to shallow burial)
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 20–40°C (evaporitic conditions)
- **Decomposition/dehydration temperature:** 
  - → Bassanite (CaSO₄·½H₂O / plaster of Paris) at ~80–120°C
  - → Anhydrite (CaSO₄) at ~120–200°C (or slowly at lower T in brines)
  - Reverse: anhydrite + H₂O → gypsum at <~60°C
- **Temperature-dependent habits:** 
  - Lower temps → larger, clearer selenite crystals
  - Rapid evaporation → fibrous satin spar / microcrystalline
  - Desert roses form at surface with sand entrainment

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:** Ca²⁺ (>100 ppm), SO₄²⁻ (>100 ppm)
- **Optional/enhancing elements:** 
  - NaCl (high salinity promotes evaporitic concentration)
  - Sr²⁺ (can substitute for Ca — strontian gypsum)
  - Ba²⁺ (minor substitution)
  - Organic molecules (can influence crystal habit — promote {010} growth)
- **Inhibiting elements:** 
  - High Mg/Ca ratio (promotes epsomite instead)
  - Citrate/phosphate (inhibits nucleation)
- **Required pH range:** 5.5–9 (broadly tolerant; prefers neutral to slightly alkaline)
- **Required Eh range:** Does not depend on redox — sulfate is already oxidized
- **Required O₂ range:** Not relevant — forms from sulfate regardless of O₂

#### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **Does it release any chemicals when forming?** No — crystallization from solution is straightforward
- **Byproducts of nucleation:** None (simply concentrates Ca²⁺ + SO₄²⁻ from solution)
- **Byproducts of dissolution:** Releases Ca²⁺ and SO₄²⁻ — both benign

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Fast (much faster than quartz — can grow cm/day in lab conditions)
- **Maximum crystal size:** Up to several meters (Naica Mine selenite crystals up to 12m!)
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** 1–50 cm (selenite blades), 1–10 cm (desert roses)
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?** Yes — warmer = faster growth up to dehydration threshold. Also retrograde solubility component: gypsum solubility slightly decreases above ~40°C
- **Competes with:** Anhydrite (high T), barite (if Ba present), celestine (if Sr present), calcite (if CO₃ present)

#### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** Yes — → bassanite at ~80–120°C → anhydrite at >120°C
- **Breaks down in light?** No
- **Dissolves in water?** Yes — moderately soluble (~2.0–2.5 g/L at 25°C), more soluble than quartz
- **Dissolves in acid?** Slowly in dilute HCl; completely in concentrated acids
- **Oxidizes?** No — sulfate is already fully oxidized
- **Dehydrates?** Yes — this is the key transformation: gypsum ↔ bassanite ↔ anhydrite, controlled by temperature and humidity
- **Radiation sensitivity:** Can darken slightly from radiation damage to water molecules; some specimens show hourglass zonations from self-irradiation of trace U

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Anhydrite hydrates to gypsum during uplift/weathering; barite and celestine may precipitate first from same brine
- **Forms BEFORE:** Sulfur (if sulfates are reduced by bacteria), calcite (if carbonates later precipitate)
- **Commonly associated minerals:** Anhydrite, halite, sylvite, carnallite, celestine, barite, calcite, aragonite, sulfur
- **Zone:** Evaporite, sedimentary, supergene (oxidation zone of sulfide deposits where sulfate is abundant), volcanic fumaroles
- **Geological environment:** 
  - Evaporite basins (marine and lacustrine)
  - Oxidation zones of sulfide deposits (where sulfuric acid meets carbonate rocks)
  - Cave systems (speleothems)
  - Hydrothermal veins (low-temperature)
  - Volcanic fumaroles
  - Shale/mudstone joints (selenite blades)

### Famous Localities
- **Naica Mine, Chihuahua, Mexico:** The Cave of Crystals — selenite crystals up to 12m long, grown in 58°C geothermal waters over ~500,000 years
- **White Sands National Park, New Mexico, USA:** Massive gypsum dune field — world's largest
- **Pulacayo, Bolivia:** Classic selenite crystals
- **Leicester, England:** Alabaster used in medieval carving
- **Segóvia, Spain:** Transparent selenite "espejo de los moros" (Moors' mirrors)
- **Notable specimens:** Naica crystals are the largest crystals of any mineral ever found in nature

### Fluorescence
- **Fluorescent under UV?** Yes — variable
- **SW (255nm) color:** Blue-white to greenish-white (rarely bright green if uranyl-activated)
- **MW (310nm) color:** Blue-white
- **LW (365nm) color:** Blue-white to greenish-white; some show orange/red fluorescence
- **Phosphorescent?** Yes — brief blue-white phosphorescence common
- **Activator:** 
  - Uranyl (UO₂²⁺) — green fluorescence
  - Mn²⁺ — orange/pink (rare)
  - Rare earth elements — various colors
  - Organic inclusions — blue-white (may be humic acid related)
- **Quenched by:** Fe²⁺, high clay content
- **Special note:** Hourglass zonation patterns visible under UV that are invisible in daylight — caused by growth-sector-dependent trace element incorporation

### Simulator Implementation Notes
- **New parameters needed:** 
  - Ca²⁺ concentration in broth (if not already tracked)
  - SO₄²⁻ concentration (may be derived from S + O₂)
  - Humidity/water activity (for hydration/dehydration cycling)
- **New events needed:** 
  - Evaporite concentration event (brine supersaturation)
  - Anhydrite → gypsum hydration (during cooling/uplift)
  - Gypsum → bassanite dehydration (heating event)
- **Nucleation rule pseudocode:**
```
IF Ca > 100ppm AND SO4 > 100ppm AND temp < 60°C AND solution_supersaturated:
  nucleation_chance = f(evaporation_rate, salinity)
  IF salinity > threshold: nucleate gypsum (evaporite habit)
  IF temp < 20°C: nucleate selenite (clear, tabular)
```
- **Growth rule pseudocode:**
```
IF gypsum_exists AND Ca_available AND SO4_available AND temp < 80°C:
  growth_rate = FAST * f(temperature, supersaturation)
  IF sand_present AND surface_exposure: grow desert_rose
  IF confined_space AND fibrous: grow satin_spar
  IF clear_solution AND slow_growth: grow selenite_blade
```
- **Habit selection logic:**
  - High supersaturation + rapid evaporation → microcrystalline/alabaster
  - Moderate supersaturation + space → tabular selenite
  - Confined + directional flow → fibrous satin spar
  - Sand present + surface → desert rose
  - Fracture/joint filling → thin blades
  - Twin probability: ~15% for swallowtail twins
- **Decomposition products:**
  - Heat 80–120°C → bassanite (CaSO₄·½H₂O)
  - Heat >120°C → anhydrite (CaSO₄)
  - Dissolution → Ca²⁺ + SO₄²⁻ in solution

### Anhydrite (CaSO₄) — Linked Paragenetic Sequence

Gypsum and anhydrite form a hydration/dehydration pair critical to evaporite geochemistry.

- **Formula:** CaSO₄ (anhydrous)
- **Crystal system:** Orthorhombic
- **Hardness:** 3–3.5
- **Specific gravity:** 2.9–3.0
- **Cleavage:** Perfect on {010}, good on {100}, {001}
- **Luster:** Vitreous to pearly
- **Color:** Colorless, white, bluish, gray, reddish
- **Habit:** Tabular to prismatic crystals; fibrous; massive; cubic-looking pseudocubic crystals
- **Formation:** Precipitates directly from brine at >~60°C or high salinity; also forms by dehydration of gypsum
- **Key relationship:** Below ~60°C and normal salinity → gypsum stable. Above ~60°C or very high salinity → anhydrite stable. The transition is the most important temperature indicator in evaporite geology.
- **Fluorescence:** White, yellow, or cream under SW; sometimes bright blue-white
- **Simulator rule:** 
```
IF Ca + SO4 AND temp > 60°C: nucleate anhydrite
IF anhydrite AND temp < 60°C AND H2O_available: convert to gypsum (slow)
IF gypsum AND temp > 120°C: convert to anhydrite (moderate rate)
```

### Variants for Game
- **Variant 1: Selenite** — Transparent tabular blades, clear as glass. Conditions: slow growth, low T (10–30°C), clean solution, space available. Most visually striking form.
- **Variant 2: Desert Rose** — Rosette aggregates with sand inclusions. Conditions: surface/near-surface, sand available, arid climate, rapid growth. Iconic evaporite morphology.
- **Variant 3: Satin Spar** — Fibrous, silky parallel aggregates. Conditions: confined space, directional fluid flow, rapid growth. Shows chatoyant luster.
- **Variant 4: Alabaster** — Fine-grained massive. Conditions: rapid precipitation, high supersaturation. Used for carving since antiquity.
- **Variant 5: Swallowtail Twin** — Arrowhead-shaped penetration twin. Conditions: 15% twin probability on nucleation. Classic mineralogy textbook form.
- **Variant 6: Anhydrite** — The dehydrated form. Conditions: high T or high salinity. Converts to gypsum on cooling. Pseudocubic habit.

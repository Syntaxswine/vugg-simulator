# Mineral Species Template — Vugg Simulator

## Species: Borax

### Identity
- **Formula:** Na₂[B₄O₅(OH)₄]·8H₂O (sodium tetraborate decahydrate)
- **Crystal system:** Monoclinic
- **Mineral group:** Borate
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 2–2.5
- **Specific gravity:** 1.69–1.73
- **Cleavage:** Perfect on {100}, distinct on {110}
- **Fracture:** Conchoidal to uneven
- **Luster:** Vitreous to resinous, pearly on cleavage surfaces; earthy when massive
- **Tenacity:** Brittle (but sectile when fresh — can be cut with a knife)

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Colorless, white, gray; rarely pale blue or pale green
- **Color causes:** Usually pure — trace Cu²⁺ can impart pale blue/green tints
- **Transparency:** Transparent to translucent
- **Streak:** White
- **Notable visual features:** Brilliant vitreous luster on fresh surfaces; efflorescent white powder (tincalconite) crust on weathered specimens; sweet alkaline taste

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Short to long prismatic crystals, often tabular on {010}
- **Common forms/faces:** {100}, {110}, {010}, {001}; well-developed monoclinic prisms with steep terminations
- **Twin laws:** Rare; contact twins on {100} reported
- **Varieties:**
  - *Tincal* — massive nodular borax from ancient lake deposits (the original "tincal" of Tibetan commerce)
  - *Cottonball* — fibrous rounded aggregates from Death Valley playas
- **Special morphologies:** Efflorescent crusts, stalactitic forms, granular massive aggregates, fibrous bundles

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** Ambient to ~60°C (evaporite lake conditions)
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 20–40°C (playa lake evaporation)
- **Decomposition temperature:** Loses water starting at ~75°C → tincalconite (Na₂B₄O₇·5H₂O); above ~320°C → anhydrous Na₂B₄O₇; melts at 743°C (anhydrous)
- **Temperature-dependent habits:** Lower T → well-formed prismatic crystals; rapid evaporation (higher T) → fibrous/cottonball habits; above 60°C → unstable, dehydrates

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:** Na⁺ (high, >1000 ppm), B (as borate B(OH)₄⁻, >500 ppm)
- **Optional/enhancing elements:** Cl⁻ (common in brine lakes, doesn't inhibit), Mg²⁺ (present in most playa brines)
- **Inhibiting elements:** Ca²⁺ (sequesters borate as colemanite/inyoite — COMPETES for B), high Fe²⁺
- **Required pH range:** 8.5–10.5 (alkaline — borate requires high pH to stay in solution as B(OH)₄⁻)
- **Required Eh range:** Not strongly redox-sensitive (borate is stable across wide Eh range)
- **Required O₂ range:** Not relevant — borate is stable in both oxic and anoxic water

#### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **Does it release any chemicals when forming?** Consumes borate from solution, slightly increases pH (borate is a weak base)
- **Byproducts of nucleation:** None significant — pure precipitation from brine
- **Byproducts of dissolution/decomposition:** Releases Na⁺ and B(OH)₄⁻ back to solution; dehydration releases H₂O vapor

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Fast — one of the most rapidly growing evaporite minerals. Much faster than quartz. Can grow visible crystals in hours/days from saturated brine.
- **Maximum crystal size:** Up to 30+ cm (Boron, California specimens); typical well-formed crystals 1–10 cm
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** 5–50 mm (but borax doesn't typically form in vugs — forms in open lake basins)
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?** Yes — higher T increases evaporation rate → faster nucleation and growth, but also increases dehydration risk
- **Competes with:** Halite (NaCl), thenardite (Na₂SO₄), trona (Na₃(CO₃)(HCO₃)·2H₂O), colemanite/ulexite (if Ca present)

#### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** Yes — dehydrates to tincalconite at 53–75°C, to kernite at higher T (slow), to anhydrous borax above 320°C
- **Breaks down in light?** No (photostable)
- **Dissolves in water?** Yes — 31.7 g/L at 25°C. Strongly temperature-dependent solubility (increases with T)
- **Dissolves in acid?** Yes — dissolves readily in dilute acids, releasing boric acid
- **Oxidizes?** No — boron is already in oxidized state (borate)
- **Dehydrates?** **YES — critically.** Effloresces in dry air to tincalconite (white powder crust). Specimens in collections MUST be stored in sealed containers. This is THE defining instability of borax.
- **Radiation sensitivity:** No significant radiation effects

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Halite (NaCl precipitates first from brine at higher concentration), thenardite/mirabilite
- **Forms BEFORE:** Kernite (buried borax dehydrates to kernite at depth), colemanite (if Ca-rich fluids react with borax), ulexite (Na+Ca borate)
- **Commonly associated minerals:** Halite, thenardite, trona, hanksite, borax → tincalconite (efflorescence), ulexite, colemanite, inyoite
- **Zone:** Supergene/evaporite — playa lake, arid-region brine evaporation. NOT hydrothermal or hypogene.
- **Geological environment:** Closed-basin playa lakes in arid regions; alkaline saline lakes; efflorescences on arid soils; hot spring deposits (rare); volcanic fumarole sublimates (very rare)

### Famous Localities
- **Classic locality 1:** Boron, Kern County, California — open-pit borax mine (US Borax/Rio Tinto), crystals to 30+ cm
- **Classic locality 2:** Searles Lake, San Bernardino County, California — brine extraction, associated hanksite, trona, halite
- **Classic locality 3:** Death Valley (Furnace Creek), Inyo County, California — historic "cottonball" borax, 20-mule team fame
- **Other notable:** Kirka, Turkey; Tincalayu, Salta, Argentina; Tibetan Plateau (historic tincal trade); Lardarello, Italy (hot spring borates)
- **Notable specimens:** 30 cm crystal groups from Boron, CA; "cottonball" aggregates from Death Valley playa surfaces

### Fluorescence
- **Fluorescent under UV?** No (or very weak)
- **SW (255nm) color:** None
- **MW (310nm) color:** None
- **LW (365nm) color:** Very weak pale yellow-white (rare, trace organic inclusions?)
- **Phosphorescent?** No
- **Activator:** None native — pure borax is non-fluorescent
- **Quenched by:** N/A

### Flavor Text

> Borax grows where water gives up. In the sun-baked playas of Death Valley, alkaline brine concentrates through endless evaporation cycles until borate ions can no longer stay dissolved — and out comes sodium tetraborate, crystallizing in prismatic shards of startling clarity. It is the most ephemeral of collectors' minerals: leave a crystal on the shelf and within weeks it effloresces to a white powder of tincalconite, losing 5 of its 10 water molecules to dry air. The 20-mule teams that hauled borax from Death Valley's furnace made it an icon of American industry, but the mineral itself is humble — soft, water-soluble, thirsty for humidity. Every borax specimen in a collection is a race against dehydration. It whispers: *nothing beautiful is permanent, and some things crystallize only on the way to disappearing.*

### Simulator Implementation Notes
- **New parameters needed:** `trace_B` (boron concentration in ppm) — already in the 30-element trace list
- **New events needed:** `event_playa_evaporation` — borax requires an evaporite scenario, not a hydrothermal vug. Needs a different formation pathway than typical vug minerals.
- **Nucleation rule pseudocode:**
```
IF temperature < 60°C
  AND pH > 8.5
  AND trace_Na > 1000 ppm
  AND trace_B > 500 ppm
  AND humidity_low (or vug connected to evaporating brine)
  AND NOT trace_Ca >> trace_B (Ca would steal B as colemanite)
  → nucleate borax
```
- **Growth rate:** Fast (rate ~15, compared to quartz baseline ~5). Grows 3× faster than quartz.
- **Growth rule pseudocode:**
```
IF σ_borax > 1.0 AND temperature < 60°C
  → grow at rate 15 × (σ_borax - 1.0)
  IF temperature > 50°C → 5% chance per step of dehydration → convert to tincalconite
```
- **Habit selection logic:**
  - T < 30°C, slow evaporation → well-formed prismatic crystals
  - T 30–50°C, fast evaporation → fibrous/cottonball habit
  - T > 50°C → dehydration risk
- **Decomposition products:**
  - 53–75°C → tincalconite (Na₂B₄O₇·5H₂O) + 5H₂O released
  - >100°C → kernite (Na₂B₄O₆(OH)₂·3H₂O) (slow, requires burial conditions)
  - >320°C → anhydrous borax (Na₂B₄O₇)
  - Dissolution → releases Na⁺ + B(OH)₄⁻ back to fluid
- **Special mechanic:** Borax should slowly dehydrate in any dry vug (low humidity). This is unique — most minerals are stable once formed. Borax demands moisture to persist. In the simulator, if humidity drops or temperature rises, borax crystals should show a "crumbling" animation and convert to tincalconite (white powder pseudomorph).

### Variants for Game
- **Variant 1: Prismatic Borax** — Well-formed monoclinic prisms. Conditions: T 20–35°C, slow evaporation, high B concentration. Classic museum specimen habit.
- **Variant 2: Cottonball Borax** — Fibrous white rounded aggregates. Conditions: T 35–50°C, rapid evaporation, playa surface. Death Valley collector's item.
- **Variant 3: Tincalconite Pseudomorph** — Borax crystal shape but now white powdery tincalconite. Conditions: borax that sat too long in warm/dry conditions. A warning to collectors — the crystal you found IS the ghost.

### Game Design Notes
Borax is a mineral that *doesn't belong in a typical hydrothermal vug*. It's an evaporite. To include it, the simulator needs one of:
1. An **evaporite scenario** (playa lake, alkaline brine concentration)
2. A **late-stage brine pocket** where the last dregs of a vug fluid concentrate Na and B as other elements are consumed
3. A **hot spring** environment where boron-rich volcanic waters evaporate

Option 2 is most compatible with existing vug mechanics. If the vug fluid is Na+B-rich and the late-stage fluid concentrates through water loss (e.g., the vug partially opens to the surface), borax could crystallize as a very late-stage mineral.

The dehydration mechanic is the unique gameplay hook: borax is the only common mineral that self-destructs in your collection unless you store it properly. This could be a "specimen care" mechanic — some minerals need special handling.

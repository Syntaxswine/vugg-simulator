**Date:** 2026-04-18
**Sequence:** Zinc sulfide primary → hexagonal polymorph → oxidation zone carbonate
**Status:** Complete

## Species: Sphalerite (Zn,Fe)S

### Identity
- **Formula:** (Zn,Fe)S — iron content 0–40 mol%, increases with formation temperature
- **Crystal system:** Cubic (isometric), hextetrahedral class 4̄3m, space group F4̄3m
- **Mineral group:** Sulfide — sphalerite group
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 3.5–4
- **Specific gravity:** 3.9–4.2 (increases with Fe content)
- **Cleavage:** Perfect dodecahedral on {011} — six directions of cleavage
- **Fracture:** Uneven to conchoidal
- **Luster:** Adamantine, resinous, greasy (the resinous luster is diagnostic)

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Yellow, honey-brown, red-brown, dark brown, black, green, rarely colorless or blue
- **Color causes:**
  - Fe²⁺ substitution: yellow → brown → black (marmatite at >20% Fe)
  - Pure ZnS: colorless to pale yellow (variety cleiophane)
  - Mn: greenish tones
  - Cu inclusions: reddish
  - Cd: yellow tints
- **Transparency:** Transparent (pure) to opaque (Fe-rich)
- **Streak:** Brownish white to pale yellow (lighter than expected — diagnostic vs galena)
- **Notable visual features:**
  - Dispersion of 0.156 (B-G interval) — over 3× diamond. Gem sphalerite has spectacular fire.
  - Refractive index 2.37 (pure) to 2.50 (40% Fe) — one of the highest RI minerals
  - Triboluminescent: yellow-orange flash when scratched

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Tetrahedra, dodecahedra; commonly massive granular
- **Common forms/faces:** {111} tetrahedron dominant, {011} dodecahedron
- **Twin laws:** Spinel-law twins on {111}, simple contact or complex lamellar
- **Varieties:**
  - **Cleiophane:** nearly pure ZnS (<0.1% Fe), colorless to pale green, highly fluorescent
  - **Marmatite:** black, Fe-rich (up to 25% Fe), opaque, named for Marmato, Colombia
  - **Ruby blende / ruby zinc:** red to orange-red, gemmy
  - **Black-jack:** dark, nearly opaque miners' term
- **Special morphologies:** Botryoidal, stalactitic, fibrous (rare), coarse cleavable masses

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** 50–500°C (extremely wide stability field)
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 150–350°C (hydrothermal vein range)
- **Decomposition temperature:** ~1020°C (transforms to wurtzite above this)
- **Temperature-dependent habits:**
  - Low T (50–150°C): colloform, botryoidal, fibrous crusts (MVT deposits)
  - Medium T (150–300°C): well-formed tetrahedra, coarse granular
  - High T (300–500°C): massive, coarse-grained with high Fe content
  - **Iron content scales with temperature** — color is a geothermometer

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:** Zn (ppm-level), S (sulfide, reducing conditions)
- **Optional/enhancing elements:**
  - Fe: substitutes for Zn, increases with temperature
  - Cd: up to 1% substitution
  - Mn: commonly with Fe
  - Ga, Ge, In: trace, deposit-type specific
- **Inhibiting elements:** High O₂ (sulfide unstable under oxidizing conditions)
- **Required pH range:** Neutral to mildly acidic (pH 4–7)
- **Required Eh range:** Reducing (negative Eh, sulfide stable)
- **Required O₂ range:** Very low — sulfide stability requires reducing environment

#### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **Oxidation products:** Zn²⁺ + SO₄²⁻ + H⁺ (acidifies fluid)
- **Byproducts of dissolution:** Releases Zn²⁺ into solution, acidity from sulfide oxidation
- **Oxidation sequence:** Sphalerite → Zn²⁺ (mobile in solution) → smithsonite (if CO₃ available) or hemimorphite

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Moderate — faster than galena, slower than pyrite
- **Maximum crystal size:** To 30+ cm (Picos de Europa, Spain); gem crystals to 10+ cm
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** 0.5–5 cm crystals, granular masses larger
- **Growth rate vs temperature:** Increases with temperature; Fe-rich high-T sphalerite grows larger
- **Competes with:** Galena (PbS — same S budget), pyrite (FeS₂ — Fe competition), chalcopyrite (CuFeS₂)

#### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** Converts to wurtzite at ~1020°C
- **Breaks down in light?** No
- **Dissolves in water?** Very slightly; more soluble than galena
- **Dissolves in acid?** HCl — dissolves with H₂S gas (rotten egg smell, diagnostic test)
- **Oxidizes?** Yes — slowly at surface to Zn sulfates, then smithsonite/hemimorphite in oxidation zone
- **Radiation sensitivity:** Can darken with radiation damage but not a notable effect

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Early pyrite/marcasite (which scavenge Fe first)
- **Forms BEFORE:** Galena (usually), late-stage carbonates, oxidation zone minerals
- **Commonly associated minerals:** Galena, pyrite, chalcopyrite, calcite, dolomite, fluorite, quartz, rhodochrosite, barite
- **Zone:** Primary/hypogene (sulfide zone), rarely supergene
- **Geological environments:**
  - Mississippi Valley-Type (MVT) deposits — low T, with galena, fluorite, calcite
  - Volcanogenic Massive Sulfide (VMS) — with pyrite, chalcopyrite
  - Sedimentary exhalative (SEDEX) — with galena, pyrite, barite
  - Hydrothermal veins — with quartz, carbonates, various sulfides
  - Contact metamorphic — skarn deposits

### Famous Localities
- **Picos de Europa, Spain:** World's largest sphalerite crystals (to 30+ cm), gemmy amber
- **Franklin/Ogdensburg, New Jersey:** Cleiophane — fluorescent green/orange, with willemite, zincite
- **Trepča, Kosovo:** Large gemmy crystals, classic European locality
- **Nanisivik, Baffin Island, Canada:** Sharp complex crystals on sulfide matrix
- **Elmwood, Tennessee:** MVT deposit, gem honey-amber sphalerite with fluorite, calcite, galena
- **Notable specimens:** Gem faceted sphalerite shows dispersion exceeding diamond — cut stones from Spain and Mexico

### Fluorescence
- **Fluorescent under UV?** Variable — depends on impurities
- **SW (255nm):** Orange-red (Fe-poor/cleiophane)
- **MW (310nm):** Similar to SW
- **LW (365nm):** Orange, blue-white, or green (Franklin cleiophane: bright orange)
- **Phosphorescent?** Sometimes brief
- **Activator:** Cu²⁺, Ag⁺, or structural defects; Mn as co-activator
- **Quenched by:** Fe²⁺ — high-iron sphalerite (marmatite) does NOT fluoresce

### Flavor Text
> Sphalerite is the deceiver — σφαλερός — named because it kept fooling miners into thinking it was galena. No lead, just zinc pretending to be important. But crack it open under the right light and it's the most dispersive mineral on Earth, three times the fire of diamond, trapped in a stone too soft to wear. The iron content in its crystal lattice is a geothermometer: clear and yellow means cool hydrothermal waters, black and metallic means the broth ran hot. Every crystal carries the temperature of its birth in its color.

### Simulator Implementation Notes
- **New parameters needed:** `trace_Zn` (already exists), `trace_Cd` (optional, for greenockite inclusions)
- **New events needed:** None — sphalerite is straightforward nucleation
- **Nucleation rule pseudocode:**
```
IF trace_Zn > threshold AND S²⁻ available AND Eh < 0 (reducing) AND T < 500°C
  → nucleate sphalerite
  Fe content = f(temperature): higher T → more Fe → darker color
```
- **Growth rule pseudocode:**
```
IF σ_sphalerite > 1 AND reducing conditions persist
  → grow at moderate rate
  Fe incorporation proportional to temperature
  color = f(Fe_content): clear → yellow → brown → black
```
- **Habit selection logic:**
  - T < 150°C: botryoidal/colloform (MVT style)
  - T 150–300°C: tetrahedral crystals
  - T > 300°C: massive granular, high Fe
- **Decomposition products:** At T > 1020°C → wurtzite. Under oxidation → Zn²⁺ + SO₄²⁻ → smithsonite

### Variants for Game
- **Variant 1: Cleiophane** — near-pure ZnS, colorless to pale green, strongly fluorescent. Low Fe, low T. Franklin-type.
- **Variant 2: Ruby blende** — red to orange-red, gemmy, moderate Fe. Collectible quality.
- **Variant 3: Marmatite** — black, Fe-rich, high-T, opaque. No fluorescence. Named for Marmato, Colombia.
- **Variant 4: Schalenblende** — banded sphalerite + wurtzite + galena, colloform concentric layering. Looks like agate but it's sulfide.

## Species: Wurtzite (Zn,Fe)S

### Identity
- **Formula:** (Zn,Fe)S — same as sphalerite, hexagonal polymorph. Fe up to 8%.
- **Crystal system:** Hexagonal, dihexagonal pyramidal 6mm, space group P6₃mc
- **Mineral group:** Sulfide — wurtzite group
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 3.5–4
- **Specific gravity:** ~4.1
- **Cleavage:** {1120} and {0001}
- **Fracture:** Uneven
- **Luster:** Resinous, brilliant submetallic on crystal faces

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Brownish-black, orange-brown, reddish-brown, black
- **Color causes:** Same Fe substitution as sphalerite
- **Transparency:** Translucent
- **Streak:** Light brown

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Radial clusters, colloform crusts, tabular prismatic crystals
- **Common forms/faces:** Pyramidal, tabular on {0001}
- **Twin laws:** Not commonly twinned
- **Special morphologies:** Fibrous radiating clusters, colloform banding with sphalerite

### Formation Conditions
- **Temperature:** Stable above 1020°C (high-T polymorph); BUT also forms at low T metastably in sedimentary/hydrothermal conditions
- **In practice:** Found in low-T hydrothermal deposits and clay-ironstone concretions — often intergrown with sphalerite
- **Chemistry:** Same as sphalerite — Zn + S in reducing conditions
- **Associated minerals:** Sphalerite (always), pyrite, chalcopyrite, barite, marcasite

### Paragenesis
- **Zone:** Primary, same as sphalerite
- **Note:** Schalenblende is a mixture of sphalerite + wurtzite + galena in banded colloform layers — forms in low-T repeated precipitation events

### Fluorescence
- **Same system as sphalerite** — variable, depends on Fe content

### Flavor Text
> Wurtzite is sphalerite's hexagonal twin — same recipe, different stacking. Above 1020°C it's the stable form; below that, it's metastable, a high-temperature ghost surviving in low-temperature rocks. The universe doesn't care about equilibrium when there's crystallizing to do. Often found intergrown with its cubic sibling in banded schalenblende, alternating layers like sedimentary pages written in sulfide ink.

### Simulator Implementation Notes
- **Nucleation rule:** Very high T (>1020°C) OR metastable low-T formation alongside sphalerite (rare, ~5% chance)
- **Growth rule:** Same chemistry as sphalerite, hexagonal crystal habit
- **Variant:** Schalenblende — banded mixture with sphalerite + galena

## Species: Smithsonite ZnCO₃

### Identity
- **Formula:** ZnCO₃
- **Crystal system:** Trigonal, hexagonal scalenohedral 3̄2/m, space group R3̄c
- **Mineral group:** Carbonate — calcite group
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 4.5
- **Specific gravity:** 4.4–4.5 (remarkably heavy for a carbonate)
- **Cleavage:** Perfect on {101̄1} (rhombohedral, like calcite)
- **Fracture:** Uneven, sub-conchoidal
- **Luster:** Vitreous, may be pearly on cleavage surfaces

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** White, grey, yellow, green (apple-green), blue, pink, purple, brown
- **Color causes:**
  - Cu²⁺: blue to green (cuprian smithsonite)
  - Co²⁺: pink to purple (cobaltoan smithsonite — highly prized)
  - Cd: yellow ("turkey fat ore" from greenockite inclusions)
  - Fe: brown
  - Pure: white to grey
- **Transparency:** Translucent
- **Streak:** White
- **Notable visual features:** Botryoidal surfaces with pearly-silky luster; banded color zones common

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Botryoidal, reniform, spherulitic — crystals are RARE
- **Common forms/faces:** When crystallized: rhombohedra, scalenohedra (uncommon)
- **Twin laws:** None observed
- **Special morphologies:** Stalactitic, earthy, compact massive, drusy crusts

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** Near-surface to ~100°C (superface/oxidation zone)
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 20–60°C (cool, near-surface)
- **Temperature-dependent habits:** Always botryoidal/massive regardless of temperature

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:** Zn²⁺ (from sphalerite oxidation), CO₃²⁻ (carbonate availability)
- **Optional/enhancing elements:** Cu (→ blue/green), Co (→ pink/purple), Cd (→ yellow)
- **Required pH range:** Slightly alkaline (pH 7–9) — carbonate stability
- **Required Eh range:** Oxidizing — this is an oxidation zone mineral
- **Required O₂ range:** High — sphalerite must oxidize first to release Zn²⁺

#### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **Formation reaction:** Zn²⁺ + CO₃²⁻ → ZnCO₃
- **Consumes:** CO₃ from the broth

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Moderate — typical carbonate rate
- **Maximum crystal size:** Crystals to ~10 cm (very rare); botryoidal masses to meters
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** Botryoidal crusts 1–10 cm thick
- **Competes with:** Hemimorphite (Zn₄Si₂O₇(OH)₂·H₂O — another zinc supergene mineral), hydrozincite

#### Stability
- **Dissolves in acid?** Effervesces in HCl (like calcite, but slower)
- **Decomposition:** At ~300°C → ZnO + CO₂

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Sphalerite (which must oxidize to release Zn²⁺)
- **Forms BEFORE:** Late-stage earthy zinc minerals
- **Commonly associated minerals:** Hemimorphite, willemite, hydrozincite, cerussite, malachite, azurite, aurichalcite, anglesite, limonite
- **Zone:** Supergene/oxidation zone — always secondary
- **Geological environment:** Oxidized tops of zinc-lead ore bodies, replacement bodies in carbonate rocks

### Famous Localities
- **Tsumeb, Namibia:** World's finest smithsonite — cobaltoan pink, cuprian blue-green, large crystals
- **Kelly Mine, New Mexico:** Blue cuprian smithsonite, classic US locality
- **Lavrion, Greece:** Botryoidal specimens from ancient slag dumps
- **Ojuela Mine, Mapimí, Durango, Mexico:** Yellow "turkey fat" smithsonite, pink cobaltoan
- **Broken Hill, Zambia:** Apple-green cuprian smithsonite

### Fluorescence
- **Fluorescent under UV?** Weakly — variable
- **SW/LW:** Pale green or pale blue under UV
- **Activator:** Possibly U or rare earths; not strongly fluorescent
- **Not a major fluorescent mineral** — distinguished from willemite (bright green) in zinc deposits

### Flavor Text
> Smithsonite is what zinc becomes when it grows up in the open air — sphalerite's oxidation zone graduation. The heavy botryoidal masses look like grape clusters made of stone, colored by whatever trace metals were passing through: cobalt pink, copper blue-green, cadmium yellow. Named for James Smithson, who identified it and later founded the Smithsonian Institution — a mineral that built a museum. Turkey fat ore, the miners called the yellow variety. Not every beautiful thing gets a beautiful name.

### Simulator Implementation Notes
- **New parameters needed:** CO₃²⁻ in broth (needed for all carbonates — already exists?)
- **Nucleation rule pseudocode:**
```
IF Zn²⁺ available (from sphalerite oxidation) AND CO₃²⁻ available
   AND Eh > 0 (oxidizing) AND T < 100°C
  → nucleate smithsonite
```
- **Growth rule pseudocode:**
```
IF σ_smithsonite > 1 AND oxidizing conditions AND CO₃ available
  → grow as botryoidal crust
  color variant = f(trace metals): Cu→blue/green, Co→pink, Cd→yellow
```
- **Habit selection:** Always botryoidal; crystals extremely rare (special event only)
- **Decomposition products:** At T > 300°C → ZnO (zincite) + CO₂

### Variants for Game
- **Variant 1: Cuprian smithsonite** — blue to apple-green, Cu substitution. Kelly Mine type.
- **Variant 2: Cobaltoan smithsonite** — pink to purple, Co substitution. Tsumeb type. Prized collector mineral.
- **Variant 3: Turkey fat ore** — bright yellow, Cd (greenockite inclusions). Ojuela Mine type.

## Paragenetic Sequence Summary

```
PRIMARY (hypogene, reducing, T 150-350°C):
  Sphalerite (Zn,Fe)S ← main zinc sulfide
    ↓ Fe content = temperature proxy
    ↓ Cd, Mn, Ge, Ga traces encoded in lattice

HIGH-T POLYMORPH (>1020°C, rare in nature):
  Wurtzite (Zn,Fe)S ← hexagonal stacking variant
    ↓ Also forms metastably at low T

OXIDATION ZONE (supergene, oxidizing, T < 60°C):
  Sphalerite + O₂ + H₂O → Zn²⁺ + SO₄²⁻ + H⁺
    ↓ Zn²⁺ mobile in acidic groundwater
    ↓ CO₃²⁻ available from limestone/host rock
  Smithsonite ZnCO₃ ← zinc carbonate cap
```

**Key game mechanic:** Sphalerite → Smithsonite is a direct oxidation chain. If the vug transitions from reducing → oxidizing conditions AND CO₃ is available, sphalerite dissolves and smithsonite precipitates. The color of smithsonite depends on trace metals present during formation.

**Competing supergene Zn mineral:** Hemimorphite (Zn₄Si₂O₇(OH)₂·H₂O) forms instead of smithsonite when Si is more available than CO₃. In carbonate-rich host rocks, smithsonite dominates. In silicate-rich environments, hemimorphite wins.

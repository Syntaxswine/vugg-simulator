# Crystals and Minerals in Demonology: A Research Document

*Compiled 2026-02-25. For historical and scholarly reference — not a practice guide.*

---

## 1. The Grimoire Traditions: Solomonic Magic and Classical Demonology

### The Seal/Ring of Solomon

The foundational legend: King Solomon's ring, by which he commanded demons (djinn in Islamic tradition) and compelled them to build the Temple. The ring was made of **brass** (for commanding good spirits) and **iron** (for commanding evil spirits), according to the Talmud (*Gittin* 68a-b) and later Arabic sources.

The stone set in the ring varies by tradition:
- **Diamond or sapphire** — some Western accounts
- In Islamic tradition, the ring bore the "Most Great Name of God" and was set with jewels; the *Sulemani Hakik* (Solomon's agate/carnelian) remains a named variety of banded agate in the gem trade, specifically associated with Solomon's power over jinn

### The Key of Solomon (Clavicula Salomonis)

The *Greater Key of Solomon* (compiled from medieval manuscripts, most famously edited by Mathers in 1889) prescribes specific materials for ritual tools and pentacles. While metals (silver, gold, copper, tin) dominate the material prescriptions, stones appear in several contexts:

- **Crystal or beryl** for scrying operations — the operator gazes into polished stone to perceive spirits
- Various pentacles are inscribed on **metal discs** associated with planetary correspondences, but the underlying tradition draws on the same lapidary logic that links stones to celestial influences

### The Lesser Key of Solomon (Lemegeton)

The five books of the Lemegeton each engage with minerals differently:

**Ars Goetia** (Book 1): The 72 demons are constrained using the magic circle, triangle of art, and specific tools. Several demons are described as having knowledge of stones:
- **Bathin** (Duke, #18): teaches the virtues of herbs and **precious stones**
- **Decarabia** (Marquis, #69): knows the virtues of all herbs and **precious stones**
- **Asmoday/Asmodeus** (#32): teaches arithmetic, astronomy, geometry, and handicrafts — traditionally associated with the building of Solomon's Temple using stones

**Ars Paulina** (Book 3): Associates spirits with planetary hours and zodiacal degrees, each linked to specific stones through the standard planetary correspondences (see Agrippa below).

**Ars Almadel** (Book 4): Requires a wax tablet (the Almadel itself) elevated on four candles, with a **crystal stone** placed atop a golden seal on the tablet. The crystal serves as the medium through which the angels of the four Altitudes appear. This is one of the most explicit grimoire uses of a crystal as a spirit-communication device.

### Trithemius: *The Art of Drawing Spirits into Crystals*

Attributed to Johannes Trithemius of Spanheim (1462–1516), though likely composed or compiled later and popularized through Francis Barrett's *The Magus* (1801). This is arguably the most important single text linking crystals to spirit evocation.

The method requires:
- A **polished crystal ball** set upon a specially constructed pedestal/table
- The crystal is consecrated and placed within a circle of divine names
- Spirits (both angelic and demonic) are invoked to manifest visibly within the crystal
- The consecration prayer specifically addresses the crystal as an "inanimate creature of God" to be "sanctified and consecrated" so that spirits "may be constrained to speak intelligibly and truly"

This text remains foundational for modern ceremonial magicians and is frequently cited as a more accessible alternative to full Goetic evocation.

### Heinrich Cornelius Agrippa: *Three Books of Occult Philosophy* (1531/1533)

Agrippa's work is the encyclopedia of Renaissance magical correspondences. Book I covers the elemental/natural world, including extensive tables of stone-planet-spirit correspondences:

**Planetary Stone Correspondences** (from Agrippa's tables):
| Planet | Stones | Associated Spirits |
|--------|--------|-------------------|
| Saturn | onyx, sapphire (dark), obsidian, jet, lead ore | Zazel (spirit), Agiel (intelligence) |
| Jupiter | sapphire (blue), amethyst, topaz, beryl | Hismael (spirit), Yophiel (intelligence) |
| Mars | ruby, garnet, bloodstone, magnetite | Bartzabel (spirit), Graphiel (intelligence) |
| Sun | diamond, chrysolite, carbuncle, heliotrope | Sorath (spirit), Nakhiel (intelligence) |
| Venus | emerald, turquoise, lapis lazuli, coral | Kedemel (spirit), Hagiel (intelligence) |
| Mercury | agate, carnelian, sardonyx, amber | Taphthartharath (spirit), Tiriel (intelligence) |
| Moon | quartz crystal, selenite, pearl, beryl | Chasmodai (spirit), Malkah be-Tarshishim (intelligence) |

Agrippa draws from Ezekiel's vision of nine stones corresponding to the nine angelic orders: **sapphire, emerald, carbuncle, beryl, onyx, chrysolite, jasper, topaz, and sardius** (sardine/sard).

The planetary spirits in Agrippa's system are not "demons" in the Christian sense but *daimones* in the Neoplatonic sense — intermediary intelligences. However, many ceremonial magicians treated them as potentially dangerous entities requiring the same binding protocols as Goetic demons.

## 2. Medieval and Renaissance Lapidaries

### Marbode of Rennes: *De Lapidibus* (c. 1090)

The most influential medieval lapidary, describing 60 stones and their virtues. Key demonic/protective associations:

- **Jet (gagates)**: "drives off serpents and demons" — one of the most consistently cited anti-demonic stones across medieval literature
- **Diamond (adamas)**: repels evil spirits, renders poisons harmless, cures madness (which was often attributed to demonic possession)
- **Jasper**: prevents fever and phantasms (demonic visions)
- **Chalcedony**: drives away phantoms and "vain illusions"
- **Agate**: multiple varieties; generally protective against venomous creatures and "enchantments"

Marbode's work was copied, adapted, and interpolated throughout the Middle Ages. It established the framework that all subsequent lapidaries followed.

### Albertus Magnus: *De Mineralibus* (c. 1260) and the pseudo-Albertan *Book of Secrets*

Albertus Magnus's genuine *De Mineralibus* (Book of Minerals) is more Aristotelian and naturalistic than Marbode's work, but still includes occult virtues:

- **Diamond**: protects against enemies and "nocturnal fears" (i.e., demonic visitations)
- **Sapphire**: calms the mind, associated with divine contemplation; said to prevent imprisonment and deception by evil spirits
- **Coral**: wards off lightning, evil spirits, and the "fascination" (evil eye)

The pseudo-Albertan *Liber Secretorum* (Book of Secrets), widely attributed to Albertus but likely spurious, goes further with magical recipes involving stones for protection against demons.

### Hildegard of Bingen: *Physica* (c. 1150)

Hildegard's lapidary section attributes stone virtues to divine origin — God placed power in stones precisely to counter the devil:

- **Emerald**: the devil "hates and despises" it; wearing emerald on the body prevents demonic attack
- **Jasper**: "the devil shuns this stone"
- **Sardonyx**: sharpens the senses against demonic deception
- **Sapphire**: "the devil hates this stone because its power operates even at night hours" when demons are most active

Hildegard's framework is distinctly Christian: stones derive protective power from God's creative act, and demons recoil from them because they recall their own fall from grace.

### Other Notable Lapidary Sources

- **Damigeron-Evax** (Hellenistic, widely circulated in medieval Latin): attributes apotropaic powers to many stones; **agate** placed under the pillow prevents nightmares and demonic visitations
- **Liber Lapidum** tradition: multiple anonymous texts circulating from the 12th century onward, often mixing Marbode, Pliny, and Arabic sources
- **Pseudo-Aristotle's *Secretum Secretorum***: widely circulated in medieval courts; includes stone-based protections against evil spirits

## 3. Scrying Stones: The Material Culture of Spirit Communication

### Obsidian

The premier scrying material, with deep cross-cultural significance:

**John Dee's Obsidian Mirror** (British Museum, object 1966,1001.1): A polished obsidian disc, approximately 18cm diameter, now confirmed through geochemical analysis (2021, *Antiquity* journal) to be of **Aztec Mexican origin**, crafted from Pachuca obsidian. It arrived in Europe sometime after the Spanish conquest (post-1521) and was in Dee's possession by the 1580s.

Dee called this and other stones his **"shewstones"** (show-stones). The mirror was used alongside:
- A **rock crystal ball** (also in the British Museum)
- A **gold disc** with a "Vision of the Four Castles" engraved on it
- The **Holy Table** upon which these objects were placed

Edward Kelley served as Dee's "scryer" or "skryer," gazing into the obsidian mirror and crystal ball to perceive angelic and spiritual entities. The entire **Enochian system** — angelic language, calls, hierarchies — was received through these sessions (1582–1589).

### Beryl

Before Dee, **beryl** was the classical scrying stone. The practice of "crystallomancy" or "beryllistics" (literally: looking into beryl) was widespread enough to give us the word itself. Clear beryl (including what we'd now call aquamarine or goshenite) was polished into spheres or flat discs.

- The *Liber Juratus Honorii* (Sworn Book of Honorius, 13th century) prescribes crystal and beryl for angel/spirit communication
- Medieval church trials frequently mention beryl-gazers (*berylistae*) alongside necromancers

### Rock Crystal (Quartz)

The generic "crystal ball" tradition:
- Used across European magical traditions from at least the 12th century
- The *Ars Almadel* (Lemegeton Book 4) prescribes a crystal stone for perceiving angelic appearances
- Trithemius's system requires a "fair crystal" in which spirits manifest
- Crystal balls appear in the inventories of accused witches and magicians throughout early modern trial records

### The Speculum (Magic Mirror)

Beyond obsidian, mirrors of various materials served for spirit-seeing:
- **Polished steel or iron** — common in medieval necromantic texts
- **Black mirrors** — any dark reflective surface; ink poured into the palm of a boy's hand was a related technique in Arabic magic (*mandal*)

## 4. Protective and Warding Stones

Stones believed to repel demons, evil spirits, and malefic influences across traditions:

### Tier 1: The "Big Five" Anti-Demonic Stones

**Jet (Gagates)**
- The most universally cited anti-demonic stone in Western tradition
- Marbode: drives off serpents and demons
- Pliny (*Natural History* XXXVI): its smoke drives away serpents
- Albert the Great: its fumes reveal those possessed by demons
- Widely burned as fumigation against evil spirits in medieval churches and homes
- Note: jet is technically fossilized wood (lignite), not a mineral — but treated as a "stone" in all historical texts

**Diamond (Adamas)**
- Nearly universal attribution of power against evil spirits
- Medieval belief: demons cannot approach a diamond
- Used in exorcism rituals: held against the body of the possessed
- Multiple lapidaries state it prevents nightmares and nocturnal demonic attacks
- The hardness of diamond was read symbolically as spiritual inviolability

**Jasper**
- Hildegard: "the devil shuns this stone"
- Multiple medieval traditions: prevents fever, phantasms, demonic visions
- Red jasper specifically associated with blood and life-force, opposing death-demons
- Widely used in carved amulets (intaglios) since antiquity

**Carnelian/Sard**
- Islamic tradition: **aqeeq** (carnelian/agate) is prophetically recommended; Ali ibn Abi Talib reportedly wore carnelian for "protection from enemies and all types of misfortunes"
- Egyptian tradition: carnelian wards off the Evil Eye
- Medieval European: protects against black magic and evil spirits
- Often engraved with Quranic verses or divine names in Islamic talismanic tradition

**Coral**
- Pseudo-Albertan tradition: wards off evil spirits, lightning, and tempests
- Italian tradition (still alive in folk practice): coral horns (*cornicelli*) protect against the *malocchio*
- Pliny: coral protects children from danger
- Note: like jet, coral is organic, not mineral — but universally treated as a "stone"

### Tier 2: Frequently Cited Protective Stones

- **Agate**: prevents nightmares when placed under the pillow (Damigeron-Evax); generally protective against enchantments
- **Sapphire**: associated with heavenly purity; demons cannot bear its presence (Hildegard); prevents fraud and deception
- **Emerald**: the devil "hates and despises" it (Hildegard); reveals truth and falsehood
- **Sardonyx**: protects against incantations and sorcery; sharpens the senses
- **Beryl**: protects travelers; prevents fascination (evil eye)
- **Topaz**: cools anger and prevents madness (demonic possession)
- **Chrysolite/Peridot**: when set in gold, dispels nocturnal terrors
- **Tourmaline (Schorl)**: black tourmaline becomes important in later folk magic traditions as a protective stone; less attested in medieval sources but prominent from the 18th century onward
- **Garnet**: placed beneath the pillow to guard against evil spirits at night (medieval lore)
- **Iron/Magnetite**: not a gemstone per se, but iron's anti-demonic properties are among the most ancient and widespread (associated with the nails of the Crucifixion in Christian tradition; cold iron against faeries in Celtic tradition)

## 5. Modern Occult and Ceremonial Magic Practice

### Contemporary Goetic Work

Modern ceremonial magicians who work with the Goetia and related grimoire traditions use crystals in several ways:

**Scrying Crystals for Evocation**
- The Trithemius *Art of Drawing Spirits into Crystals* remains the most popular crystal-based evocation method among contemporary practitioners
- Typically uses a clear quartz crystal ball (2–4 inches) on a consecrated pedestal within the Triangle of Art
- Some practitioners use **obsidian spheres** or **black mirrors** as alternatives, following the Dee tradition
- The crystal serves as the "container" or manifestation point where the spirit appears

**Crystal Triangle**
- In Goetic evocation, the Triangle of Art (where the spirit manifests) sometimes incorporates a crystal or obsidian sphere at its center
- Modern practitioners have developed elaborate setups combining brass or copper triangles with crystal spheres, often adding stones like **moldavite, smoky quartz, or phantom quartz** around the perimeter

**Planetary Stones in Ritual**
- Following Agrippa's correspondences, practitioners select stones matching the planetary rulership of the demon being evoked
- Example: evoking a demon ruled by Mars might incorporate **bloodstone, garnet, or ruby** into the ritual setup
- Stones are placed on altars, worn as rings, or set into lamens (ritual pendants worn by the magician)

### The Golden Dawn and Thelemic Traditions

- The Hermetic Order of the Golden Dawn (1888–1903) incorporated Agrippan stone correspondences into their elaborate system of correspondences
- Aleister Crowley's edition of the *Goetia* (1904) maintains the traditional tool set; his broader magical system incorporates stones via the *777* tables of correspondences
- Modern Thelemic practitioners often use stones corresponding to specific sephiroth on the Kabbalistic Tree of Life for spirit work

### Chaos Magic and Eclectic Approaches

- Less bound by traditional correspondences; practitioners may use any stone that "feels right" or that they have personally charged
- **Black obsidian** and **black tourmaline** are popular for "banishing" work and psychic protection during demonic/spirit contact
- **Labradorite** has gained modern popularity as a "psychic protection" stone during trance and spirit work
- **Hematite** is widely used in modern occult practice for grounding after spirit contact

## 6. Cross-Cultural Traditions

### Islamic Tradition: Jinn and Stones

The Solomonic tradition is shared between Jewish, Christian, and Islamic magic. In Islamic practice:

- **Aqeeq (Carnelian/Agate)**: the Prophet Muhammad is narrated to have worn an aqeeq ring; carnelian is considered the first stone to testify to the Oneness of God. Widely used in talismanic rings engraved with Quranic verses for protection against jinn
- **Sulemani Hakik** (Solomon's Agate): banded agate specifically named after Solomon (Sulaiman), associated with his power over jinn. Still commercially available and culturally significant
- **Turquoise (Feroza/Firuzeh)**: associated with divine help and victory; Ali reportedly wore it
- **Yaqut (Ruby/Corundum)**: for beauty and dignity
- **Dur-e-Najaf**: a specific variety of quartz from Najaf, Iraq, particularly valued in Shia tradition; believed to protect against evil eye and negative spiritual influences

Arabic and Persian magical texts (*kutub al-ruhaniyyat*) extensively prescribe engraved gemstones as containers or bindings for jinn — directly paralleling the Solomonic tradition of binding spirits in vessels.

### Mesoamerican Tradition: Obsidian and Tezcatlipoca

**Tezcatlipoca** ("Smoking Mirror") — one of the supreme Aztec deities, god of night, sorcery, and fate:
- His primary symbol was the **obsidian mirror**, which he wore on his chest, in his headdress, or replacing his right foot
- Obsidian mirrors were used by Aztec priests for **divination** and to access the underworld (*Mictlan*)
- The mirrors served to communicate with the realm of the dead and perceive hidden truths
- Obsidian's volcanic origin (fire + earth) gave it special spiritual significance
- For the Aztecs, mirrors were **doorways to other worlds** — "once you deeply gaze in, you have opened up that connection" (Karl Taube, UC Riverside)
- John Dee's mirror was literally one of these Aztec mirrors, transported to Europe after the Conquest — a remarkable case of cross-cultural magical artifact transfer

### Hindu/Vedic Tradition: Navaratna and Graha

The **Navaratna** (nine gems) system links gemstones to the nine *graha* (planetary influences, sometimes translated as "seizers" — etymologically related to the concept of demonic seizure):

| Graha | Stone | Nature |
|-------|-------|--------|
| Surya (Sun) | Ruby | Benefic |
| Chandra (Moon) | Pearl | Benefic |
| Mangala (Mars) | Red Coral | Malefic |
| Budha (Mercury) | Emerald | Neutral |
| Guru (Jupiter) | Yellow Sapphire | Benefic |
| Shukra (Venus) | Diamond | Benefic |
| Shani (Saturn) | Blue Sapphire | Malefic |
| Rahu (North Node) | Hessonite (Gomed) | Malefic |
| Ketu (South Node) | Cat's Eye (Chrysoberyl) | Malefic |

**Rahu** and **Ketu** are particularly relevant — they are literally demonic entities (the severed head and body of a demon) in Hindu mythology. Wearing their corresponding stones (**hessonite garnet** and **cat's eye chrysoberyl**) is prescribed to *propitiate* them, essentially appeasing the demon rather than repelling it. This is a fundamentally different paradigm from Western protective stone use.

The *graha* concept — celestial "seizers" who grip humans and cause affliction — bridges the gap between astrology and demonology. Stones are prescribed as *remedies* to pacify these entities, much as offerings might appease a spirit.

### Tibetan Buddhist Tradition

- **Zi/Dzi beads** (etched agate beads): ancient Tibetan talisman stones believed to ward off evil spirits and bring protection. The etched "eye" patterns are specifically designed to watch for and repel malevolent entities
- **Turquoise**: considered a living stone in Tibetan tradition; protects the soul
- **Coral**: red coral and turquoise together form the most common Tibetan protective combination

### African and African Diasporic Traditions

- In various West African traditions, specific stones are associated with particular *orisha* or spirits
- **Thunderstones** (prehistoric stone tools, often celts or axes of polished stone): widely believed across West Africa and the Caribbean to be thrown to earth by thunder deities (Shango/Xangô); these are literally mineral specimens with spirit associations
- In Vodou and related traditions, specific stones may be placed in vessels (*govi*) or on altars (*peristil*) as seats or anchors for spirits

### Ancient Near Eastern Traditions

- **Hematite**: in Mesopotamian tradition, hematite cylinder seals were inscribed with protective formulas against *Lamashtu* and other demons
- **Lapis lazuli**: sacred to Inanna/Ishtar; protective against evil in Sumerian/Akkadian texts
- **Carnelian**: widely used in Mesopotamian and Egyptian apotropaic amulets
- The Mesopotamian tradition of **stone-based demon protection** (particularly hematite and carnelian) is arguably the ultimate source for much of the Western and Islamic lapidary tradition

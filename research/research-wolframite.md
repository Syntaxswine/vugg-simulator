# Mineral Species — Wolframite

## Species: Wolframite

### Identity
- **Formula:** (Fe,Mn)WO₄
- **Crystal system:** Monoclinic
- **Mineral group:** Oxide (tungstate subgroup)
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 4–4.5
- **Specific gravity:** 7.0–7.5 (remarkably heavy — diagnostic)
- **Cleavage:** Perfect on {010}
- **Fracture:** Uneven to rough
- **Luster:** Submetallic to resinous

### Solid Solution Series
Wolframite is the intermediate member between two end members:
- **Ferberite** — FeWO₄ (iron-dominant, darker, more black)
- **Hübnerite** — MnWO₄ (manganese-dominant, reddish-brown, more transparent)
- Any proportion between them is simply called "wolframite"
- The Fe/(Fe+Mn) ratio is a petrogenetic indicator: high H/F = strong magmatic imprint, low H/F = fluid/rock interaction

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Grayish to brownish black (ferberitic) to reddish brown (hübneritic)
- **Color causes:** Iron content → darker; manganese → reddish-brown tones
- **Transparency:** Opaque to translucent on thin edges (hübnerite more translucent)
- **Streak:** Reddish brown (diagnostic — distinguishes from magnetite which has black streak)
- **Notable visual features:** Extreme density is the most immediate diagnostic. Feels much heavier than it looks. Bladed/tabular habit is distinctive.

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Tabular to short prismatic, often bladed
- **Common forms/faces:** Thick tabular crystals with striated faces; also lamellar and prismatic habits
- **Twin laws:** Contact twins on {100}, rare
- **Varieties:**
  - Ferberite: nearly pure FeWO₄, iron-black, commonly massive or bladed
  - Hübnerite: nearly pure MnWO₄, reddish-brown to amber, more likely to form well-developed transparent crystals
  - "Reinite": pseudomorph of scheelite after wolframite (scheelite replacing wolframite, retaining its shape)
- **Special morphologies:** Massive, bladed aggregates, radiating groups, embedded in quartz

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** 250–450°C
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 300–400°C (primary mineralizing window, per Wood & Samson 2000)
- **Decomposition temperature:** Stable to very high temperatures (W melts at 3,422°C — the mineral itself is refractory)
- **Temperature-dependent habits:** Higher temperatures favor more equant crystals; lower temperatures in the hydrothermal range produce bladed/tabular habits. Fe/(Fe+Mn) ratio shifts with temperature — ferberite favored at higher T, hübnerite at lower T or in more evolved fluids.

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:** W (tungsten), Fe²⁺ and/or Mn²⁺, O²⁻
- **Minimum concentrations:** W needs hundreds of ppm in hydrothermal fluid (solubility at 300–400°C, 500–1500 bars, 5–15 wt% NaCl eq., pH 4–6 = hundreds of ppm W)
- **Optional/enhancing elements:** Nb, Ta (can substitute in small amounts), Sn (cassiterite paragenesis)
- **Inhibiting elements:** Ca²⁺ (promotes scheelite crystallization instead — competes for W)
- **Required pH range:** 4–6 (mildly acidic hydrothermal fluids)
- **Required Eh range:** Moderately reducing to neutral
- **Required O₂ range:** Low — forms in reduced hydrothermal environments

#### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **Nucleation byproducts:** Consumes Fe²⁺ and/or Mn²⁺ from fluid
- **Dissolution/decomposition:** Wolframite is very stable. Supergene weathering can produce tungstite (WO₃·H₂O) and ferritungstite ((W,Fe)(O,OH)₃). Replacement by W-rich goethite documented.

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Slow — comparable to or slower than quartz. Tungsten has low crustal abundance (~1 ppm), so sustained W supply is the bottleneck.
- **Maximum crystal size:** Up to 20+ cm blades (Panasqueira); typical vug specimens 1–10 cm
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** 1–5 cm bladed crystals on quartz matrix
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?** Higher solubility at higher T; growth rate increases with temperature in the 300–400°C window.
- **Competes with:** Scheelite (CaWO₄) — if Ca is available, scheelite takes the W. Also competes with any Fe/Mn oxide for those cations.

#### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** Extremely refractory. Does not decompose at vug temperatures.
- **Breaks down in light?** No.
- **Dissolves in water?** Negligible solubility at surface conditions. Soluble in hydrothermal brines at elevated T/P.
- **Dissolves in acid?** Resistant. Soluble in hot concentrated acids (HCl + HNO₃).
- **Oxidizes?** Very stable under surface conditions. Supergene alteration is slow, produces tungstite, ferritungstite, or W-bearing goethite.
- **Dehydrates?** No — not a hydrate.
- **Radiation sensitivity:** No documented effects.

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Quartz (wolframite typically crystallizes in open fractures within quartz veins), high-T sulfides (molybdenite, bismuthinite)
- **Forms BEFORE:** Scheelite (can form later at lower T with Ca influx), sulfides (pyrite, arsenopyrite, chalcopyrite often post-date wolframite)
- **Commonly associated minerals:** Quartz (always), cassiterite, scheelite, arsenopyrite, pyrite, galena, sphalerite, molybdenite, native bismuth, bismuthinite, topaz, fluorite, tourmaline
- **Zone:** Primary/hypogene — forms from high-temperature magmatic-hydrothermal fluids
- **Geological environment:**
  - Quartz veins in or near granitic intrusions (most common)
  - Greisen (altered granite with quartz-muscovite-topaz)
  - Granitic pegmatites
  - Skarn deposits (contact metamorphic)
  - Alluvial/eluvial (being very dense and resistant, accumulates in placers)

### Famous Localities
- **Panasqueira Mine, Portugal** — Operating since 1886. Classic bladed wolframite on quartz with arsenopyrite and cassiterite. World-class specimens.
- **Jiangxi/Hunan, China** — World's largest tungsten producer (~60-80% of global supply). Yaogangxian mine produces superb specimens.
- **Llallagua, Bolivia** — Tin-tungsten veins. Fine crystal specimens.
- **Cornwall/Devon, UK** — Historic. Hemerdon (Drakelands) mine.
- **Pine Creek, California, USA** — Scheelite/wolframite skarn deposit.

### Fluorescence
- **Fluorescent under UV?** No — wolframite does NOT fluoresce. This is diagnostic: scheelite fluoresces bright blue-white under SW UV, wolframite does not.
- **SW (255nm):** None
- **MW (310nm):** None
- **LW (365nm):** None
- **Phosphorescent?** No
- **Activator:** N/A
- **Quenched by:** N/A

### Simulator Implementation Notes
- **New parameters needed:** trace_W (tungsten) — needs to be added to broth if not already present. Fe and Mn already tracked.
- **New events needed:**
  - `wolframite_vein_event` — W-rich fluid pulse from granitic source at 300–400°C
  - Fe/(Fe+Mn) ratio determines ferberite vs hübnerite coloring
- **Nucleation rule pseudocode:**
```
IF temperature >= 250 AND temperature <= 450
  AND trace_W > threshold (high — needs pulse event)
  AND (trace_Fe > 0 OR trace_Mn > 0)
  AND pH >= 4 AND pH <= 6
  AND trace_Ca < threshold (low Ca avoids scheelite competition)
  → nucleate wolframite
```
- **Growth rule pseudocode:**
```
IF sigma_wolframite > 1.0 AND temperature 300-400°C
  → grow at rate 3 (slow, comparable to quartz)
  Fe_ratio = trace_Fe / (trace_Fe + trace_Mn)
  → color = lerp(reddish_brown, iron_black, Fe_ratio)
  → habit = IF temperature > 350: more_equant ELSE: bladed_tabular
```
- **Habit selection logic:**
  - High T (>350°C): more equant/prismatic crystals
  - Lower T (250-350°C): bladed, tabular, often striated
  - Mn-rich (hübnerite): more likely transparent, well-formed
  - Fe-rich (ferberite): opaque, darker, more massive
- **Decomposition products:** None at vug temperatures. Supergene → tungstite (WO₃·H₂O) or ferritungstite. Replacement by W-bearing goethite documented.
- **Key competition:** If Ca is present, scheelite (CaWO₄) will form preferentially. Wolframite needs Ca-poor, Fe/Mn-rich environment. In a vug with both Ca and Fe/Mn, partition W between scheelite and wolframite based on Ca/(Fe+Mn) ratio.

### Variants for Game
- **Variant 1: Ferberite** — Fe-dominant (Fe/(Fe+Mn) > 0.8). Iron-black, opaque, submetallic. Higher T favored. Slightly higher SG (~7.5).
- **Variant 2: Hübnerite** — Mn-dominant (Fe/(Fe+Mn) < 0.2). Reddish-brown, translucent on edges, more resinous luster. Lower T favored. Slightly lower SG (~7.0). Better crystal form.
- **Variant 3: Reinite** — Pseudomorph of scheelite after wolframite. Wolframite shape but scheelite composition. Triggers if Ca-rich fluid infiltrates existing wolframite at lower T. Fluoresces blue-white (scheelite signature) in wolframite's shape — a mineral wearing another mineral's ghost.

### Connection to Existing Game Minerals
- **Stolzite (PbWO₄)** and **Raspite (PbWO₄ monoclinic)** — same tungstate anion, different cation (Pb vs Fe/Mn). Stolzite/raspite form in the oxidation zone from lead + tungsten. Wolframite is the primary source of W that feeds stolzite/raspite formation.
- **Ferrimolybdite** — similar paragenetic position (primary ore mineral altered in oxidation zone)
- **Scheelite** — the Ca end of the tungstate family. Main competitor for W. Fluorescent; wolframite is not.
- **Paragenetic chain:** Granitic intrusion → W-bearing hydrothermal fluid → wolframite (in quartz veins) + molybdenite + cassiterite → cooling → later sulfides (pyrite, arsenopyrite) → oxidation zone → stolzite/raspite (from Pb + W) or scheelite (from Ca + W)

### Etymology (for lore)
"Wolfram" = "wolf's froth" — medieval German tin miners' curse. The dark mineral ate their tin yields like a wolf eating sheep. The element tungsten takes its symbol W from this name. "Tungsten" itself = Swedish "heavy stone." Two names for one ore, born from frustration and heft.

# Research: Rhodochrosite — Vugg Simulator

## Species: Rhodochrosite

### Identity
- **Formula:** MnCO₃
- **Crystal system:** Trigonal (hexagonal scalenohedral, space group R3c)
- **Mineral group:** Carbonate (calcite group)
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 3.5–4.0
- **Specific gravity:** 3.45–3.70
- **Cleavage:** Perfect on {1011} (rhombohedral, 3 directions at ~75°/105°)
- **Fracture:** Uneven to conchoidal; brittle
- **Luster:** Vitreous to pearly

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Rose-red, pink, cherry-red; less commonly yellowish, brown, grey, banded
- **Color causes:** Mn²⁺ is the chromophore. Pure MnCO₃ = deep rose-red. Ca, Fe, Mg substitution dilutes color toward pink, tan, or brown. Banding from rhythmic precipitation (like agate — seasonal or chemical pulses)
- **Transparency:** Transparent to translucent
- **Streak:** White
- **Notable visual features:** Stunning rose-red color is unmistakable. Banded stalactitic forms from Capillitas, Argentina ("Inca Rose") are iconic. Rhombohedral crystals from Sweet Home Mine are among the most sought-after mineral specimens in the world

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Rhombohedral and scalenohedral crystals
- **Common forms/faces:** {1011} rhombohedron dominant, {0221} scalenohedron, {0001} basal pinacoid
- **Twin laws:** {1012} contact and lamellar twins — common
- **Varieties:**
  - **Capillitas-type:** Banded stalactitic/botryoidal, pink-white layers, Argentina
  - **Sweet Home-type:** Sharp cherry-red rhombohedral crystals on quartz/pyrite matrix
  - **Manganoan calcite confusion zone:** Ca-rich rhodochrosite grades into Mn-bearing calcite
- **Special morphologies:** Botryoidal, stalactitic, columnar, bladed, granular, massive

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** 50–300°C (hydrothermal vein range; best 100–200°C)
- **Optimal growth temperature:** ~150°C (low-to-moderate hydrothermal)
- **Decomposition temperature:** ~400–500°C (decomposes to MnO + CO₂)
- **Temperature-dependent habits:** Lower T → botryoidal/stalactitic bands; higher T → sharper rhombohedral crystals

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:** Mn²⁺ (primary), CO₃²⁻ or dissolved CO₂
- **Optional/enhancing elements:** Ca²⁺ (dilutes color, lightens to pink), Fe²⁺ (siderite solid solution, darkens to brown), Mg²⁺ (limited substitution), Zn²⁺ (limited)
- **Inhibiting elements:** High O₂/Eh (oxidizes Mn²⁺ to Mn⁴⁺ → forms oxides instead), high S²⁻ (Mn prefers carbonate over sulfide but alabandite MnS can compete)
- **Required pH range:** Neutral to slightly alkaline (7–9). Carbonate stability zone.
- **Required Eh range:** Reducing to mildly oxidizing. Mn²⁺ must remain soluble. Too oxidizing → MnO₂ (pyrolusite) instead.
- **Required O₂ range:** Low. Dissolved O₂ must be low enough to keep Mn as Mn²⁺, not Mn⁴⁺.

#### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **Byproducts of nucleation:** Removes Mn²⁺ and CO₃²⁻ from solution
- **Byproducts of dissolution:** Releases Mn²⁺ + HCO₃⁻ (acidifies slightly in low-pH water)
- **Byproducts of decomposition (heat):** MnO (manganosite) + CO₂ gas

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Moderate (comparable to calcite)
- **Maximum crystal size:** Rhombohedra to ~15 cm (Sweet Home Mine). Banded masses to meters.
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** 1–10 cm for crystals; banded masses can fill entire vugs
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?** Yes — higher CO₂ partial pressure increases growth rate (surface speciation studies show MnHCO₃⁰ surface complex drives growth)
- **Competes with:** Calcite (CaCO₃ wins if Ca>>Mn), siderite (Fe>>Mn), rhodonite (MnSiO₃ in silica-rich systems), kutnohorite (CaMn(CO₃)₂ — ordered intermediate)

#### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** ~400–500°C → MnO + CO₂
- **Breaks down in light?** No significant photo-sensitivity
- **Dissolves in water?** Slightly soluble (Ksp ~10⁻¹¹), more soluble than calcite. Dissolves in acidic conditions.
- **Dissolves in acid?** Yes, readily in HCl with effervescence (weaker reaction than calcite — needs warm/dilute acid)
- **Oxidizes?** Yes — surface oxidation to brown/black Mn oxides (pyrolusite, manganite) on exposure
- **Dehydrates?** No (anhydrous carbonate)
- **Radiation sensitivity:** Not notable

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Primary Mn-bearing minerals (rhodonite, spessartine garnet, Mn-bearing sulfides) have been weathered/dissolved, releasing Mn²⁺ into solution
- **Forms BEFORE:** Mn oxides (pyrolusite, manganite, psilomelane) which form when conditions become more oxidizing
- **Commonly associated minerals:** Quartz, pyrite, fluorite, galena, sphalerite, tetrahedrite, chalcopyrite, rhodonite, alabandite, calcite, siderite
- **Zone:** Primary gangue in hydrothermal veins (moderate T); also supergene in oxidation zones of Mn deposits; sedimentary (banded formations)
- **Geological environment:** Hydrothermal veins (especially Ag-bearing), pegmatites (Mn-rich), metamorphic Mn deposits, sedimentary Mn-rich formations (marine), cave/stalactitic environments

### Famous Localities
- **Sweet Home Mine, Alma, Colorado:** The iconic locality. Cherry-red rhombohedra on quartz. "Alma King" (largest known crystal, ~15 cm). Now a specimen mine (closed for silver).
- **Capillitas, Catamarca, Argentina:** Banded stalactitic "Inca Rose." National gemstone of Argentina.
- **Cavnic and Huancavelica, Romania:** Type locality (1813). Historical Ag-Mn veins.
- **Kalahari Manganese Field, South Africa:** Massive rhodochrosite in Mn orebodies
- **Minas Gerais, Brazil:** Pink botryoidal specimens
- **Notable specimens:** "Alma King" (Denver Museum), "Alma Rose" (Rice NW Museum), "The Searchlight" (large red crystal, Sweet Home)

### Fluorescence
- **Fluorescent under UV?** Generally no
- **SW (255nm):** None
- **MW (310nm):** None
- **LW (365nm):** None (some Mn-bearing calcites fluoresce pink/red, but rhodochrosite itself does not)
- **Phosphorescent?** No
- **Activator:** N/A
- **Quenched by:** N/A

Mn²⁺ is the chromophore — manganese sitting quietly in its carbonate cage, absorbing everything except the frequencies that make human hearts skip. The Sweet Home Mine in Colorado was a silver mine that went broke. Then miners found blood-red rhombohedra lining quartz vugs, and it became the most famous specimen mine on Earth. Sometimes the gangue mineral is worth more than the ore. Argentina made it their national gemstone; Colorado made it their state mineral. It forms a complete solid solution with siderite — add iron and the rose goes brown. Add enough calcium and it fades to nothing. Pure rhodochrosite is rare. The rose is always compromised, always alloyed with the earth around it. That's what makes the deep red crystals so valuable: they grew in a pocket where manganese had the stage alone.

### Simulator Implementation Notes
- **New parameters needed:** trace_Mn (already in trace elements list), CO₃²⁻ / dissolved CO₂ (needed for carbonate system — shared with malachite, azurite, cerussite, smithsonite)
- **New events needed:** None special — forms during normal carbonate precipitation if Mn is available
- **Nucleation rule pseudocode:**
```
IF trace_Mn > threshold AND CO3_available AND pH > 7 AND Eh < oxidizing_threshold AND T < 400°C:
  σ = (Mn²⁺ × CO₃²⁻) / Ksp_rhodochrosite
  IF σ > 1 → nucleate
```
- **Growth rule pseudocode:**
```
IF rhodochrosite exists AND σ > 1:
  growth_rate = base_rate × (σ - 1) × CO2_pressure_factor
  IF Fe²⁺ present → incorporate Fe into solid solution (darkens color, shifts toward siderite)
  IF Ca²⁺ present → incorporate Ca (lightens color)
```
- **Habit selection logic:**
  - T > 200°C AND space available → sharp rhombohedral crystals
  - T < 150°C AND continuous flow → botryoidal/banded (rhythmic precipitation)
  - Vertical surface + dripping → stalactitic
  - Confined space → massive/granular fill
- **Decomposition products:** MnO (manganosite) + CO₂ (gas) at T > 400°C

### Variants for Game
- **Variant 1: Inca Rose (banded)** — Botryoidal/stalactitic, pink-white banding. Conditions: low T, continuous CO₂-rich drip, rhythmic Mn supply pulses. Argentina type.
- **Variant 2: Sweet Home Cherry** — Sharp cherry-red rhombohedra. Conditions: moderate T (~150°C), high purity Mn (low Ca, Fe), grows on quartz. Rare, high value.
- **Variant 3: Kutnohorite** — CaMn(CO₃)₂ ordered intermediate. Conditions: Ca ≈ Mn in broth. Pale pink to tan. Less dramatic, but fills the compositional gap between calcite and rhodochrosite.

### Solid Solution Notes (Siderite-Rhodochrosite Series)
Complete solid solution: (Fe,Mn)CO₃. Any ratio possible.
- Pure MnCO₃ = rhodochrosite (rose-red)
- Pure FeCO₃ = siderite (tan/brown)
- Intermediate = "oligonite" or "siderodot" (historical names, now just compositional descriptors)
- Color shifts predictably: more Fe → darker, more brown/yellow
- Both share identical crystal structure (calcite-type, R3c), same cleavage, same twin laws
- In simulator: blend color based on Fe:Mn ratio in the crystal

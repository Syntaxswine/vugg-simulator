## Species: Uraninite (Pitchblende)

### Identity
- **Formula:** UO₂ (uranium dioxide) — typically partially oxidized to U₃O₈
- **Crystal system:** Isometric (cubic)
- **Mineral group:** Oxide
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 5–6
- **Specific gravity:** 10.63–10.95 (decreases with oxidation)
- **Cleavage:** Indistinct
- **Fracture:** Conchoidal to uneven
- **Luster:** Submetallic, greasy, dull

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Steel-black to velvet-black, brownish black; thin fragments pale green to yellow
- **Color causes:** Intrinsic to U⁴⁺ absorption; radiation damage darkens over time; Th and REE inclusions may tint
- **Transparency:** Opaque; transparent in thin fragments (pale green/yellow in transmitted light)
- **Streak:** Brownish black, gray, olive-green
- **Notable visual features:** Massive pitchblende has a characteristic greasy-black luster; well-formed octahedral crystals are uncommon but striking. Radioactive — will register on Geiger counter or scintillator. Always contains radiogenic lead (²⁰⁶Pb, ²⁰⁷Pb) and trace helium.

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Massive, botryoidal, granular (pitchblende variety)
- **Common forms/faces:** Octahedral {111} crystals (rare, mainly in pegmatites); cubic {100} faces uncommon
- **Twin laws:** None commonly reported
- **Varieties:**
  - **Pitchblende** — massive botryoidal form, typical of hydrothermal veins
  - **Cleveite** — REE-bearing variety, historically important (first terrestrial helium discovery)
  - **Braggite** — not a variety but sometimes confused; separate mineral (Pt,Pd,Ni)S
  - **Nasturan** — older synonym for pitchblende
- **Special morphologies:** Botryoidal crusts with concentric layering (hydrothermal); colloform banding; dendritic forms rare

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** 150–600°C (varies by environment)
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 200–400°C (hydrothermal veins), >500°C (pegmatitic)
- **Decomposition temperature:** Stable to very high temperatures; UO₂ melts at 2,865°C. No thermal decomposition in game range.
- **Temperature-dependent habits:**
  - High-T pegmatitic (500–700°C): well-formed octahedral crystals
  - Medium-T hydrothermal (200–400°C): botryoidal pitchblende crusts
  - Low-T sedimentary (<100°C): microcrystalline masses in roll-front deposits

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:** U (uranium) — the sine qua non
- **Optional/enhancing elements:**
  - Th (thorium) — substitutes for U in pegmatitic uraninite; high Th = high-T formation indicator
  - REE (rare earth elements) — can substitute into structure
  - Pb (lead) — accumulates as radiogenic product over time
  - Si (silica) — associated in pegmatites
  - Ti, Zr — can be present in pegmatitic varieties
- **Inhibiting elements:**
  - High O₂ (oxidizing conditions) — converts U⁴⁺ to U⁶⁺, destabilizing uraninite in favor of secondary uranium minerals
  - Fe³⁺ in solution — promotes oxidation of U⁴⁺
- **Required pH range:** 4–9 (broad); most stable under neutral to slightly alkaline reducing conditions
- **Required Eh range:** Strongly reducing (Eh < 0.0 V). U⁴⁺ is only stable under reducing conditions. Oxidizing conditions mobilize U as soluble U⁶⁺ uranyl ion (UO₂²⁺).
- **Required O₂ range:** Low — uraninite requires anoxic or reducing conditions to precipitate and persist

#### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **Does it release any chemicals when forming?** No significant release during precipitation; U⁴⁺ removes oxygen from solution
- **Byproducts of nucleation:** Minimal — direct precipitation from U⁴⁺-bearing fluids
- **Byproducts of dissolution/decomposition:**
  - **Oxidation:** UO₂ + O₂ → UO₂²⁺ (soluble uranyl ion) — this is THE key reaction for the uranium paragenetic sequence. Oxidation releases mobile U⁶⁺ into groundwater.
  - **Radiogenic:** α-decay produces He (helium) and Pb (lead) — accumulates over geologic time
  - **Radiation damage:** Self-irradiation damages crystal lattice over millions of years (metamictization), making mineral increasingly amorphous

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Slow — uranium is relatively rare in most fluids
- **Maximum crystal size:** Crystals up to several cm in pegmatites (rare); botryoidal masses can be meters across
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** 1–20 mm crystals (pegmatites); botryoidal crusts 1–50 mm thick
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?** Yes — higher temperature = better crystal development; low-T forms are cryptocrystalline
- **Competes with:** Coffinite (USiO₄·nH₂O) at lower temperatures in silica-rich environments; zircon (ZrSiO₄) when Zr present

#### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** No — extremely refractory (melting point 2,865°C)
- **Breaks down in light?** No
- **Dissolves in water?** No — UO₂ is insoluble under reducing conditions. Under oxidizing conditions, dissolves as uranyl ion.
- **Dissolves in acid?** Yes — dissolves in nitric acid and sulfuric acid under oxidizing conditions
- **Oxidizes?** YES — critically. This is the engine of the uranium secondary mineral paragenesis:
  - UO₂ (uraninite, U⁴⁺) → oxidation → UO₂²⁺ (soluble uranyl) → combines with PO₄³⁻, AsO₄³⁻, VO₄³⁻, SiO₄⁴⁻, CO₃²⁻ → torbernite, zeunerite, carnotite, autunite, etc.
  - The oxidation of uraninite is what feeds the entire secondary uranium mineral family
- **Dehydrates?** No (anhydrous)
- **Radiation sensitivity:** Is itself radioactive. Self-damage over geological time causes metamictization (loss of crystallinity). Old uraninite may be completely amorphous. Specific gravity decreases as lattice expands from alpha-recoil damage.

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Primary mineral — precipitates from U-bearing hydrothermal fluids or late-stage pegmatitic fluids. Requires earlier concentration of uranium by magmatic or hydrothermal processes.
- **Forms BEFORE:** All secondary uranium minerals (torbernite, autunite, carnotite, zeunerite, tyuyamunite, etc.) form from uraninite oxidation. Also: coffinite (can replace uraninite in silica-rich reducing conditions), lead minerals (from radiogenic Pb accumulation).
- **Commonly associated minerals:**
  - **Pegmatitic:** quartz, feldspar, mica, zircon, monazite, thorite
  - **Hydrothermal veins:** sulfides (galena, sphalerite, chalcopyrite), quartz, calcite, fluorite, barite
  - **Sandstone roll-fronts:** pyrite/marcasite (reducing trap), vanadium minerals (montroseite, davidite), organic matter (petrified wood, carbonaceous material)
- **Zone:** Primary/hypogene — uraninite is the PRIMARY uranium mineral from which all secondary species derive
- **Geological environments:**
  1. **Granite/syenite pegmatites** — well-crystallized octahedral uraninite
  2. **Medium-high-T hydrothermal veins** — botryoidal pitchblende (Jáchymov type)
  3. **Unconformity-type deposits** — high-grade masses (Athabasca Basin type)
  4. **Sandstone roll-front deposits** — microcrystalline, organic-reduced (Colorado Plateau type)
  5. **Quartz-pebble conglomerates** — detrital (Witwatersrand type — paleoplacer)

### Famous Localities
- **Jáchymov (Joachimsthal), Czech Republic** — TYPE LOCALITY. Historic silver-uranium mining district, Ore Mountains. Pitchblende from here used by Klaproth to discover uranium (1789), by Marie Curie to isolate radium (1910).
- **Shinkolobwe, Democratic Republic of Congo** — Extraordinarily high-grade uranium ore. Primary source for Manhattan Project. Famous for huge uraninite masses and exceptional secondary uranium minerals.
- **Athabasca Basin, Saskatchewan, Canada** — World's highest-grade uranium deposits. Unconformity-type. McArthur River, Cigar Lake mines.
- **Great Bear Lake, Northwest Territories, Canada** — Port Radium. Pitchblende associated with native silver.
- **Colorado Plateau, USA** — Sandstone-hosted roll-front deposits. Moab, Uravan district. Carnotite-bearing.
- **Witwatersrand, South Africa** — Paleoplacer gold-uranium deposits. Detrital uraninite in quartz pebble conglomerates.
- **Erzgebirge (Ore Mountains), Germany/Czech Republic** — Historic mining district. Niederschlema-Alberoda, Johanngeorgenstadt.
- **Topsham, Maine, USA** — Notable octahedral crystals in granite pegmatite

### Fluorescence
- **Fluorescent under UV?** NO — uraninite does not fluoresce. (Unlike its secondary minerals.)
- **SW (255nm) color:** None
- **MW (310nm) color:** None
- **LW (365nm) color:** None
- **Phosphorescent?** No
- **Activator:** N/A
- **Quenched by:** N/A
- **Note:** The ABSENCE of fluorescence distinguishes uraninite from its bright-green fluorescent secondary minerals (autunite, torbernite). If a uranium mineral fluoresces, it's secondary, not uraninite.

### Simulator Implementation Notes
- **New parameters needed:** None — trace_U already in game
- **New events needed:**
  - `uraninite_oxidation` — when oxidizing conditions reach uraninite crystals, convert U⁴⁺ to mobile U⁶⁺ (uranyl), releasing it back to fluid. This is the feedstock for ALL secondary uranium minerals.
  - `metamictization` — optional: over long timescales, uraninite crystal quality degrades (visual effect only, or slight density decrease)
- **Nucleation rule pseudocode:**
```
IF Eh < 0.0 AND trace_U > high_threshold AND temp > 150 → nucleate uraninite
IF temp > 500 → octahedral habit (pegmatitic)
IF temp 200-400 → botryoidal habit (hydrothermal pitchblende)
IF temp < 100 → microcrystalline (sedimentary)
```
- **Growth rule pseudocode:**
```
IF Eh < 0.0 AND trace_U > threshold → grow uraninite
Growth rate proportional to trace_U concentration
Rate enhanced at higher temperature
IF Eh rises above 0.0 → STOP growth, begin oxidation dissolution
```
- **Habit selection logic:**
  - T > 500°C AND slow cooling → octahedral crystals (pegmatite)
  - T 200–400°C AND moderate fluid flow → botryoidal crusts (pitchblende)
  - T < 100°C AND organic matter present → microcrystalline masses (roll-front)
- **Decomposition products:**
  - Oxidation → releases U⁶⁺ (uranyl ion) to fluid → feeds torbernite, carnotite, zeunerite, autunite
  - Long-term radiogenic → Pb accumulation (could lead to galena or cerussite in extreme cases)
  - Silica-rich reducing → coffinite replacement (USiO₄·nH₂O)

### Variants for Game
- **Variant 1: Pitchblende** — massive botryoidal habit, hydrothermal (200–400°C). Most common game form. Greasy black luster, no crystal faces. The "default" uraninite.
- **Variant 2: Pegmatitic Uraninite** — well-formed octahedral crystals, high-T (>500°C). Rare. Higher Th content. The collector's form — octahedrons in feldspar matrix.
- **Variant 3: Cleveite** — REE-bearing variety, pegmatitic. Slightly different chemistry (trace Ce, La, Y). Historically famous for helium discovery. Visual distinction: slightly brownish tint.

## Paragenetic Sequence: Uranium Mineral Family

Since uraninite is the primary uranium mineral, here's how the full sequence works in the simulator:

### Primary Phase (Reducing, High-T)
1. **Uraninite** (UO₂) precipitates from U⁴⁺-bearing fluids under reducing conditions
2. Associated with sulfides, quartz, fluorite, calcite

### Oxidation Phase (Groundwater invasion, Eh rises)
3. **Uraninite oxidizes** → releases U⁶⁺ as mobile uranyl ion (UO₂²⁺)
4. Uranyl combines with available anions:
   - **+ PO₄³⁻ + Cu²⁺** → **Torbernite** Cu(UO₂)₂(PO₄)₂·12H₂O
   - **+ AsO₄³⁻ + Cu²⁺** → **Zeunerite** Cu(UO₂)₂(AsO₄)₂·12H₂O
   - **+ VO₄³⁻ + K⁺** → **Carnotite** K₂(UO₂)₂(VO₄)₂·3H₂O
   - **+ PO₄³⁻ + Ca²⁺** → **Autunite** Ca(UO₂)₂(PO₄)₂·11H₂O (not yet in game — needs Ca)

### Late Oxidation
5. Secondary minerals dehydrate: torbernite → metatorbernite, zeunerite → metazeunerite
6. Iron oxyhydroxides (limonite) coat everything as final oxidation product

### Key Simulator Insight
Uraninite is the **gatekeeper mineral** for the entire uranium family. Without uraninite forming first and then oxidizing, none of the green/fluorescent secondary minerals can form. This makes it a high-value target for players who want to unlock uranium mineral diversity in their vug.
